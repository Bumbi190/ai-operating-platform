## Canonical Text

### 8.1 Purpose of This Chapter

The Daily Executive Brief is the primary daily leadership interface between Omnira and the founder.
It should give the founder a clear understanding of the organization without requiring manual inspection of projects, workflows, agents, dashboards, performance systems, approvals, calendars, or decision history.
The brief must answer:
What changed?
What matters now?
What should receive focus today?
What requires a decision?
What should not be started?
What risks need attention?
What is operating safely without intervention?
What does founder capacity realistically allow?
The Daily Executive Brief is not a status report.
It is not an activity feed.
It is not a collection of dashboards converted into text.
It is not a list of every task completed overnight.
It is a leadership artifact.
Its purpose is to transform organizational state into daily executive clarity.

### 8.2 The Core Thesis

A Daily Executive Brief must do three things:
Orient.
Prioritize.
Activate.
Orient
Explain the current position of the organization.
Prioritize
Recommend what matters most today.
Activate
Make decisions, approvals, interventions, and next actions easy to execute.
If the brief only describes activity, it fails.
If the brief contains many facts but no recommendation, it fails.
If the brief requires the founder to reconstruct context before making a decision, it fails.
The core rule is:
A brief without direction is reporting.
A brief with direction is leadership.

### 8.3 The Founder Experience

The founder should be able to open Omnira and understand the day within minutes.
The experience should feel like receiving a competent executive briefing from a trusted Chief of Staff, CEO advisor, and operating leader.
The brief should be:
Direct.
Calm.
Evidence-based.
Prioritized.
Honest about uncertainty.
Aware of founder capacity.
Serious about decisions.
Human in presentation.
Expandable when deeper detail is needed.
The founder should not feel buried under the organization.
The brief should make the organization feel understandable.

### 8.4 Hybrid Briefing Model

The Daily Executive Brief should use a hybrid model combining three executive perspectives.
CEO Perspective
What matters strategically?
Chief of Staff Perspective
What needs to happen today?
Board Perspective
What risks, decisions, and performance changes require attention?
The brief should not force the founder to choose between a strategic summary and an operational plan.
It should provide a concise top layer with structured depth underneath.

### 8.5 The Briefing Layers

The brief should support three information layers.
Layer 1 — Executive Snapshot
Readable in less than one minute.
Contains:
Primary focus.
Critical decision.
Material risk.
Founder capacity.
Do-Not-Start instruction.
Layer 2 — Executive Detail
Readable in several minutes.
Contains:
Portfolio position.
Project changes.
Mission status.
Approval queue.
Performance signals.
Upcoming decisions.
Layer 3 — Evidence and Traceability
Opened only when needed.
Contains:
Evidence.
Data sources.
Assumptions.
Confidence.
Decision history.
Workflow logs.
Performance detail.
Risk analysis.
The founder should receive clarity first and complexity only when requested.

### 8.6 Stable Structure, Dynamic Content

The structure of the brief should remain familiar.
The content should change according to organizational state.
A stable structure reduces cognitive load.
The founder should always know where to find:
Today’s focus.
Approvals.
Risks.
Project status.
Calendar capacity.
Do-Not-Start items.
However, empty or irrelevant sections should be collapsed.
A stable structure does not require displaying every section every day.

### 8.7 Canonical Brief Structure

The canonical Daily Executive Brief should include:
1. Executive Snapshot
2. Today’s Primary Focus
3. Founder Capacity
4. Material Changes
5. Portfolio Position
6. Project Executive Summaries
7. Mission and Workforce Status
8. Approval Queue
9. Risks and Escalations
10. Performance Signals
11. Decisions Due for Review
12. Do-Not-Start
13. Recommended Calendar Blocks
14. Upcoming Decision Points
15. Confidence and Evidence Notes
The order may adapt slightly when urgency requires it.
Critical risk should move to the top.

### 8.8 Executive Snapshot

The Executive Snapshot should communicate the day in a compact form.
A conceptual example:
Primary Focus:
Complete the Memory Stage 1 review.
Critical Decision:
Approve or defer The Prompt newsletter draft.
Portfolio Position:
The Prompt remains the main autonomy proving ground.
Familje-Stunden remains Observer lifecycle mode / Learning operating mode.
GainPilot remains hibernated.
Risk:
Project isolation validation remains incomplete.
Founder Capacity:
Moderate.
Do Not Start:
No new architecture epic today.
The snapshot should not contain unexplained metrics.
Every item should have executive meaning.

### 8.9 Today’s Primary Focus

Every brief should recommend one primary focus.
The primary focus should be the highest-value founder-directed outcome for the day.
It should consider:
Current portfolio objective.
Weekly Executive Objective.
Urgency.
Revenue impact.
Strategic leverage.
Risk.
Dependencies.
Founder capacity.
Opportunity cost.
Open commitments.
The primary focus should be written as an outcome, not a vague topic.
Weak:
Work on Memory.
Strong:
Complete the Memory Stage 1 review required before implementation continues.

### 8.10 Secondary Focus

The brief may contain one or two secondary focus items.
Secondary focus should remain subordinate to the primary focus.
Example:
Primary:
Complete the Memory review.
Secondary:
Approve The Prompt article batch.
Review one open autonomy recommendation.
The brief should not present several equally weighted priorities.
Priority requires ranking.

### 8.11 Focus Recommendation Format

The focus recommendation should include:
Recommended outcome.
Reason.
Time sensitivity.
Expected impact.
Founder effort.
What it unlocks.
What it delays.
Example:
Recommended outcome:
Approve the final Memory Stage 1 contract.
Reason:
It is a hard dependency for deeper Executive implementation.
Founder effort:
Approximately one focused review block.
Unlocks:
Implementation planning for the next Memory stage.
Delays if ignored:
Executive Intelligence roadmap progress.

### 8.12 Founder Capacity

The brief should include a founder capacity signal.
Capacity may be:
High.
Moderate.
Low.
Unavailable.
The classification should reflect more than free calendar time.
It should consider:
Deep-work windows.
Meeting density.
Personal obligations.
Study commitments.
Family commitments.
Recent workload.
Decision fatigue.
Approval backlog.
Urgent external obligations.
A free hour should not automatically be classified as high capacity.

### 8.13 Capacity Translation

The capacity signal should affect the recommended workload.
Examples:
Capacity: High
Recommended workload: One deep strategic task and two short approvals.
Capacity: Moderate
Recommended workload: One focused review and routine approvals.
Capacity: Low
Recommended workload: Approvals only. No new deep architecture work.
Capacity: Unavailable
Recommended workload: Existing automation continues. Escalate only critical items.
Capacity should shape leadership recommendations.

### 8.14 Founder Privacy

The brief may use detailed calendar context internally.
It should normally present derived capacity rather than private detail.
Allowed:
Approval availability is limited after 14:00.
Not normally required:
Detailed explanation of the private appointment causing the limitation.
Atlas may know more.
The brief should expose only what is useful.

### 8.15 Material Changes

The brief should emphasize what changed since the previous briefing.
Material changes may include:
Mission completed.
Mission delayed.
New approval required.
Performance anomaly.
Customer complaint pattern.
Autonomy violation.
Project mode recommendation.
Provider failure.
Revenue threshold crossed.
Risk severity change.
Founder capacity change.
Decision review date reached.
Stable information should be compressed.

### 8.16 Change Baseline

Material change should be measured against:
Previous Daily Brief.
Current Weekly Plan.
Active Executive Missions.
Expected performance.
Known risks.
Decision Ledger.
Current project modes.
Founder calendar.
A change is meaningful only relative to an expected state.

### 8.17 No Material Change

When no material change occurred, the brief should say so.
Example:
No material portfolio change since yesterday.
The Prompt workflows remain within approved scope.
No new approvals are required.
The system should not manufacture urgency or novelty.

### 8.18 Portfolio Position

The portfolio section should summarize the current organizational position.
It may include:
Global objective.
Active project priorities.
Lifecycle modes.
Operating modes.
Portfolio risk.
Shared dependency.
Founder capacity impact.
The section should remain short.
Detailed project information belongs in expandable project summaries.

### 8.19 Project Inclusion Rules

A project should appear prominently when:
A material change occurred.
A decision is required.
A risk changed.
A mission is active.
A review date is due.
A project mode may change.
Founder attention is needed.
Stable projects may be collapsed.
A hibernated project should not appear daily unless a restart condition or relevant event occurs.

### 8.20 Project Executive Summary

Each included project summary should contain:
Project name.
Lifecycle mode.
Operating mode.
Top project priority.
Material change.
Mission status.
Risk.
Approval need.
Autonomy state.
Recommended action.
Example:
The Prompt
Mode: Active / Build
Priority: Website news automation
Change: Five drafts completed
Approval: Article batch ready
Risk: No serious violations
Autonomy: L2 — Prepare
Recommendation: Review the batch today

### 8.21 Project Summary Depth

The founder should be able to expand a project summary into:
Detailed mission status.
Performance data.
Workflow runs.
Approval history.
Risk analysis.
Decision history.
Autonomy license.
Evidence sources.
The top-level brief should not copy all project detail.

### 8.22 Project Scope Integrity

Every project summary must remain project-scoped.
The brief may combine governed summaries.
It must not expose unnecessary raw data from several projects in one context.
Each item should clearly identify its project origin.

### 8.23 Mission Status

The brief should summarize active Executive Missions.
Mission status may be:
On track.
At risk.
Blocked.
Waiting for approval.
Completed.
Paused.
Failed.
Cancelled.
Each mission shown should include:
Project.
Objective.
Status.
Material change.
Next executive need.

### 8.24 On-Track Missions

On-track missions should be compressed.
Example:
The Prompt social publishing remains on schedule.
No intervention required.
The brief should not spend significant space describing healthy execution unless the outcome is strategically important.

### 8.25 At-Risk Missions

An at-risk mission should include:
Cause.
Potential impact.
Current control.
Recommended action.
Decision deadline.
Example:
Mission:
The Prompt website news automation.
Status:
At risk.
Cause:
Source validation is incomplete.
Impact:
Article approval may be delayed.
Recommendation:
Continue drafting but block publication until source review is complete.

### 8.26 Blocked Missions

A blocked mission should explain:
What is blocked?
Why?
Who or what must unblock it?
What work can continue?
What happens if it remains blocked?
The founder should not need to inspect task logs to understand the blocker.

### 8.27 Workforce Summary

Manager should provide a structured summary of Workforce activity.
The brief may include:
Active teams.
Completed outputs.
Significant failures.
Blocked tasks.
Capacity constraints.
Escalations.
Approval dependencies.
The brief should avoid listing every agent action.

### 8.28 Agent Visibility

Agents should appear in the brief only when:
A role is missing.
An agent failed materially.
An agent produced a high-value result.
A policy violation occurred.
A new role is recommended.
A resource conflict exists.
Routine agent activity belongs in the Operating Graph and execution logs.

### 8.29 Approval Queue

The Approval Queue is a primary action surface.
The founder should be able to make decisions without reconstructing context.
Each approval item should include:
Project.
Requested action.
Action type.
Reason.
Recommended decision.
Risk.
Reversibility.
Deadline.
Consequence of delay.
Supporting evidence.

### 8.30 Approval Priority

Approval items should be ranked by:
Damage potential.
Urgency.
Dependency impact.
Revenue impact.
Customer impact.
Reversibility.
Deadline.
Founder effort.
The most recent approval should not automatically appear first.

### 8.31 Approval Categories

Approvals may include:
Publish.
Send.
Spend.
Connect.
Change strategy.
Change project mode.
Increase autonomy.
Create calendar event.
Respond to sensitive customer case.
Deploy production change.
Delete or permanently remove data.
The category should be visible.
Different categories may have different review interfaces.

### 8.32 Recommended Decision

Executive Intelligence should provide a recommendation.
Example:
Recommended:
Approve with edits.
Reason:
The article is strategically relevant, but the source wording should be corrected before publication.
Confidence:
High.
The system should not force the founder to decide without guidance.

### 8.33 Approval Actions

The founder should be able to:
Approve.
Reject.
Edit and approve.
Request more evidence.
Request an alternative.
Defer.
Escalate to Deep Executive Analysis.
Each action should preserve auditability.

### 8.34 Approval Batching

Low-risk, similar approvals may be batched.
A batch should share:
Project.
Workflow.
Action category.
Risk class.
Approval conditions.
Destination.
Review period.
The founder should be able to inspect individual items before batch approval.

### 8.35 Approval Burden Indicator

The brief should show approval burden when material.
Example:
Approval load:
6 items
Estimated review effort:
20 minutes
Potential batch candidates:
4
The estimate should help the founder plan attention.

### 8.36 Approval Delay

The brief should explain the consequence of delay.
Examples:
No immediate consequence.
Blocks publication today.
Delays weekly mission.
Creates customer response risk.
Prevents workflow continuation.
Expires at 16:00.
This improves prioritization.

### 8.37 Risk Summary

The brief should surface material risks.
Risk levels may be:
Critical.
High.
Moderate.
Watch.
Each risk should contain:
Project or portfolio scope.
Cause.
Potential impact.
Current control.
Required action.
Owner.
Review time.

### 8.38 Critical Risk

Critical risks should appear before the normal Executive Snapshot.
Critical risks may include:
Security incident.
Customer data exposure.
Unauthorized spending.
Wrong-project publication.
Serious customer harm risk.
Shared authorization failure.
Uncontrolled external action.
The brief should make the required immediate action explicit.

### 8.39 High Risk

High risks require action during the current operating day.
Examples:
Sensitive customer response waiting.
Major publishing error before release.
Repeated workflow failure.
Autonomy license breach.
Critical provider degradation.

### 8.40 Moderate Risk

Moderate risks should influence daily or weekly planning but may not require immediate action.
Examples:
Approval backlog increasing.
Content quality trending down.
Performance data delayed.
Mission deadline at risk.
Capacity becoming constrained.

### 8.41 Watch Items

Watch items are early signals.
They should not dominate the brief.
Examples:
Engagement trend weakening.
Provider latency increasing.
Correction rate slightly higher.
Support volume gradually increasing.
Repeated watch items may become moderate risk.

### 8.42 Risk Suppression

Unchanged low-level risks should not be repeated every day.
They should reappear when:
Severity changes.
A deadline approaches.
A control fails.
New evidence appears.
Founder action becomes necessary.

### 8.43 Performance Signals

The Daily Brief should include only performance signals that may affect current decisions.
Possible signals include:
Revenue anomaly.
Traffic anomaly.
Subscriber change.
Workflow success rate.
Publishing reliability.
Customer support spike.
Cost anomaly.
Trust Score change.
Policy violation.
Routine metrics belong in project dashboards.

### 8.44 Signal Interpretation

Every performance signal should include interpretation.
Weak:
Traffic increased 18%.
Strong:
The Prompt traffic increased 18%, driven mainly by short news posts.
Recommendation: Maintain the current short-form mix while collecting another week of evidence.
Data without interpretation does not belong in an executive brief.

### 8.45 Data Freshness

Performance signals should state data freshness when relevant.
Possible states:
Current.
Delayed.
Incomplete.
Estimated.
Unverified.
Stale.
Example:
Subscriber data is delayed by 12 hours.
Do not make a growth decision from the current count.

### 8.46 Confidence

Material recommendations should include confidence.
Suggested levels:
High.
Medium.
Low.
Confidence should reflect:
Evidence quality.
Data freshness.
Consistency.
Historical reliability.
Assumption burden.
Reversibility.

### 8.47 Confidence Presentation

Confidence should be visible when it materially affects the recommendation.
Example:
Recommendation:
Keep GainPilot hibernated.
Confidence:
High.
Would change if:
New revenue or user traction appears.
Routine high-confidence information does not need a confidence label on every line.

### 8.48 Evidence

The brief should allow the founder to inspect evidence.
Evidence may include:
Performance measurements.
Manager reports.
Decision Ledger entries.
Memory records.
Calendar capacity.
Approval history.
Risk signals.
Customer feedback summaries.
The top layer should summarize.
Evidence should remain one step away.

### 8.49 Assumptions

Material recommendations should identify important assumptions.
Example:
Assumption:
Founder capacity remains limited this week.
Assumption:
The Prompt website workflow remains the highest-value proving ground.
Hidden assumptions reduce trust.

### 8.50 What Would Change the Recommendation

Important recommendations should state what could change them.
Example:
Would change if:
The Prompt publishing workflow records a serious policy violation.
This makes reasoning dynamic rather than absolute.

### 8.51 Decisions Due for Review

The brief should surface decisions whose review dates have arrived.
Each item should show:
Decision.
Scope.
Original reason.
Current evidence.
Recommended outcome.
Available actions.
Available outcomes may include:
Renew.
Revise.
Expire.
Reverse.
Escalate.

### 8.52 Decision Continuity

The brief should connect current recommendations to previous decisions.
Example:
Current recommendation:
Keep newsletters approval-gated.
Previous decision:
Approval required until 30 successful drafts.
Current evidence:
18 successful drafts completed.
This prevents the system from restarting strategic reasoning every day.

### 8.53 Do-Not-Start

The Do-Not-Start section should protect daily focus.
Items may include:
New projects.
New epics.
Unapproved autonomy expansion.
Unnecessary platform work.
Low-priority redesign.
Premature Growth operating mode.
New external integrations.
Each item should include a brief reason.

### 8.54 Do-Not-Start Relevance

The section should appear only when useful.
It should not become a permanent list of everything not being done.
The brief should surface the distractions most likely to interfere with today’s objective.

### 8.55 Recommended Calendar Blocks

Memory review.
13:30–13:50
Approval batch.
15:00–15:30
The Prompt strategy check.
The brief should not create calendar events without approval unless a future calendar mandate explicitly allows it.

### 8.56 Calendar Proposal Logic

Calendar proposals should consider:
Existing events.
Deep-work windows.
Task type.
Decision fatigue.
Approval deadlines.
Personal commitments.
Transition time.
Recovery.
The system should avoid filling every free space.
Unscheduled capacity has value.

### 8.57 Calendar Approval

The founder should be able to:
Approve all blocks.
Approve selected blocks.
Edit times.
Move blocks.
Reject the schedule.
Request a lighter plan.
After approval, events may be created.

### 8.58 Upcoming Decision Points

### 8.59 Time Horizon Labels

This month.
Quarter.

Failures.

### 8.61 Successful Automation

### 8.66 Customer-Affecting Events

### 8.67 Project Mode Changes

Yes.

### 8.68 Autonomy Changes

Workflow.

Proposed level.
Evidence.
Risk.

### 8.69 Trust Score Changes

A Trust Score change should appear when material.

### 8.70 Policy Violations

Policy violations should be shown according to severity.
The brief should include:
Project.
Workflow.
Severity.
Violation.
External impact.
Containment.
Autonomy impact.
Required action.
Minor style issues should not be presented like critical boundary failures.

### 8.71 Isolation Health

Project isolation health should appear when:
A violation occurred.
A validation failed.
A migration affected ownership.
A cross-project access grant exists.
Autonomy expansion is being considered.
Poor isolation health should block autonomy recommendations.

### 8.72 Dependency Changes

The brief should surface dependencies that materially changed.
Examples:
A hard dependency was completed.
A dependency became blocked.
A soft dependency was incorrectly treated as hard.
A shared capability became available.
A provider dependency failed.
Dependency changes often affect focus.

### 8.73 Opportunity Cost

The brief should state opportunity cost when recommending a significant focus change.
Example:
Recommendation:

Executive judgment:
The delay is acceptable because isolation is a hard dependency for broader autonomy.

### 8.74 Alternatives

For significant decisions, the brief should provide alternatives.
Example:
Option A:
Approve all five articles.
Option B:
Approve three and return two for edits.
Option C:
Defer the batch.
Recommended:
Option B.
Alternatives should be limited to realistic options.

### 8.75 Founder Override

The founder may reject the recommendation.
When that happens, Executive Intelligence should:
Record the decision when material.
State the impact.
Update plans.
Adjust missions.
Preserve the original recommendation.
Set a review date if appropriate.
The system should not argue endlessly.
It should remain honest and adaptive.

The brief should use Executive Mode.
Executive Mode is:

Direct.
Prioritized.

### 8.80 Dashboard View

### 8.125 The Risk Test

### 8.126 Failure Modes

The Daily Executive Brief architecture must prevent several failure modes.
Failure Mode 1 — Activity Feed
The brief lists events without executive interpretation.
Failure Mode 2 — Too Many Priorities
The brief presents several equal priorities and avoids ranking.
Failure Mode 3 — Approval Dump
The founder receives approval items without enough context.
Failure Mode 4 — Risk Spam
Unchanged low-level risks appear every day.
Failure Mode 5 — Dashboard in Text Form
The brief reproduces metrics without meaning.
Failure Mode 6 — Calendar Blindness
The recommended workload ignores founder capacity.
Failure Mode 7 — False Confidence
Recommendations appear certain despite weak or stale evidence.
Failure Mode 8 — Project Blending
Project summaries expose or combine inappropriate data.
Failure Mode 9 — Repetitive Briefing
Stable information is repeated daily.
Failure Mode 10 — Hidden Opportunity Cost
A recommendation does not explain what it delays.
Failure Mode 11 — No Action Surface
The brief describes decisions but does not allow action.
Failure Mode 12 — Excessive Detail
The default view becomes too long to use.
Failure Mode 13 — Excessive Brevity
The brief lacks enough context to trust.
Failure Mode 14 — Missing Decision Continuity
Recommendations ignore previous decisions.
Failure Mode 15 — Notification Duplication
The same event appears in several channels unnecessarily.
Failure Mode 16 — Healthy Automation Noise
Normal automation generates constant status chatter.
These failure modes should be treated as product and architecture constraints.

### 8.127 Design Requirements

Any implementation of the Daily Executive Brief should preserve these requirements:
Every brief must recommend one primary focus.
Every brief must consider founder capacity.
Every material recommendation must be evidence-based.
Every material recommendation should expose confidence when relevant.
Every approval item must be decision-ready.
Every risk must be prioritized by severity and materiality.
Every project summary must preserve project scope.
Every performance signal must include interpretation.
Every material change must identify what changed.
Every Do-Not-Start item must protect current focus.
Every calendar block must require appropriate approval.
Every stable workflow should remain quiet unless intervention is needed.
Every brief should connect to the Weekly Executive Plan.
Every material decision should reference relevant decision history.
Every brief must be concise by default and expandable for evidence.
Every critical incident must override normal presentation.
Every brief must reduce founder coordination burden.

### 8.129 The Daily Executive Brief Contract

The Daily Executive Brief must:
Explain the current organizational position.
Recommend the highest-value daily focus.
Show what is running without founder intervention.
Surface only material changes.

Respect founder capacity.

### 8.130 Closing Principle

The Daily Executive Brief is where Executive Intelligence proves whether it can lead usefully.
Not through abstract intelligence.
Not through long reports.
Not through constant notifications.
Through clarity.
The founder should begin the day knowing:
What matters.
What changed.
What needs a decision.
What can continue autonomously.
What risk requires attention.
What should not be started.
A strong brief turns complexity into direction.
It makes autonomy visible without making it noisy.
It protects focus without hiding reality.
It gives the founder control without requiring constant supervision.
The Daily Executive Brief is not the place where Omnira shows everything it knows.
It is the place where Omnira shows what the founder needs to know now.
