## Canonical Text

### 9.1 Purpose of This Chapter

Executive Intelligence cannot lead well if it plans for an imaginary founder with unlimited time, energy, attention, and availability.
The founder is not an abstract resource.
The founder has:
Work.
Education.
Family responsibilities.
Appointments.
Health needs.
Meetings.
Recovery needs.
Personal commitments.
Limited deep-work capacity.
Limited decision capacity.
Executive Intelligence must understand those constraints.
It must plan Omnira around the founder’s real life instead of forcing the founder’s real life to absorb unrealistic plans.
At the same time, founder-private information must remain protected.
Project Executives, Manager, Workforce, agents, and tools should not receive detailed private calendar context merely because that context affects planning.
This chapter defines how Atlas and Executive Intelligence should use calendar and capacity information to improve prioritization, scheduling, approval timing, mission planning, and founder workload while preserving privacy and human authority.

### 9.2 The Core Thesis

Founder capacity is a strategic input.
It is not an afterthought.
The quality of an executive plan depends not only on whether the work is valuable, but on whether the organization has the real capacity to carry it out.
The core rule is:
A plan that ignores founder capacity is not ambitious.
It is inaccurate.
Executive Intelligence should therefore transform private founder context into governed operational signals.
Atlas may understand the detailed context.
Executive systems may use derived capacity.
Projects and Workforce should receive only the minimum information required to plan safely.

### 9.3 Founder Capacity as an Executive Resource

Founder capacity should be treated like any other constrained organizational resource.
Omnira already needs to reason about:
Money.
Workforce capacity.
Agent capability.
Infrastructure.
Model budget.
Review capacity.
Approval authority.
Founder capacity belongs in the same executive model.
It includes more than hours available.
It also includes:
Attention.
Energy.
Cognitive load.
Decision quality.
Emotional bandwidth.
Deep-work readiness.
Interruption tolerance.
Approval availability.
Recovery state.
Two calendar hours are not always equal.
A free hour after a demanding day may not support deep strategic architecture work.
A short morning block may be more valuable than several fragmented hours later.
Executive Intelligence must learn this difference.

### 9.4 Calendar Time vs Usable Capacity

Calendar availability and usable capacity are related but not identical.
Calendar availability answers:
Is time technically free?
Usable capacity answers:
Can the founder perform this type of work effectively during that time?
A calendar may show two free hours.
Those hours may still be unsuitable because of:
Travel.
Transition time.
Fatigue.
Family obligations.
Recent meetings.
Decision overload.
Interrupted attention.
Insufficient preparation.
Recovery need.
Executive Intelligence should avoid equating empty calendar space with available deep work.

### 9.5 Capacity Dimensions

Founder capacity should be evaluated across several dimensions.
Time Capacity
How much usable time exists?
Deep-Work Capacity
How much uninterrupted strategic or creative work is realistic?
Decision Capacity
How many meaningful decisions can be made well?
Approval Capacity
How many approval items can be reviewed without overload?
Communication Capacity
How much meeting, email, or customer interaction is realistic?
Emotional Capacity
How much difficult, sensitive, or high-stakes work is appropriate?
Recovery Capacity
How much rest or unscheduled space is required?
Capacity should not be reduced to one number when several dimensions materially differ.

### 9.6 Capacity Classification

For everyday planning, Executive Intelligence may use a simple top-level capacity classification:
High.
Moderate.
Low.
Unavailable.
This classification should summarize the founder’s overall usable capacity.
High
The founder has meaningful deep-work availability and sufficient decision capacity.
Moderate
The founder can complete one focused task and routine approvals.
Low
The founder should handle only short decisions, urgent approvals, or light work.
Unavailable
The founder should not be scheduled for normal work. Only critical escalation should interrupt.
The classification should remain explainable.

### 9.7 Capacity Profile

A richer internal capacity profile may include:
Overall capacity.
Deep-work capacity.
Approval capacity.
Meeting tolerance.
Decision fatigue.
Preferred work windows.
Hard unavailability.
Soft unavailability.
Recovery need.
Confidence.
Example:
Overall: Moderate
Deep work: High between 09:00–11:00
Approval capacity: Low after 15:00
Meeting tolerance: Low
Hard unavailable: 13:00–14:30
Recovery need: Moderate
Confidence: High
The founder does not need to see every internal field every day.
The profile supports better planning.

### 9.8 Founder-Private Context

Founder-private context may include:
Personal calendar event titles.
Family events.
Health-related appointments.
Education schedule.
Private meetings.
Travel.
Personal communications.
Recovery needs.
Personal financial obligations.
Sensitive life events.
This information should be treated as Founder Private.
It must not be exposed simply because Omnira uses it for planning.

### 9.9 The Privacy Transformation Principle

Founder-private information should be transformed before it moves into operational systems.
Example:
Private input:
Dentist appointment at 14:00.
Derived executive signal:
Founder unavailable 13:30–15:30.
No deep work recommended afterward.
Project-visible signal:
Approval response may be delayed this afternoon.
Workforce-visible signal:
Do not block workflow on founder review before tomorrow.
The detailed reason remains private.
The operational consequence is shared.
This is the correct direction of information flow.

### 9.10 Privacy Tiers

Calendar and capacity access should use several privacy tiers.
Tier 1 — Atlas Private Access
Atlas may access detailed calendar context when authorized.
Tier 2 — Executive Capacity Access
Portfolio Executive and relevant planning functions receive derived capacity signals.
Tier 3 — Project Planning Access
Project Executives receive only project-relevant availability and approval constraints.
Tier 4 — Workforce Scheduling Access
Manager and Workforce receive task timing constraints without private reasons.
Tier 5 — External Access
External tools or collaborators receive only explicitly approved scheduling information.
Each lower tier should receive less detail.

### 9.11 Atlas as the Private Context Holder

Atlas should be the primary user-facing system allowed to understand detailed private context.
Atlas may use that context to:
Prepare Daily Executive Briefs.
Recommend workload.
Protect family and personal time.
Suggest calendar blocks.
Delay noncritical approvals.
Explain capacity changes.
Warn against overload.
Coordinate weekly planning.
Atlas should not automatically store every personal calendar detail as durable Memory.
Use does not imply permanent retention.

### 9.12 Executive Capacity Signals

Executive Intelligence should receive derived signals such as:
Available.
Unavailable.
Deep-work window.
Approval window.
High-load day.
Low-load day.
Decision capacity constrained.
Recovery required.
Founder response delay expected.
These signals should be sufficient for most executive planning.
Detailed event titles should remain unnecessary.

### 9.13 Project-Level Capacity Signals

Project Executives may receive signals such as:
Founder review available this morning.
No founder input expected today.
Approval window closes at 14:00.
Project work requiring founder judgment should be deferred.
Light founder involvement available this week.
A project should not know why the founder is unavailable unless the founder explicitly chooses to share it.

### 9.14 Workforce-Level Capacity Signals

Manager and Workforce should receive only execution-relevant constraints.
Examples:
Continue autonomous work.
Do not wait for approval today.
Prepare items for tomorrow’s approval batch.
Escalate only Critical issues.
Founder decision unavailable until Monday.
Workforce does not need access to private life details.

### 9.15 Calendar Event Classification

Calendar events may be classified by planning effect rather than by private content.
Possible classifications:
Hard unavailable.
Soft unavailable.
Deep-work block.
Meeting block.
Travel block.
Personal commitment.
Recovery block.
Approval window.
Flexible block.
This classification supports planning without exposing unnecessary event meaning.

### 9.16 Hard Unavailability

Hard unavailability means the founder should not be scheduled.
Examples may include:
Appointments.
Education sessions.
Family obligations.
Travel.
Important meetings.
Sleep or recovery.
Events explicitly marked protected.
Executive Intelligence should not schedule over hard unavailability.
It should not recommend that the founder ignore a protected commitment for routine work.

### 9.17 Soft Unavailability

Soft unavailability means work may be technically possible but should not be assumed.
Examples:
Low-energy periods.
Transition time.
Flexible personal tasks.
Commute.
Post-meeting decompression.
Light administrative windows.
Executive Intelligence may suggest optional light work.
It should not schedule deep strategic work automatically.

### 9.18 Protected Time

The founder should be able to mark time as protected.
Protected time may include:
Family time.
Recovery.
Exercise.
Education.
Sleep.
Personal projects.
Unscheduled rest.
Protected time should be respected as a strategic boundary.
It should not be treated as unused capacity.

### 9.19 Unscheduled Capacity

Empty calendar space has value.
Executive Intelligence should not fill every free block.
Unscheduled capacity provides:
Flexibility.
Recovery.
Buffer for delays.
Room for opportunities.
Reduced stress.
Space for reflection.
A calendar optimized to 100% utilization is usually fragile.
The goal is not maximum calendar density.
The goal is effective capacity.

### 9.20 Buffer Time

Executive planning should include buffer.
Buffer may be needed for:
Task overrun.
Context switching.
Unexpected family needs.
Technical problems.
Approval delays.
Recovery.
Travel.
The amount of buffer may depend on the day’s uncertainty.
High-uncertainty days require more buffer.

### 9.21 Transition Time

Executive Intelligence should consider transition cost between activities.
Examples:
Meeting to deep architecture work.
Travel to approval review.
Family obligation to customer escalation.
Exercise to strategic planning.
A five-minute calendar gap is not a meaningful work block.
Transitions should be treated realistically.

### 9.22 Deep-Work Windows

Deep-work windows should be treated as high-value resources.
They should be reserved for work such as:
Architecture decisions.
Strategy.
Complex reviews.
Major implementation planning.
Board-level analysis.
Difficult writing.
High-impact decision-making.
Routine approvals and administrative work should not consume the best deep-work blocks unless urgent.

### 9.23 Approval Windows

Approvals should be grouped into suitable windows.
Approval windows may be:
Morning review.
Midday batch.
End-of-day check.
Weekly approval session.
Batching reduces context switching.
Sensitive or high-risk approvals may require dedicated attention.

### 9.24 Decision Fatigue

Executive Intelligence should account for decision fatigue.
Decision fatigue may increase when:
Many approvals are pending.
Several architecture choices are open.
The founder has attended many meetings.
Sensitive customer cases require judgment.
Personal obligations are heavy.
The day already included high-stakes decisions.
The system should not continuously add nonurgent choices.
It should batch, defer, recommend, or reduce options when appropriate.

### 9.25 Decision Budget

The founder may have a practical daily decision budget.
This is not necessarily a fixed number.
The concept means:
High-impact decisions consume more capacity.
Ambiguous decisions consume more capacity.
Sensitive customer decisions consume more capacity.
Routine approvals consume less capacity.
Executive Intelligence should prioritize decision quality over decision volume.

### 9.26 Approval Budget

The approval budget represents how much approval work the founder can reasonably complete.
The system should consider:
Number of items.
Complexity.
Risk.
Required evidence review.
Expected edit burden.
Deadline.
Ten simple content approvals may be easier than one difficult strategy approval.
Volume alone is insufficient.

### 9.27 Capacity and Cognitive Budget

Executive reasoning depth should align with founder capacity.
On a high-capacity day, Atlas may propose:
Deep Executive Analysis.
Architecture review.
Strategy comparison.
Long-form planning.
On a low-capacity day, Atlas may propose:
Short approval batch.
Simple yes/no decisions.
Deferred deep analysis.
Autonomous continuation of existing workflows.
The system should not deliver a board-level decision package when the founder cannot review it properly, unless urgency requires it.

### 9.28 Capacity and Risk

Low founder capacity does not reduce risk.
It changes response strategy.
When capacity is low:
Critical risks still escalate.
High-risk external actions may pause.
Noncritical decisions may defer.
Existing safe automation may continue.
Approval-dependent workflows may queue.
Crisis rules may override normal availability.
The organization should not become unsafe because the founder is unavailable.

### 9.29 Capacity and Autonomy

Safe autonomy should reduce founder dependence.
If the founder is unavailable, workflows with valid autonomy licenses may continue.
Workflows requiring approval should wait unless:
An emergency mandate applies.
A pre-approved fallback exists.
The action can be safely downgraded.
The action can be prepared without external execution.
Founder unavailability must not be interpreted as implied approval.

### 9.30 Capacity and Do-Not-Start

Founder capacity should influence Do-Not-Start recommendations.
Examples:
Do not begin a new architecture chapter on a low-capacity day.
Do not restart a project during a high-load week.
Do not schedule customer strategy decisions after several major reviews.
Do not open a new epic before current approvals are resolved.
The founder may still override.
Executive Intelligence must state the capacity risk honestly.

### 9.31 Capacity and Project Priorities

Projects may compete for founder attention.
Executive Intelligence should allocate attention based on:
Portfolio priority.
Urgency.
Revenue.
Risk.
Dependency.
Founder expertise required.
Decision deadline.
Opportunity cost.
Not every active project deserves founder attention every day.

### 9.32 Capacity and Project Modes

Project mode should influence founder involvement.
Active / Build
May require focused review or architecture decisions.
Active / Growth
May require performance and commercial decisions.
Observer / Learning
May require periodic interpretation, not daily intervention.
Hibernate
Should require minimal founder attention.
Crisis
May temporarily dominate founder capacity.
Mode should shape planning burden.

### 9.33 Capacity and Weekly Planning

Sunday planning should evaluate the founder’s full upcoming week.
It should consider:
Hard calendar commitments.
Expected deep-work windows.
Approval windows.
Study schedule.
Family responsibilities.
Recovery.
Known deadlines.
Travel.
Existing project commitments.
The weekly plan should be built around usable capacity.
It should not create work first and look for time afterward.

### 9.34 Capacity Forecast

Executive Intelligence should forecast capacity for the week.
A conceptual forecast:
Monday: Moderate
Tuesday: High
Wednesday: Low
Thursday: Moderate
Friday: Unavailable
Weekend: Light planning only
The forecast should include confidence.
Unexpected life events may change it.

### 9.35 Capacity Confidence

Capacity estimates should include confidence when uncertainty is material.
Examples:
High confidence:
Calendar is stable and known.
Medium confidence:
Several flexible commitments exist.
Low confidence:
Family or work demands may change quickly.
Low-confidence capacity should create more buffer.

### 9.36 Capacity-Based Work Placement

Work should be placed according to cognitive demand.
High-Capacity Windows
Strategy.
Architecture.
Difficult reviews.
Major writing.
Complex decisions.
Moderate-Capacity Windows
Planning.
Standard reviews.
Approval batches.
Project check-ins.
Structured analysis.
Low-Capacity Windows
Routine approvals.
Light administration.
Status checks.
Simple responses.
Review of prepared summaries.
Unavailable Windows
No routine founder work.

### 9.37 Workload Shaping

Executive Intelligence should shape workload to fit capacity.
Possible actions include:
Reduce scope.
Split a task.
Move work.
Delegate preparation.
Batch approvals.
Delay noncritical decisions.
Create a lighter alternative.
Continue automation without founder involvement.
The system should not simply label the founder overloaded.
It should redesign the plan.

### 9.38 Task Splitting

A large founder task may be split into:
Evidence review.
Decision review.
Approval.
Follow-up.
Implementation handoff.
Example:
Instead of:
Review the full autonomy architecture.
Split into:
1. Review risk model.
2. Approve autonomy levels.
3. Review The Prompt application.
Task splitting should reduce burden without fragmenting the decision beyond usefulness.

### 9.39 Preparation Before Founder Review

Workforce should prepare founder decisions.
Before a founder review, Omnira should ideally provide:
Recommendation.
Alternatives.
Evidence.
Risk.
Opportunity cost.
Requested decision.
Estimated review effort.
The founder should not spend high-value capacity gathering context that the system could prepare.

### 9.40 Founder-Only Work

Executive Intelligence should identify work that genuinely requires the founder.
Examples:
Final strategic choice.
High-impact autonomy approval.
Sensitive customer judgment.
Major budget decision.
Personal preference.
Vision definition.
Constitutional policy.
Work that does not require the founder should be delegated.

### 9.41 False Founder Dependency

Omnira should detect unnecessary founder dependency.
Examples:
Routine approvals repeated unchanged.
Requests for information already in Memory.
Manual status collection.
Low-risk internal choices.
Formatting decisions.
Workflow retries.
Standard scheduling.
False dependency should be removed over time.

### 9.42 Founder Leverage

Founder leverage increases when the founder spends more time on:
Vision.
Strategy.
High-impact judgment.
Creative direction.
Critical relationships.
Unique decisions.
and less time on:
Status collection.
Routine coordination.
Repeated approvals.
Manual scheduling.
Operational troubleshooting.
Context reconstruction.
Calendar-aware planning should improve founder leverage, not only calendar efficiency.

### 9.43 Capacity and Revenue Work

Revenue-related work may deserve priority.
However, revenue work must still fit capacity.
Examples:
A high-value customer decision may override routine architecture work.
A revenue opportunity may justify rescheduling.
A low-confidence campaign decision may wait for a high-capacity window.
Routine revenue reporting should not consume deep-work time.
Executive Intelligence should distinguish urgent revenue activity from merely revenue-adjacent activity.

### 9.44 Capacity and Strategic Work

Strategic work requires quality attention.
Executive Intelligence should avoid scheduling strategic decisions:
Between meetings.
Late after decision-heavy days.
During expected interruptions.
Without sufficient preparation.
When critical evidence is missing.
Poor timing can reduce decision quality even when the strategy process is technically complete.

### 9.45 Capacity and Sensitive Decisions

Sensitive decisions may include:
Unhappy customer responses.
Refunds.
Child-related issues.
Health-related recommendations.
Security incidents.
Staff or collaborator conflict.
Project termination.
These decisions may require emotional as well as cognitive capacity.
The system should not batch them casually with routine approvals.

### 9.46 Capacity and Crisis

During Crisis Mode, normal capacity rules may be overridden.
However, Executive Intelligence should still:
Minimize unnecessary decisions.
Present the smallest viable decision set.
Delegate containment.
Separate facts from uncertainty.
Protect rest where possible.
Schedule the next review time.
Avoid continuous interruption.
Crisis leadership must not destroy founder effectiveness through information overload.

### 9.47 Capacity Recovery After Crisis

After a crisis, the system should expect reduced capacity.
Recovery planning may include:
Lighter workload.
Deferred strategic decisions.
Reduced meeting load.
Post-mortem scheduled later.
Temporary approval support.
Continued automation at safe scope.
The end of an incident does not instantly restore cognitive capacity.

### 9.48 Capacity and Personal Boundaries

The founder should be able to define personal boundaries.
Examples:
No work after a specific time.
No meetings during family blocks.
No Sunday implementation.
Only light planning on weekends.
Protected training time.
Protected education time.
Executive Intelligence should treat these as planning rules.
Personal boundaries should not need to be renegotiated every day.

### 9.49 Boundary Exceptions

The founder may define exceptions for:
Critical incidents.
High-value revenue opportunities.
Security issues.
Customer harm.
Time-sensitive legal matters.
Exceptions should remain narrow.
Everything should not become urgent enough to override personal boundaries.

### 9.50 Calendar Proposal Model

Executive Intelligence may propose calendar blocks.
A proposal should include:
Task.
Purpose.
Project.
Duration.
Preferred time.
Cognitive demand.
Deadline.
Flexibility.
Expected outcome.
Example:
Task:

Purpose:

20 minutes.

13:30.

Moderate.

### 9.51 Calendar Approval Model

Calendar changes require approval unless the founder grants a future scheduling mandate.
The founder should be able to:
Approve.
Edit.
Move.
Shorten.
Split.
Reject.
Request a lighter schedule.
The scheduling interface should preserve control.

### 9.52 Calendar Mandates

Future bounded scheduling autonomy may be granted.
A calendar mandate could define:
Allowed event types.
Maximum duration.
Allowed hours.
Protected time.
Maximum weekly load.
Projects included.
Notification rules.
Review date.
Example:
Atlas may automatically schedule up to two 30-minute approval blocks per week between 09:00 and 15:00, excluding protected family and study time.
Scheduling autonomy must be scoped like any other autonomy.

### 9.53 Calendar Conflict Detection

Before proposing or creating events, Omnira should detect:
Hard conflicts.
Travel conflicts.
Insufficient transition time.
Double booking.
Protected time violation.
Excessive daily load.
Repeated context switching.
Approval deadline mismatch.
The system should not treat technical availability as scheduling quality.

### 9.54 Calendar Conflict Resolution

When conflicts exist, Executive Intelligence should offer alternatives.
Example:
Option A:
Move Memory review to Tuesday morning.
Option B:
Split review into two 30-minute blocks.
Option C:
Defer The Prompt strategy review.
Recommended:
Option A.
The system should explain the tradeoff.

### 9.55 Calendar Priority

Calendar proposals should follow executive priority.
Higher priority may be given to:
Critical risk.
Revenue deadline.
Founder-only decision.
Hard dependency.
Customer-sensitive issue.
Weekly objective.
Low-priority work should not occupy the best capacity windows.

### 9.56 Calendar Stability

The calendar should not be continuously reorganized.
Executive Intelligence should avoid:
Moving approved blocks without cause.
Frequent micro-adjustments.
Filling every cancellation immediately.
Replanning for minor workflow changes.
Calendar stability supports execution.

### 9.57 Rescheduling Threshold

Rescheduling should occur when:
Priority materially changes.
Capacity changes.
A hard dependency is delayed.
A crisis emerges.
A time-sensitive opportunity appears.
The original block becomes impossible.
Small changes should not trigger full replanning.

### 9.58 Founder-Initiated Calendar Changes

When the founder changes the calendar, Executive Intelligence should update:
Capacity forecast.
Approval windows.
Mission timing.
Weekly plan.
Expected decision dates.
The system should not interpret every manual change as a strategic signal.
It should assess materiality.

### 9.59 Calendar Context and Daily Brief

The Daily Executive Brief should show derived capacity.
Example:
Founder Capacity:
Moderate.
Best deep-work window:
09:00–10:30.
Approval availability:
Before 14:00.
Recommendation:
Complete one strategic review and one approval batch.
The brief should not reveal private calendar detail unnecessarily.

### 9.60 Calendar Context and Weekly Plan

The Weekly Plan should map major founder commitments to realistic windows.
It should identify:
Deep-work blocks.
Approval blocks.
Decision deadlines.
Protected time.
Buffer.
Unavailable periods.
The weekly Gantt view should reflect these constraints.

### 9.61 Calendar Context and Workforce

Workforce should know when to expect founder input.
Examples:
Submit approval batch by 12:00.
Do not block on founder feedback before Tuesday.
Prepare alternatives before the review window.
Escalate Critical issues immediately.
Workforce should not know the private reason for timing.

### 9.62 Calendar Context and Manager

Manager may coordinate around:
Approval windows.
Founder review deadlines.
Blocked missions.
Submission timing.
Escalation windows.
Calendar-created milestones.
Manager should receive operational timing, not personal context.

### 9.63 Calendar Context and Portfolio Executive

Portfolio Executive should use capacity to:
Allocate founder attention.
Reduce active priorities.
Delay project reactivation.
Protect deep-work windows.
Balance project demands.
Recommend Do-Not-Start items.
Portfolio planning that ignores the calendar is incomplete.

### 9.64 Calendar Context and Project Executives

A Project Executive should adapt its plan based on founder availability.
Examples:
The Prompt prepares a batch for the next approval window.
Familje-Stunden defers a nonurgent strategy review.
GainPilot remains hibernated.
Omnira schedules architecture work in a high-capacity block.
Projects should compete through executive priority, not through constant notifications.

### 9.65 Capacity Events

Capacity changes may be event-driven.
Examples:
Calendar event added.
Meeting cancelled.
Family obligation added.
Founder marks low energy.
Critical deadline appears.
Crisis occurs.
Travel changes.
A capacity event may trigger plan adjustment.
It should not always trigger a new notification.

### 9.66 Manual Capacity Input

The founder should be able to tell Atlas:
I have low energy today.
I can work deeply this morning.
Do not schedule anything tonight.
I only want to handle approvals today.
This week is overloaded.
Manual input should override inferred capacity when current and explicit.
The system should not argue with the founder’s self-assessment.

### 9.67 Inferred Capacity

Executive Intelligence may infer capacity from:
Calendar density.
Meeting load.
Recent work patterns.
Approval completion.
Repeated rescheduling.
Time of day.
Task completion.
Founder feedback.
Inferences should remain probabilistic.
They should not be presented as medical or psychological facts.

### 9.68 Capacity Uncertainty

When capacity is uncertain, the system should:
Use a lower-confidence label.
Recommend more buffer.
Avoid hard scheduling.
Ask only when the answer materially changes the plan.
Prefer reversible proposals.
Uncertainty should not force unnecessary questioning.

### 9.69 Capacity Learning

The system should learn from:
Accepted calendar blocks.
Moved calendar blocks.
Rejected schedules.
Completed deep-work sessions.
Approval timing.
Founder feedback.
Repeated overload.
Preferred work windows.
Learning should improve future planning.
It should not create rigid assumptions.

### 9.70 Capacity Preferences

The founder may have stable preferences such as:
Best deep work in the morning.
Approvals after lunch.
No major decisions late at night.
Sunday planning preferred.
Shorter blocks on study days.
Protected family evenings.
Stable preferences may be remembered.
Temporary conditions should not become permanent preferences.

Critical security incident.

### 9.127 Failure Modes

Founder Capacity and Calendar-Aware Planning must prevent several failure modes.
Failure Mode 1 — Calendar Equals Capacity
The system treats every free calendar slot as usable work time.
Failure Mode 2 — Private Detail Leakage
Personal calendar titles reach Project Executives, Workforce, agents, or external systems.
Failure Mode 3 — Over-Scheduling
The system fills every free block.
Failure Mode 4 — Deep-Work Misuse
High-value cognitive windows are consumed by routine approvals.
Failure Mode 5 — Decision Overload
Too many high-impact decisions are scheduled together.
Failure Mode 6 — Approval Overload
The founder receives more approvals than can be reviewed well.
Failure Mode 7 — No Buffer
Plans fail because no space exists for delays or unexpected events.
Failure Mode 8 — Protected-Time Violation
Omnira repeatedly schedules over family, education, health, or recovery boundaries.
Failure Mode 9 — Capacity Blindness
Project priorities ignore founder availability.
Failure Mode 10 — Implied Approval
Founder unavailability is treated as permission to proceed.
Failure Mode 11 — False Founder Dependency
Work remains founder-owned despite being delegable.
Failure Mode 12 — Calendar Instability
Approved blocks are repeatedly moved without material reason.
Failure Mode 13 — Sensitive Inference
The system presents inferred emotional or health states as facts.
Failure Mode 14 — Personal Data Over-Retention
Detailed private calendar history is stored unnecessarily.
Failure Mode 15 — Crisis Exhaustion
Crisis operations overwhelm the founder with continuous requests.
Failure Mode 16 — Capacity as Excuse
The system uses limited capacity to avoid important work instead of improving delegation and sequencing.
These failure modes should be treated as privacy, product, and architecture constraints.

### 9.128 Design Requirements

Any implementation of Founder Capacity and Calendar-Aware Planning should preserve these requirements:
Founder capacity must influence executive planning.
Calendar availability must not be treated as equivalent to usable capacity.
Atlas may access detailed calendar context only when authorized.
Project Executives should receive derived capacity signals, not private details.
Manager and Workforce should receive only execution-relevant timing constraints.
Protected time must not be treated as unused capacity.
Unscheduled buffer should remain available.
Calendar events must not be created without applicable approval or mandate.
Founder unavailability must never imply approval.
Critical issues may escalate despite low capacity.
Existing safe automation may continue during founder unavailability.
Founder-only work must be distinguished from delegable work.
Approval and decision burden must be measured.
Capacity predictions should be calibrated against outcomes.
Personal calendar data must be minimized and revocable.
Every scheduling proposal should expose purpose, duration, flexibility, and expected outcome.
Every plan should preserve founder authority and personal boundaries.

### 9.130 The Founder Capacity Contract

Executive Intelligence must:
Plan for the founder who actually exists.
Treat time, energy, attention, and decision capacity as constrained resources.
Protect private calendar details.
Transform private context into minimal operational signals.
Use the best cognitive windows for the highest-value work.

### 9.131 Closing Principle

Omnira is being built to expand the founder’s ability to lead.
It should not require the founder to behave like an unlimited machine.
Founder capacity is not a weakness in the system.
It is a reality the system must understand.
The purpose of Calendar-Aware Planning is not to fill more hours.
It is to protect the hours that matter.
Atlas may understand the private context.
Executive Intelligence may translate it into capacity.
Project Executives may adapt their plans.
Manager may coordinate timing.
Workforce may continue safely.
But personal life should not become project data.
A strong Executive system knows what the organization should do.
A mature Executive system also knows what the founder can realistically do today.
The best plan is not the plan with the most work.
It is the plan that creates the most meaningful progress without damaging the human being responsible for the whole organization.
