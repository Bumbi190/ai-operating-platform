## Canonical Text

### 11.1 Purpose of This Chapter

An intelligent organization must remember its decisions.
It is not enough to remember that an action happened.
Omnira must remember:
What was decided?
Why was it decided?
What evidence existed?
Which assumptions were accepted?
Who had authority?
What alternatives were considered?
What risk was accepted?
What was expected to happen?
When should the decision be reviewed?
What actually happened?
The Decision Ledger is the institutional decision memory of Omnira.
It preserves material organizational decisions in a form that is:
Human-readable.
Machine-readable.
Project-scoped.
Auditable.
Reviewable.
Versioned.
Connected to outcomes.
The Decision Ledger prevents Omnira from becoming a system that continuously acts but repeatedly forgets why.

### 11.2 The Core Thesis

Memory remembers context.
The Decision Ledger remembers commitments.
A memory may state:
The founder prefers approval before newsletter sending.
A Decision Ledger entry should state:
Decision:
Newsletter sending for The Prompt remains approval-gated.
Authority:
Founder.
Reason:
Subscriber trust and limited production evidence.
Review condition:
Review after 30 successful newsletter drafts.
Date:
Defined decision date.
The distinction is foundational:
Memory preserves what is useful to remember.
The Decision Ledger preserves what the organization formally decided.

### 11.3 The Decision Ledger Is Not a General Activity Log

The Decision Ledger must not contain every event.
It should not become a duplicate of:
Workflow logs.
Agent logs.
Task history.
Audit logs.
Chat history.
Performance events.
Calendar events.
Approval notifications.
Those systems record activity.
The Decision Ledger records material organizational judgment.
A successful workflow run is not normally a decision.
The decision to grant that workflow more autonomy is.

### 11.4 The Institutional Memory Problem

Without a Decision Ledger, Omnira may suffer from decision amnesia.
Decision amnesia occurs when the organization remembers the current rule but forgets:
Why the rule exists.
Who approved it.
Which evidence supported it.
Whether it was temporary.
When it should be reviewed.
What would justify reversal.
This creates several risks:
Outdated policies remain active.
The same debate repeats.
Contradictory decisions accumulate.
Temporary restrictions become permanent.
Autonomy expands without history.
Founder preferences are misinterpreted.
Projects drift from strategy.
The Decision Ledger provides continuity.

### 11.5 Decision Ledger vs Memory

The two systems should complement one another.
Memory
May preserve:
Preferences.
Patterns.
Lessons.
Context.
Recurring observations.
Project history.
User feedback.
Decision Ledger
Preserves:
Formal decision.
Decision authority.
Decision scope.
Decision rationale.
Approval state.
Effective period.
Review condition.
Reversal path.
Outcome.
Memory may help explain a decision.
The Decision Ledger proves that the decision existed.

### 11.6 Decision Ledger vs Audit Log

An audit log answers:
Who performed which action and when?
The Decision Ledger answers:
Why was the action authorized?
Example:
Audit Log:
Newsletter sent at 08:02 by workflow X.
Decision Ledger:
Newsletter workflow permitted to send under batch approval policy Y for The Prompt.
Both are necessary.
Neither replaces the other.

### 11.7 Decision Ledger vs Approval History

Approval history records individual approval actions.
The Decision Ledger records material decisions created or reinforced by those approvals.
Example:
Approval:
Approve five short news articles.
Decision Ledger:
No entry required unless the approval changes strategy, policy, autonomy, or another material organizational state.
Another example:
Approval:
Approve L4 publishing autonomy for a 14-day trial.
Decision Ledger:
Required.
Not every approval becomes a strategic decision.

### 11.8 Decision Ledger Scope

Ledger entries should have explicit scope.
Possible scopes include:
Constitutional.
Global.
Portfolio.
Project.
Mission.
Workflow.
Incident.
Temporary exception.
Scope determines:
Who may read the entry.
Who may act on it.
Which policies apply.
Which projects are affected.
How the decision may be reused.
Who may review or reverse it.

### 11.9 Constitutional Decisions

Constitutional decisions define non-negotiable system principles.
Examples:
Executive Intelligence may not grant itself higher autonomy.
Customer data remains project-scoped.
Founder-private calendar details may not flow to Workforce.
High-impact actions require governed authority.
Constitutional decisions should require the highest authority.
They should not be modified by normal Executive reasoning.

### 11.10 Global Decisions

Global decisions affect Omnira broadly.
Examples:
Adopt a universal policy severity model.
Require project scope on every mission.
Define the global Damage Boundary.
Require review dates for material decisions.
Global decisions apply across projects unless explicitly limited.

### 11.11 Portfolio Decisions

Portfolio decisions affect organizational direction across projects.
Examples:
The Prompt remains the first autonomy proving ground.
GainPilot remains hibernated.
Project isolation is prioritized before broader autonomy.
Omnira platform foundations receive the majority of current focus.
Portfolio decisions belong to Portfolio Executive or founder authority.

### 11.12 Project Decisions

Project decisions affect one project.
Examples:
The Prompt newsletters remain approval-gated.
Familje-Stunden remains in Observer lifecycle mode / Learning operating mode.
GainPilot does not resume implementation.
The Prompt stale content should be archived rather than permanently deleted.
Project decisions must identify the affected project.

### 11.13 Mission Decisions

Mission-level decisions affect one Executive Mission.
Examples:
Extend a mission deadline.
Change mission success criteria.
Pause a mission.
Reduce mission scope.
Add an approval gate.
Mission decisions should not silently change project strategy.

### 11.14 Workflow Decisions

Workflow decisions affect one automation or workflow.
Examples:
Increase autonomy.
Reduce autonomy.
Change allowed sources.
Add a QA step.
Modify retry policy.
Change publishing cadence.
Workflow decisions must remain project- and workflow-scoped.

### 11.15 Incident Decisions

Incident decisions arise from:
Crisis Mode.
Security incidents.
Project isolation failures.
Customer harm.
Unauthorized spending.
Serious publishing errors.
Incident decisions should preserve:
Containment.
Authority.
Temporary restrictions.
Recovery conditions.
Post-mortem references.

### 11.16 Temporary Exception Decisions

Temporary exceptions allow limited deviation from normal policy.
Examples:
Temporary cross-project access.
Emergency publishing freeze.
Temporary budget exception.
Short-term manual approval requirement.
Break-glass system access.
Every exception should have:
Explicit scope.
Reason.
Authority.
Start time.
Expiration.
Review.
Revocation path.
Temporary exceptions must not become permanent through neglect.

### 11.17 What Belongs in the Decision Ledger

The ledger should contain material decisions involving:
Strategy.
Portfolio priority.
Project mode.
Autonomy.
Publishing policy.
Customer communication policy.
Spending authority.
Budget mandate.
Roadmap direction.
Project creation.
Project hibernation.
Project reactivation.
Project retirement.
Major architecture.
Workforce mission authority.
High-impact tool connection.
Crisis activation.
Risk acceptance.
Policy exception.
Founder authority delegation.

### 11.18 What Does Not Normally Belong

The ledger should not normally contain:
Routine task assignment.
Individual low-risk article approval.
Normal workflow retry.
Minor copy edit.
Agent status update.
Routine report generation.
Low-level technical log.
Unchanged daily priority.
Standard calendar adjustment.
These may belong elsewhere.
The materiality threshold must remain high enough to prevent noise.

### 11.19 The Materiality Test

A decision should enter the ledger when it materially affects:
Strategy.
Authority.
Autonomy.
Money.
Risk.
Customers.
Brand.
Project mode.
Roadmap.
Major architecture.
External action policy.
Organizational commitments.
The system should ask:
Would forgetting this decision create meaningful future confusion or risk?
If yes, it likely belongs in the ledger.

### 11.20 The Decision Ledger Entry

A canonical Decision Ledger entry should contain:
Decision identifier.
Title.
Decision statement.
Scope.
Affected project or projects.
Decision class.
Authority.
Decision status.
Recommendation.
Final decision.
Rationale.
Evidence.
Assumptions.
Confidence.
Alternatives.
Risks.
Opportunity cost.
Expected impact.
Effective date.
Review date or condition.
Expiration.
Reversal conditions.
Related approvals.
Related missions.
Related workflows.
Related policies.
Outcome status.
Version.
Not every field must appear equally prominently in the human interface.
The structured record should preserve the full contract.

### 11.21 Decision Identifier

Every ledger decision should have a stable identifier.
The identifier allows:
References from missions.
References from approvals.
References from policies.
References from performance reports.
References from future decisions.
Graph relationships.
Auditability.
The identifier should not change when the decision is updated.
Versions should belong to the same decision lineage.

### 11.22 Decision Title

The title should be specific and meaningful.
Weak:
Newsletter decision.
Strong:
The Prompt Newsletter Sending Remains Approval-Gated.
A founder should understand the subject without opening the full record.

### 11.23 Decision Statement

The decision statement should describe what the organization has committed to.
Example:
The Prompt newsletter workflow may prepare drafts autonomously, but sending requires explicit human approval until the defined review condition is met.
The statement should not contain unnecessary analysis.
It should be clear enough for machines and humans to interpret consistently.

### 11.24 Recommendation Field

The ledger should preserve the Executive recommendation separately from the final decision.
Example:
Executive Recommendation:
Approve a 14-day L4 autonomy trial.
Final Decision:
Remain at L2 until 30 successful runs are completed.
This distinction supports calibration and learning.

### 11.25 Final Decision Field

The final decision records the authorized outcome.
It should identify:
Approved option.
Conditions.
Scope.
Authority.
Effective time.
The final decision may differ from the recommendation.

### 11.26 Decision Rationale

The rationale explains why the final decision was made.
It should summarize:
Primary reason.
Most important evidence.
Material risk.
Key tradeoff.
Authority perspective.
The rationale should remain concise enough to read.
Supporting detail should be linked.

### 11.27 Evidence References

The ledger should link to relevant evidence rather than copying every data point.
Evidence references may include:
Performance reports.
Approval statistics.
Risk assessments.
Customer summaries.
Architecture documents.
Manager reports.
Post-mortems.
External primary sources.
Calendar capacity summaries.
Linked evidence should preserve timestamp and scope.

### 11.28 Evidence Snapshot

For important decisions, the ledger should preserve a snapshot of the evidence state at decision time.
This protects against future data changes.
The snapshot may include:
Key measurements.
Data freshness.
Evidence version.
Important source references.
Known gaps.
The ledger should not depend entirely on a live dashboard that may later change.

### 11.29 Assumptions

Material assumptions should be preserved.
Example:
Assumption:
The approval rate will remain stable during the trial.
Assumption:
Source quality controls remain active.
Assumption:
Founder capacity supports weekly review.
Future review should be able to test those assumptions.

### 11.30 Confidence

The decision record should preserve recommendation confidence when material.
Example:
Confidence:
Medium.
Reason:
Performance is positive, but the sample size remains limited.
The final human decision may still differ.

### 11.31 Alternatives

The ledger should preserve serious alternatives considered.
Example:
Option A:
Approve L4 autonomy.
Option B:
Remain at L2.
Option C:
Run a smaller L3 internal trial.
This prevents future reviewers from assuming that no other path existed.

### 11.32 Rejected Alternatives

Rejected alternatives should include a concise reason.
Example:
Rejected:
Full permanent autonomy.
Reason:
Insufficient evidence and no long-term rollback validation.
The system should not preserve every hypothetical idea.
Only realistic alternatives belong.

### 11.33 Risk Record

The ledger should preserve the material risks known at decision time.
Examples:
Brand risk.
Customer trust risk.
Financial exposure.
Project isolation risk.
Operational failure.
Founder overload.
Strategic distraction.
It should also preserve accepted controls.

### 11.34 Risk Acceptance

Some decisions explicitly accept residual risk.
The record should state:
Risk accepted.
Authority accepting risk.
Reason.
Control measures.
Review condition.
Risk must not be considered accepted merely because action occurred.

### 11.35 Opportunity Cost

Material decisions should preserve what they displaced.
Example:
Opportunity Cost:
Prioritizing project isolation delays The Prompt website autonomy by one week.
This helps future reviews evaluate whether the tradeoff was justified.

### 11.36 Expected Impact

The ledger should record what was expected.
Examples:
Reduce founder approval burden.
Increase website freshness.
Improve workflow reliability.
Protect customer trust.
Unlock future project autonomy.
Expected impact creates a basis for later evaluation.

### 11.37 Success Criteria

Where possible, a decision should define success.
Example:
Success:
30 successful runs.
Approval rate above 90%.
No P0 or P1 policy violations.
Correction rate below defined threshold.
A decision without success criteria is harder to review objectively.

### 11.38 Failure Criteria

Material decisions may also define failure.
Example:
Failure:
Any cross-project publication.
Any P0 or P1 violation.
Correction rate above 15%.
Rollback unavailable.
Failure criteria may trigger automatic escalation or reversal.

### 11.39 Decision Authority Record

The ledger must identify the authority behind the decision.
Examples:
Founder.
Portfolio Executive under mandate.
Project Executive under mandate.
Delegated approver.
Governance policy.
Crisis authority.
The authority record should include any applicable limit.

### 11.40 Authority Basis

The ledger should identify why the actor had authority.
Examples:
Founder ownership.
Approved budget mandate.
Active autonomy license.
Emergency crisis mandate.
Delegated editorial authority.
Project Executive planning authority.
This prevents authority from being assumed retrospectively.

### 11.41 Approval Record

When approval is required, the ledger should reference:
Approver.
Approval action.
Time.
Conditions.
Edited terms.
Related approval object.
A decision requiring approval is not effective until approval exists.

### 11.42 Conditional Approval

Conditional approval should preserve every condition.
Example:
Approved if:
Publishing remains limited to approved categories.
Rollback remains available.
Daily performance monitoring continues.
The trial expires after 14 days.
Conditions are part of the decision.
They are not optional notes.

### 11.43 Effective Date

The effective date defines when the decision begins to govern behavior.
It may differ from the approval date.
Example:
Approved:
July 7.
Effective:
July 10 after deployment validation.
The distinction matters for audit and execution.

### 11.44 Decision Duration

A decision may be:
Permanent until changed.
Temporary.
Trial-based.
Condition-based.
Incident-limited.
Project-mode-limited.
Duration should be explicit.

### 11.45 Expiration

Temporary decisions should expire safely.
Expiration should define:
Exact time or condition.
State after expiration.
Required notification.
Whether renewal is allowed.
Expiration should normally return the system to a safer state.

### 11.46 Review Date

Every material decision should have a review date or condition.
This chapter defines the field.
Chapter 12 defines the full review and decay architecture.
Review may be:
Time-based.
Outcome-based.
Threshold-based.
Incident-based.
Mode-change-based.

### 11.47 Reversal Conditions

The ledger should record conditions that require reversal or reconsideration.
Example:
Reverse the autonomy grant if:
A serious policy violation occurs.
Project isolation health falls below the required threshold.
Correction rate exceeds the agreed limit.

### 11.48 Decision Status

A ledger decision may have status:
Draft.
Proposed.
Under Review.
Approved.
Active.
Rejected.
Deferred.
Expired.
Superseded.
Reversed.
Cancelled.
Completed.
The status should reflect the decision lifecycle.

### 11.49 Draft Decision

A draft is still being prepared.
It should not authorize action.
Draft decisions may contain incomplete evidence or alternatives.

### 11.50 Proposed Decision

A proposed decision is ready for review but not yet authorized.
It may enter the Approval Inbox.

### 11.51 Approved Decision

Approved means the authorized actor has accepted the decision.
It may still have a future effective date.

### 11.52 Active Decision

An active decision currently governs behavior.
Systems should be able to query active decisions relevant to:
Project.
Workflow.
Action.
Policy.
Authority.

### 11.53 Rejected Decision

A rejected decision should preserve:
Original proposal.
Recommendation.
Authority rejecting it.
Reason if supplied.
Future reconsideration condition.
Rejected proposals must not authorize action.

### 11.54 Deferred Decision

A deferred decision should contain:
Reason for delay.
Interim rule.
Missing evidence.
Next review date.
Risk of waiting.
Deferred decisions must not disappear.

### 11.55 Expired Decision

An expired decision no longer authorizes action.
The system should apply the defined fallback.
Expiration should not depend on manual memory.

### 11.56 Superseded Decision

A decision becomes superseded when replaced by a newer decision.
The relationship should be explicit:
Superseded by Decision X.
The historical record remains intact.

### 11.57 Reversed Decision

A reversed decision was actively undone.
The record should preserve:
Reason for reversal.
Authority.
Evidence.
Affected missions.
Recovery requirements.
Reversal is different from expiration.

### 11.58 Completed Decision

Some decisions have a finite outcome.
Example:
Approve a one-time infrastructure migration.
After successful execution and review, the decision may be completed.

### 11.59 Decision Versioning

A decision may change without losing identity.
Versioning should preserve:
Original statement.
Updated statement.
Changed fields.
Reason.
Authority.
Time.
Affected systems.
Every material edit should create a new version.

### 11.60 Immutable History

Previous versions should remain immutable.
The current decision may evolve.
The historical record should not be rewritten.
This protects trust and auditability.

### 11.61 Minor Corrections

Non-material corrections may include:
Spelling.
Formatting.
Broken reference.
Clarified wording without changing meaning.
Minor corrections may not require full reapproval.
They should still be traceable.

### 11.62 Material Amendments

A material amendment changes:
Scope.
Authority.
Risk.
Duration.
Autonomy.
Budget.
Approval requirement.
External action permission.
Success criteria.
Material amendments require the applicable decision process.

### 11.63 Decision Lineage

Decision lineage connects related versions and replacements.
A lineage may show:
Original decision.
Amendment.
Renewal.
Temporary exception.
Superseding decision.
Reversal.
The founder should be able to understand the full history.

### 11.64 Decision Relationships

Ledger decisions may relate through:
Depends on.
Implements.
Constrains.
Supersedes.
Extends.
Temporarily overrides.
Conflicts with.
Triggered by.
Reversed by.
These relationships support the Intelligence Graph.

### 11.65 Decision Dependencies

A decision may depend on another decision.
Example:
The Prompt L4 autonomy decision depends on the project isolation policy and the publishing governance decision.
If a dependency expires or changes, dependent decisions may require review.

### 11.66 Decision Conflicts

The ledger should detect conflicts.
Examples:
One decision grants publishing autonomy.
Another requires approval for all publishing.
One decision places a project in Hibernate.
Another creates active implementation missions.
Conflicts must be surfaced.
The system should not silently choose whichever entry is newest.

### 11.67 Authority Precedence

When decisions conflict, precedence may depend on:
Constitutional authority.
Founder authority.
Global governance.
Portfolio authority.
Project authority.
Workflow authority.
Temporary exception scope.
A lower-level decision cannot override a higher-level prohibition.

### 11.68 Temporal Precedence

A newer decision may supersede an older one only when:
Authority is valid.
Scope matches.
The relationship is explicit.
Higher-level policy is respected.
Newness alone is not enough.

### 11.69 Scope Precedence

A specific decision may refine a broader one.
Example:
Global rule:
External publishing requires governance.
Project decision:
The Prompt short-news workflow may publish approved low-risk categories under L4 autonomy.
The project rule operates inside the global rule.

### 11.70 Decision Retrieval

Executive Intelligence should retrieve decisions by:
Scope.
Project.
Workflow.
Status.
Authority.
Decision class.
Review date.
Effective date.
Related mission.
Related risk.
Retrieval must respect authorization.

### 11.71 Active Decision Retrieval

Before acting, systems should be able to ask:
Which active decisions govern this action?
The result may include:
Global policy.
Project policy.
Workflow autonomy decision.
Temporary exception.
Crisis restriction.
Decision retrieval should inform policy evaluation.

### 11.72 Decision Retrieval Hierarchy

Relevant decisions should be evaluated in order of authority and specificity.
A conceptual hierarchy:
Constitutional.
Global.
Portfolio.
Project.
Mission.
Workflow.
Temporary exception.
The most specific rule does not override a higher authority unless permitted.

### 11.73 Project Isolation

Every Decision Ledger entry must preserve project boundaries.
Project-level decisions should not expose:
Unrelated customer data.
Other project strategy.
Founder-private information.
Unnecessary financial detail.
Restricted credentials.
Portfolio entries should use governed summaries.

### 11.74 Founder-Private Information

The ledger should avoid storing detailed private calendar or life information.
Preferred:
Founder capacity was Low during the decision period.
Avoid:
Detailed private reason unless explicitly required and authorized.
The decision rationale needs operational meaning, not unnecessary personal detail.

### 11.75 Data Classification

Decision entries may have classifications such as:
Public.
Internal.
Confidential.
Restricted.
Founder Private.
Customer Restricted.
Classification should control access and presentation.

### 11.76 Decision Visibility

Visibility may include:
Founder only.
Portfolio leadership.
Project leadership.
Specific approvers.
Workforce summary.
Audit only.
Customer-visible.
Not every decision should be visible to every system.

### 11.77 Workforce Visibility

Workforce usually needs the operational rule, not the full executive reasoning.
Example:
Workforce-visible:
Newsletter sending requires approval.
Executive-visible:
Full reasoning, risk, alternatives, and review condition.
Information should narrow as it moves toward execution.

### 11.78 Manager Visibility

Manager may need:
Decision status.
Effective date.
Mission impact.
Approval requirement.
Workflow restriction.
Deadline.
Manager usually does not need every strategic assumption.

### 11.79 Project Executive Visibility

Project Executive should receive the project-scoped decision record and relevant portfolio constraints.
It should not receive unrelated project decisions.

### 11.80 Portfolio Executive Visibility

Portfolio Executive may access:
Portfolio decisions.
Governed project summaries.
Cross-project dependencies.
Material project decisions.
Systemic risk decisions.
Raw sensitive detail should remain minimized.

### 11.81 Decision Ledger and Atlas

Atlas should make decisions understandable.
The founder should be able to ask:
What did we decide?
Why?
Who approved it?
Is it still active?
When is it reviewed?
What happened afterward?
Atlas should answer from the structured ledger.

### 11.82 Human-Readable View

The human-readable view should prioritize:
Decision.
Status.
Scope.
Authority.
Reason.
Key evidence.
Risk.
Effective period.
Review date.
Outcome.
It should not resemble raw database output.

### 11.83 Machine-Readable View

The machine-readable record should support:
Policy evaluation.
Mission creation.
Approval routing.
Review scheduling.
Autonomy enforcement.
Graph relationships.
Outcome analysis.
Audit.
Human and machine views should derive from one canonical record.

### 11.84 Formal Decision Summary

A formal summary may look like:
Decision:
The Prompt newsletter sending remains approval-gated.
Scope:
The Prompt newsletter workflow.
Authority:
Founder.
Status:
Active.
Reason:
Limited sending history and subscriber trust risk.
Effective:
Immediately.
Review:
After 30 successful drafts.
Reversal:
Not applicable; approval remains required until superseded.

### 11.85 Conversational Decision Summary

Atlas may say:
We decided to keep newsletter sending approval-gated because the workflow has not yet built enough production history. We will review that after 30 successful drafts.
The conversational version should not alter the formal meaning.

### 11.86 Decision Creation Flow

A canonical decision creation flow may be:
Decision identified.
Decision framed.
Analysis prepared.
Recommendation produced.
Authority checked.
Approval requested if required.
Final decision recorded.
Decision activated.
Affected systems updated.
Review scheduled.

### 11.87 Decision Activation

### 11.202 Failure Modes

The Decision Ledger architecture must prevent several failure modes.
Failure Mode 1 — Decision Amnesia
The organization forgets why a rule or priority exists.
Failure Mode 2 — Log Spam
Minor operational activity overwhelms material decisions.
Failure Mode 3 — Chat-Only Decisions
Important decisions remain trapped in conversations.
Failure Mode 4 — Missing Authority
A decision is active without valid authority.
Failure Mode 5 — Scope Leakage
A project decision is applied to another project.
Failure Mode 6 — Silent Mutation
Decision meaning changes without a new version.
Failure Mode 7 — Conflicting Active Decisions
Contradictory rules govern the same action.
Failure Mode 8 — Unimplemented Decisions
The ledger says one thing while systems behave differently.
Failure Mode 9 — Permanent Temporary Authority
Trials and exceptions never expire.
Failure Mode 10 — Missing Review
Material decisions remain active indefinitely.
Failure Mode 11 — Outcome Blindness
The organization never evaluates whether the decision worked.
Failure Mode 12 — Retrospective Rewriting
History is modified to hide uncertainty or error.
Failure Mode 13 — Raw Private Detail
Founder or customer-private information is stored unnecessarily.
Failure Mode 14 — Approval Confusion
An approval is mistaken for a broader policy decision.
Failure Mode 15 — Recommendation Confusion
An Executive recommendation is treated as an authorized decision.
Failure Mode 16 — Decision Without Execution Trace
No connection exists between decision, mission, workflow, and outcome.
These failure modes should be treated as architectural constraints.

### 11.203 Design Requirements

Any implementation of the Decision Ledger should preserve these requirements:
Every material decision must have a stable identifier.
Every decision must have explicit scope.
Every active decision must have valid authority.
Every recommendation must remain distinguishable from the final decision.
Every material decision must preserve its rationale.
Every material decision should link relevant evidence and assumptions.
Every high-impact decision must preserve risk and opportunity cost.
Every temporary decision must have expiration or review.
Every active decision must be versioned rather than silently edited.
Every superseded or reversed decision must remain historically visible.
Every decision must respect project isolation and privacy.
Every material decision should connect to implementation.
Every material decision should connect to outcomes when measurable.
Every conflict between active decisions must be surfaced.
Every expired decision must stop granting authority.
Every ledger entry must remain both human-readable and machine-readable.
The ledger must remain selective enough to preserve executive meaning.

### 11.205 The Decision Ledger Contract

The Decision Ledger must:
Preserve what was decided.
Preserve why it was decided.
Preserve who had authority.
Preserve the evidence and assumptions that mattered.
Preserve the difference between recommendation and final decision.
Preserve project scope.
Preserve review and expiration conditions.
Preserve amendments, reversals, and supersession.
Connect decisions to missions and workflows.
Connect decisions to outcomes.
Expose conflicts.
Support human understanding.
Support machine enforcement.
Avoid unnecessary private detail.
Avoid log spam.
Prevent decision amnesia.
A feature that does not support this contract should not define the institutional decision record.

### 11.206 Closing Principle

An organization does not become intelligent merely because it can make decisions.
It becomes intelligent when it can remember:
What it decided.
Why it decided it.
What it expected.
What authority existed.
What happened afterward.
What it should learn.
The Decision Ledger is the institutional backbone of Executive Intelligence.
Memory gives Omnira continuity.
The Decision Ledger gives Omnira accountability.
Performance shows what happened.
Review determines whether the decision should survive.
Without a Decision Ledger, Omnira may act intelligently in isolated moments while remaining organizationally forgetful.
With a Decision Ledger, every material decision can become part of a continuous chain:
Evidence
→ Recommendation
→ Authority
→ Decision
→ Mission
→ Action
→ Outcome
→ Review
→ Learning
That chain is how Omnira stops repeating decisions and starts building institutional judgment.
