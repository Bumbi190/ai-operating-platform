## Canonical Text

### 12.1 Purpose of This Chapter

A decision may be correct when it is made and become incorrect later.
Evidence changes.
Assumptions fail.
Projects change mode.
Customers behave differently.
Technology evolves.
Costs change.
Risks increase.
Founder capacity changes.
Authority expires.
A decision that remains active indefinitely may gradually become disconnected from reality.
Executive Intelligence therefore requires a formal system for:
Review dates.
Decision expiration.
Evidence decay.
Assumption decay.
Authority expiration.
Condition-based review.
Decision renewal.
Decision revision.
Decision supersession.
Decision reversal.
This chapter defines how Omnira ensures that decisions remain valid over time.
The goal is not to constantly reopen every decision.
The goal is to prevent outdated decisions from silently governing the organization.

### 12.2 The Core Thesis

Decisions are not permanent truths.
They are authorized judgments made under specific conditions.
Every material decision exists inside a context:
Available evidence.
Accepted assumptions.
Current strategy.
Current project mode.
Current risk.
Current authority.
Current resources.
Current time horizon.
When that context changes materially, the decision may need review.
The core principle is:
A decision remains valid only while its authority, assumptions, evidence, and operating conditions remain sufficiently valid.

### 12.3 Decision Decay

Decision decay is the gradual reduction in a decision’s reliability or relevance over time.
Decay may occur because:
Evidence becomes stale.
Assumptions become weaker.
The environment changes.
Expected outcomes fail to appear.
Authority expires.
A dependency changes.
Risk increases.
The decision is repeatedly overridden.
The project changes mode.
A newer strategy emerges.
Decision decay does not mean the original decision was poor.
It means the decision must be reconsidered under current conditions.

### 12.4 Decision Decay vs Decision Failure

Decay and failure are different.
Decision Decay
The conditions supporting the decision have weakened or changed.
Decision Failure
The decision produced unacceptable outcomes or violated its success criteria.
Example of decay:
A publishing policy was created six months ago using an outdated workflow and old risk assumptions.
Example of failure:
The workflow created a serious policy violation during its current trial.
Failure may require immediate reversal.
Decay may require scheduled review.

### 12.5 Review Dates

A review date defines when a decision should be reconsidered.
A review date does not automatically invalidate the decision.
It creates a required decision point.
At review, Omnira should determine whether to:
Renew.
Revise.
Narrow.
Expand.
Expire.
Supersede.
Reverse.
Request more evidence.

### 12.6 Review Conditions

Not every review should depend on a calendar date.
A decision may be reviewed when a condition occurs.
Examples:
After 30 successful workflow runs.
When revenue reaches a defined threshold.
After the next customer cohort.
When a project changes operating mode.
When a serious policy violation occurs.
When a provider changes pricing.
When project isolation health falls.
When a strategic dependency is completed.
Condition-based reviews are often more meaningful than arbitrary dates.

### 12.7 Review Types

Omnira should support several review types.
Time-Based Review
Triggered at a specific date or interval.
Threshold-Based Review
Triggered when a measurement crosses a threshold.
Outcome-Based Review
Triggered when expected results can be evaluated.
Event-Based Review
Triggered by an incident or material event.
Dependency-Based Review
Triggered when a related decision or capability changes.
Mode-Based Review
Triggered when the project enters a new lifecycle or operating mode.
Authority-Based Review
Triggered when permission, delegation, or mandate expires.

### 12.8 Time-Based Review

Time-based review may be appropriate for:
Project strategy.
Budget mandates.
Temporary policies.
Operating modes.
Roadmaps.
Portfolio priorities.
Founder capacity rules.
External tool permissions.
Examples:
Review in 30 days.
Review at month end.
Review quarterly.
Review annually.
The interval should reflect how quickly the decision context may change.

### 12.9 Threshold-Based Review

Threshold-based review is appropriate when the decision depends on measurable performance.
Examples:
Review autonomy after 30 successful runs.
Review Growth operating mode after 1,000 monthly users.
Review budget when 75% has been consumed.
Review approval policy when unchanged approval rate exceeds 95%.
Review provider choice when cost increases by 20%.
The threshold must be measurable and project-scoped.

### 12.10 Outcome-Based Review

Outcome-based review occurs when enough evidence exists to evaluate the expected impact.
Example:
Decision:
Increase short-form content production.
Expected outcome:
Higher website traffic and newsletter conversion.
Review:
After four weeks of comparable performance data.
The review should compare expected and actual outcomes.

### 12.11 Event-Based Review

Material events may require immediate reconsideration.
Events may include:
P0 or P1 policy violation.
Security incident.
Customer harm.
Project isolation failure.
Unauthorized spending.
Major provider outage.
Significant revenue loss.
Unexpected customer growth.
Founder override.
New legal requirement.
Event-based review should not wait for the normal review date.

### 12.12 Dependency-Based Review

A decision may rely on another capability or decision.
Example:
Decision:
Prepare The Prompt for L4 website publishing.
Dependency:
Reliable project isolation.
If the dependency fails or changes, the decision must be reviewed.
Dependency links should therefore be machine-readable.

### 12.13 Mode-Based Review

A decision created under one project mode may become inappropriate under another.
Example:
Build operating mode:
Prioritize architecture and controlled experimentation.
Growth operating mode:
Prioritize distribution and revenue.
Crisis Mode:
Pause external autonomy.
A mode change should trigger review of decisions affected by the new mode.

### 12.14 Authority-Based Review

Authority may expire independently of the decision rationale.
Examples:
Temporary budget mandate.
Delegated approval authority.
Autonomy trial.
Emergency exception.
Temporary cross-project access.
Calendar scheduling mandate.
When authority expires, the system must return to a safe default unless renewed.

### 12.15 Review Horizon

Every material decision should have a review horizon.
Possible horizons:
Immediate.
Daily.
Weekly.
Monthly.
Quarterly.
Annual.
Condition-based.
Event-triggered.
The horizon should reflect decision volatility.
A crisis restriction may require hourly review.
A constitutional principle may require review only through a formal governance process.

### 12.16 Decision Volatility

Decision volatility describes how quickly a decision may become outdated.
High Volatility
Examples:
Daily campaign allocation.
Provider routing.
Temporary approval policy.
Crisis restrictions.
Founder workload plan.
Medium Volatility
Examples:
Project operating mode.
Workflow autonomy.
Monthly strategy.
Budget mandate.
Low Volatility
Examples:
Project purpose.
Global project-isolation rules.
Constitutional authority boundaries.
Review cadence should match volatility.

### 12.17 Decision Half-Life

Decision half-life is a conceptual measure of how quickly confidence in a decision should decline without new confirmation.
Examples:
Daily focus:
Very short half-life.
Weekly priorities:
Short half-life.
Project strategy:
Medium half-life.
Architecture principles:
Long half-life.
Constitutional rules:
Very long half-life.
The concept helps Omnira avoid treating all decisions as equally durable.

### 12.18 Evidence Decay

Evidence decay occurs when evidence becomes less useful over time.
Evidence may decay because:
The data becomes stale.
The measurement model changes.
The market changes.
The workflow changes.
The customer population changes.
The source loses reliability.
The sample becomes unrepresentative.
An old metric may remain historically true while becoming strategically irrelevant.

### 12.19 Evidence Freshness Rules

Evidence should include:
Collected time.
Relevant time period.
Source.
Freshness status.
Decision relevance.
Expiration or revalidation condition.
Possible freshness states:
Current.
Recent.
Historical.
Stale.
Superseded.
Invalid.

### 12.20 Evidence Relevance Decay

Evidence may remain accurate but lose relevance.
Example:
A content format performed well before a platform algorithm change.
The historical result remains true.
Its predictive value may decline.
Decision Intelligence should separate factual validity from current relevance.

### 12.21 Evidence Replacement

New evidence may:
Confirm.
Strengthen.
Weaken.
Contradict.
Replace.
Invalidate.
The Decision Ledger should preserve both the original evidence state and later evidence.
History must not be rewritten.

### 12.22 Assumption Decay

Assumptions should not survive indefinitely without validation.
An assumption may decay because:
No evidence confirms it.
Contradictory evidence appears.
The context changes.
The assumption depended on a temporary condition.
The expected result fails to appear.
Example:
Assumption:
Founder capacity will remain high this month.
Reality:
Capacity becomes constrained.
Impact:
Roadmap and approval timing require review.

### 12.23 Assumption Status

Assumptions may have status:
Unvalidated.
Partially supported.
Supported.
Weakened.
Contradicted.
Invalidated.
No longer relevant.
Material assumption changes should trigger review.

### 12.24 Assumption Expiration

Some assumptions should have explicit expiration.
Examples:
Market demand remains stable for the next quarter.
Provider pricing remains unchanged during the trial.
Founder capacity remains moderate this week.
Source quality remains acceptable during the publishing test.
Expired assumptions require revalidation.

### 12.25 Assumption Dependency

Decisions should identify which assumptions are critical.
A critical assumption is one whose failure changes the recommendation.
Example:
Critical assumption:
Project isolation is reliable.
If false:
Autonomy expansion must stop.
Noncritical assumptions may affect expected impact without invalidating the decision.

### 12.26 Critical Assumption Failure

When a critical assumption fails, the system should:
Pause affected authority if necessary.
Create an immediate review.
Notify the relevant Executive.
Assess external impact.
Apply safe fallback.
Record the assumption failure.

### 12.27 Authority Decay

Authority may decay or expire even if the strategy remains valid.
Authority decay may result from:
Mandate expiration.
Role change.
Ownership change.
Project transfer.
Policy update.
Crisis end.
Revocation.
Security concern.
An expired authority cannot continue authorizing actions.

### 12.28 Autonomy License Decay

Autonomy should decay when trust or operating conditions weaken.
Potential triggers:
Correction rate increases.
Policy violations occur.
Rollback fails.
Project isolation health declines.
Performance becomes unstable.
Source quality weakens.
Workflow configuration changes materially.
Autonomy should not remain fixed merely because it was previously earned.

### 12.29 Budget Mandate Decay

A budget mandate may require review when:
Time period ends.
Budget is mostly consumed.
Cost assumptions change.
Purpose changes.
Vendor risk changes.
Project mode changes.
Expected value does not appear.
Remaining funds do not automatically justify continued spending.

### 12.30 Strategy Decay

Strategy may decay when:
Customer evidence changes.
Revenue fails to develop.
The market changes.
A competitor changes the landscape.
Project purpose changes.
Cost becomes unsustainable.
A stronger opportunity emerges.
Strategy should not be changed casually.
It should not survive indefinitely through inertia.

### 12.31 Roadmap Decay

A roadmap may become outdated when:
Dependencies change.
Milestones fail.
New risks emerge.
Capability becomes unnecessary.
Project priority changes.
Founder capacity shifts.
A better sequence appears.
Roadmaps should be reviewed as hypotheses, not treated as permanent contracts.

### 12.32 Project Mode Decay

A project may remain in a mode longer than appropriate.
Examples:
Build operating mode continues despite readiness for Growth.
Observer lifecycle mode continues despite strong traction.
Hibernate continues despite a time-sensitive opportunity.
Crisis Mode continues after recovery.
Mode decisions require review conditions.

### 12.33 Temporary Decisions

Temporary decisions must define:
Start.
End.
Scope.
Safe fallback.
Renewal process.
Authority.
Review.
A temporary decision without expiration is not truly temporary.

### 12.34 Default Fallback

Every expiring authority decision should define a fallback state.
Examples:
Autonomy trial expires:
Return to previous autonomy level.
Temporary budget expires:
Stop new spending.
Cross-project access expires:
Revoke access.
Crisis exception expires:
Return to standard governance.
Calendar mandate expires:
Require manual approval.
The fallback should normally be safer than the temporary state.

### 12.35 Renewal

Renewal continues an existing decision.
A renewal should require:
Current evidence.
Outcome review.
Assumption review.
Risk review.
Authority confirmation.
New review date.
Renewal should not merely update the date.

### 12.36 Automatic Renewal

High-impact authority should not renew automatically.
Automatic renewal may be acceptable only when:
Risk is low.
Scope is narrow.
The action is reversible.
Monitoring is reliable.
No policy violations occurred.
The founder explicitly authorized automatic renewal.
Even then, renewal should remain visible and revocable.

### 12.37 Manual Renewal

Manual renewal should be required for:
High-impact autonomy.
Material budget authority.
Cross-project access.
Customer-sensitive policy.
Crisis exceptions.
Constitutional or governance exceptions.

### 12.38 Revision

Revision changes part of a decision while preserving its overall purpose.
Examples:
Reduce the budget.
Narrow autonomy categories.
Extend the review period.
Add a QA requirement.
Change the success threshold.
Revision creates a new version.

### 12.39 Expansion

A decision may be expanded when evidence supports broader scope.
Example:
Original:
The Prompt may prepare short news posts.
Expanded:
The Prompt may publish approved low-risk short news categories.
Expansion requires new authority when impact increases.

### 12.40 Narrowing

A decision may be narrowed when:
Risk increases.
Evidence weakens.
Performance becomes unstable.
Founder capacity falls.
Project mode changes.
Policy violation occurs.
Narrowing may be temporary or permanent.

### 12.41 Expiration

Expiration ends the decision’s active authority.
Expiration should:
Change status.
Apply fallback.
Notify affected systems.
Update missions.
Update workflows.
Preserve history.

### 12.42 Supersession

A decision is superseded when replaced by a newer decision.
Supersession should identify:
New decision.
Reason.
Authority.
Effective time.
Affected systems.
The old decision remains historically visible.

### 12.43 Reversal

Reversal actively undoes a decision.
Reversal may be triggered by:
Failure criteria.
Critical assumption failure.
Serious policy violation.
Unexpected harm.
Founder override.
Strategy change.
Invalid authority.
Reversal should include rollback and recovery.

### 12.44 Emergency Reversal

A high-risk decision may need immediate reversal before a complete review.
Emergency reversal may:
Pause external actions.
Reduce autonomy.
Stop spending.
Revoke access.
Return to safe defaults.
Create a crisis review.
The full rationale may be completed after containment.

### 12.45 Review Package

A review should use a structured package.
It should include:
Original decision.
Original evidence.
Original assumptions.
Expected impact.
Current evidence.
Actual outcome.
Risk changes.
Authority status.
Dependency changes.
Recommended next state.

### 12.46 Review Recommendation

The Executive recommendation should be one of:
Renew unchanged.
Renew with revised review date.
Revise.
Expand.
Narrow.
Expire.
Supersede.
Reverse.
Request more evidence.
The recommendation should include confidence.

### 12.47 Review Authority

Review authority may differ from original decision authority.
However, a lower authority should not renew or expand a higher-level decision without permission.
Example:
A Project Executive may recommend renewal.
The founder may be required to approve it.

### 12.48 Review Ownership

Every material decision should have a review owner.
The owner ensures that:
Evidence is collected.
Review occurs.
Affected systems are updated.
Expired authority stops.
Outcome is recorded.
The review owner may be a Project Executive, Portfolio Executive, or designated human role.

### 12.49 Review Queue

Omnira should maintain a review queue.
The queue should include:
Decision.
Scope.
Review trigger.
Deadline.
Impact.
Authority.
Recommended review depth.
Current evidence status.

### 12.50 Review Queue Priority

Review priority should consider:
Damage potential.
Expiration proximity.
Authority level.
Autonomy.
Financial impact.
Customer impact.
Evidence change.
Failed conditions.
Dependency impact.
The oldest review should not automatically be first.

### 12.51 Review Preparation Automation

Omnira may automatically:
Collect current evidence.
Compare expected and actual outcomes.
Check assumptions.
Detect failed conditions.
Identify related incidents.
Calculate decision age.
Prepare a recommendation.
Automatic preparation does not equal automatic renewal.

### 12.52 Review Depth

Review depth should match the decision.
Fast Review
For low-impact, stable decisions.
Standard Review
For routine project policy and operating decisions.
Deep Review
For autonomy, strategy, budgets, major architecture, and risk.
Board-Level Review
For portfolio direction, project lifecycle, and constitutional matters.

### 12.53 Review Effort

A review package should estimate founder effort.
Example:
Estimated review:
Five minutes.
Required decision:
Renew or expire.
Recommended:
Renew for 30 days.
Review burden should remain visible.

### 12.54 Review Deferral

A review may be deferred when:
Evidence is incomplete.
The outcome is not yet measurable.
Founder capacity is insufficient.
A related dependency is pending.
Deferral should define:
Interim state.
Risk of waiting.
New review date.
Authority for the interim state.

### 12.55 Safe Deferral

Deferral should not silently extend risky authority.
Possible safe responses:
Maintain current low-risk state.
Reduce autonomy.
Pause spending.
Continue preparation only.
Require manual approval.

### 12.56 Overdue Review

A review becomes overdue when its required time or condition has passed.
Overdue decisions should be classified by risk.
Possible responses:
Notify owner.
Escalate.
Freeze expansion.
Apply fallback.
Expire authority.
Reduce autonomy.

### 12.57 Grace Period

Some decisions may allow a grace period.
A grace period should define:
Duration.
Allowed actions.
Restrictions.
Escalation.
Fallback.
High-risk authority should have little or no grace period.

### 12.58 Review Failure

Review failure occurs when the system cannot complete the review.
Reasons may include:
Missing data.
Broken performance integration.
Unknown authority.
Unavailable approver.
Conflicting decisions.
Missing project ownership.
The safe response should depend on impact.

### 12.59 Decision Age

Decision age is the time since activation or last material review.
Age alone does not make a decision invalid.
It is one signal among:
Volatility.
Evidence change.
Assumption status.
Outcome.
Authority.
Risk.

### 12.60 Decay Score

Omnira may eventually calculate a Decision Decay Score.
The score may consider:
Decision age.
Decision volatility.
Evidence freshness.
Assumption validity.
Outcome deviation.
Dependency changes.
Override frequency.
Policy changes.
Project mode changes.
Authority age.
The score should prioritize reviews.
It should not automatically invalidate decisions without policy.

### 12.61 Decay Score Interpretation

A conceptual classification may be:
Stable.
Review approaching.
Review recommended.
Review required.
Invalid or expired.
The system should explain why the score changed.

### 12.62 Decay Is Not Universal

Different decision components may decay at different rates.
Example:
Project purpose:
Stable.
Current publishing frequency:
Volatile.
Approved source list:
Moderately volatile.
Constitutional privacy rule:
Highly stable.
The system should not apply one expiration period to everything.

### 12.63 Evidence Change Trigger

A material evidence change may trigger review when it:
Contradicts the rationale.
Changes expected impact.
Changes risk.
Changes opportunity cost.
Invalidates a critical assumption.
Minor metric movement should not reopen major decisions constantly.

### 12.64 Outcome Deviation Trigger

Review may trigger when actual outcomes differ materially from expected outcomes.
Examples:
Revenue remains below threshold.
Founder burden increases instead of decreasing.
Correction rate exceeds expectation.
Customer trust declines.
Cost becomes materially higher.

### 12.65 Risk Change Trigger

Risk review should trigger when:

Risk velocity increases.
A control fails.

### 12.66 Authority Change Trigger

### 12.67 Policy Change Trigger

A policy change may affect existing decisions.
Example:

### 12.68 Constitutional Change Trigger

### 12.69 Project Mode Trigger

### 12.70 Workflow Change Trigger

New provider.
New prompt.
New source.
New tool permission.
New external channel.

### 12.71 Model or Provider Change

Changing the AI model or provider may affect:
Quality.
Cost.
Latency.
Safety.
Consistency.
Tool use.
Risk.

### 12.72 Personnel or Role Change

Reviewer leaves.

### 12.73 Founder Preference Change

A stable founder preference may influence decisions.
When the founder explicitly changes the preference, dependent decisions should be reviewed.

### 12.74 Capacity Change Trigger

A material founder capacity change may trigger review of:
Weekly priorities.
Approval expectations.
Project activation.
Calendar mandates.
Founder-dependent missions.
It should not automatically reopen unrelated long-term strategy.

### 12.75 Opportunity Change Trigger

A new opportunity may justify reviewing a previous Do-Not-Start or hibernation decision.
The review should evaluate:
Evidence.
Urgency.
Expected value.
Capacity.
Risk.
Opportunity cost.
Novelty alone is insufficient.

### 12.76 Repeated Override Trigger

If the founder repeatedly overrides an active decision, the system should review whether:
The decision is outdated.
The policy is too strict.
The founder preference changed.
The implementation is inconvenient.
The decision is misunderstood.
Repeated overrides are evidence.

### 12.77 Repeated Exception Trigger

Frequent temporary exceptions may indicate:
A bad base policy.
A missing workflow category.
Insufficient authority design.
A new operating reality.
The system should review the rule rather than normalize endless exceptions.

### 12.78 Review and Decision Ledger

Every review should create a linked Decision Ledger event.
It should preserve:
Review date.
Evidence considered.
Assumption status.
Outcome.
Recommendation.
Final review decision.
New review condition.
The original decision remains unchanged.

### 12.79 Review Versioning

Review outcomes should create:
Renewal version.
Revision version.
Expiration event.
Reversal event.
Superseding decision.
Silent date updates are insufficient.

### 12.80 Review and Memory

Memory may preserve durable lessons such as:
Autonomy reviews require larger sample sizes.
Founder approval burden was underestimated.
Quarterly strategy reviews work better than monthly changes.
Temporary exceptions often indicate missing policy.
The formal review remains in the Decision Ledger.

### 12.81 Review and Performance Intelligence

Performance Intelligence should provide:
Outcome metrics.
Trend changes.
Threshold status.
Cost changes.
Trust Score changes.
Policy violation history.
Founder burden.
Performance Intelligence measures.
Executive Intelligence decides renewal or change.

### 12.82 Review and Governance

Governance should enforce:
Expiration.
Fallback.
Authority limits.
Review requirements.
Renewal conditions.
Reversal triggers.
A review date that exists only in a document is insufficient.

### 12.83 Review and Manager

Manager should receive operational consequences such as:
Mission paused.
Approval rule changed.
Workflow autonomy reduced.
Deadline extended.
New QA step required.
Manager does not own the review decision.

### 12.84 Review and Workforce

### 12.85 Review and Daily Executive Brief

### 12.86 Review and Weekly Planning

### 12.87 Review and Monthly Review

Decisions renewed.

### 12.88 Review and Quarterly Strategy

### 12.89 Review and Annual Direction

Notifications should occur when:

### 12.131 Anti-Perpetual-Decision Principle

### 12.144 Failure Modes

Review Dates and Decision Decay must prevent several failure modes.
Failure Mode 1 — Permanent Temporary Authority
Trials and exceptions never expire.
Failure Mode 2 — Stale Evidence
Old evidence continues supporting current action.
Failure Mode 3 — Hidden Assumption Failure
Critical assumptions fail without review.
Failure Mode 4 — Automatic High-Risk Renewal
Authority continues because no one responded.
Failure Mode 5 — Review Spam
Stable decisions are reopened too frequently.
Failure Mode 6 — Review Theatre
Reviews occur but produce no meaningful judgment.
Failure Mode 7 — No Safe Fallback
Expiration causes uncontrolled failure or continued unauthorized action.
Failure Mode 8 — Overdue High-Impact Review
Autonomy, budget, or access continues after review deadlines.
Failure Mode 9 — Age-Only Decay
Decisions are treated as invalid merely because they are old.
Failure Mode 10 — Outcome-Only Renewal
Lucky outcomes justify unsafe decisions.
Failure Mode 11 — Silent Renewal
Dates change without versioning or authority.
Failure Mode 12 — Dependency Blindness
Related decisions change without triggering review.
Failure Mode 13 — Project Mode Blindness
Old decisions remain active after mode changes.
Failure Mode 14 — Founder Unavailability Equals Renewal
Missing response extends authority automatically.
Failure Mode 15 — Crisis Restriction Persistence
Emergency restrictions remain after recovery without review.
Failure Mode 16 — Strategic Whiplash
Every small signal reopens long-term strategy.
These failure modes should be treated as architecture and governance constraints.

### 12.145 Design Requirements

Any implementation of Review Dates and Decision Decay should preserve these requirements:
Every material decision must have a review date or condition.
Every temporary authority must have expiration.
Every expiration must define a fallback.
Every high-impact renewal must require current evidence.
Every review must assess assumptions.
Every review must assess authority.
Every review must assess risk.
Every review must compare expected and actual outcomes when available.
Every material review must create a versioned Decision Ledger event.
Every expired decision must stop granting authority.
Every dependency change must be able to trigger affected reviews.
Every project mode change must review affected decisions.
Every failed critical assumption must escalate.
Every overdue high-impact review must become visible.
Every review must preserve project scope and privacy.
Stable decisions should not be reopened without material reason.
The system must balance continuity with safety.

### 12.147 The Review and Decay Contract

Executive Intelligence must:
Treat decisions as contextual judgments.
Track when decisions require review.
Track when authority expires.

### 12.148 Closing Principle

The danger of organizational memory is not only forgetting.
It is remembering something for too long.
A decision may once have been correct.
A policy may once have been necessary.
A restriction may once have protected the organization.
An autonomy level may once have been appropriate.
But Omnira must continuously ask:
Is this still true?
Is the evidence still current?
Are the assumptions still valid?
Does the authority still exist?
Did the expected outcome occur?
Should this decision survive?
The Decision Ledger preserves history.
Review Dates create accountability.
Decision Decay creates adaptation.
Together, they allow Omnira to maintain continuity without becoming trapped by its own past.
A mature Executive system does not constantly change its mind.
It also does not mistake an old decision for a permanent truth.
