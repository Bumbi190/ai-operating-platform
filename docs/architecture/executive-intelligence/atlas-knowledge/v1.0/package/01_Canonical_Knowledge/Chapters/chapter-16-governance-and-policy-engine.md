### 16.1 Purpose of This Chapter

Executive Intelligence must be able to reason broadly.
It must not be able to act broadly without authority.
A recommendation may be strategically intelligent.
A plan may create revenue.
A workflow may be technically capable.
An agent may have access to the required tool.
None of these facts alone mean that the action is allowed.
Omnira therefore requires a Governance & Policy Engine that determines:
Is this action permitted?
Who has authority?
Which policy applies?
Is approval required?
Does an autonomy license cover the action?
Does the action cross the Damage Boundary?
Does project scope match?
Does a higher-level rule prohibit it?
What should happen if the rules conflict?
What evidence must be preserved?
This chapter defines the architecture through which Omnira converts organizational rules, human authority, project boundaries, permissions, approvals, and autonomy licenses into enforceable decisions.
The Governance & Policy Engine is the control boundary between intelligence and action.

### 16.2 The Core Thesis

Executive Intelligence decides what should happen.
Governance decides what may happen.
The two functions must remain separate.
Executive Intelligence:
Recommends.
Governance:
Permits, restricts, conditions, or denies.
Manager:
Coordinates permitted work.
Workforce:
Executes within granted authority.
The foundational principle is:
A recommendation is not permission.
Capability is not authority.
Access is not approval.
Autonomy is not freedom from governance.

### 16.3 Governance as an Operating System Function

Governance must not exist only as documentation.
It must influence actual system behavior.
A policy that says:
Newsletter sending requires approval.
has little value if a workflow can still send without checking the policy.
Governance must therefore participate in:
Decision preparation.
Mission creation.
Workflow activation.
Tool access.
Data retrieval.
Publishing.
Spending.
Customer communication.
Autonomy progression.
Crisis response.
Governance must be operational, not ceremonial.

### 16.4 Policy as Executable Organizational Intent

A policy expresses organizational intent in a form that can guide or constrain behavior.
Policies may define:
Allowed actions.
Forbidden actions.
Required approvals.
Authority limits.
Risk thresholds.
Project boundaries.
Data restrictions.
Budget limits.
Publishing rules.
Escalation conditions.
Expiration.
Review requirements.
A policy should be understandable by humans and usable by systems.

### 16.5 Governance Is Broader Than Policy

Governance includes more than policies.
It also includes:
Authority.
Ownership.
Approvals.
Autonomy licenses.
Decision rights.
Project boundaries.
Auditability.
Review.
Escalation.
Revocation.
Emergency control.
Policy is one mechanism inside the larger governance system.

### 16.6 Governance Layers

Omnira should support several governance layers.
1. Constitutional rules.
2. Global Omnira policy.
3. Portfolio policy.
4. Project policy.
5. Mission constraints.
6. Workflow policy.
7. Tool permissions.
8. Task-specific authority.
9. Temporary exceptions.
10. Crisis restrictions.
Each lower layer must remain compatible with higher layers.

### 16.7 Constitutional Rules

Constitutional rules define non-negotiable system principles.
Examples:
Executive Intelligence may not grant itself higher autonomy.
Projects are isolated by default.
Customer data must remain project- or tenant-scoped.
Founder-private calendar details must not flow to Workforce.
High-impact external action requires governed authority.
Human authority may revoke autonomy.
Material actions must remain auditable.
Constitutional rules should be difficult to change.
They should not be overridden by normal project decisions.

### 16.8 Global Omnira Policy

Global policies apply across every project.
Examples:
No unauthorized spending.
No unlogged external high-impact action.
No permanent deletion without applicable authority.
No cross-project credential reuse.
No autonomy expansion after a serious unresolved violation.
No action under ambiguous project scope.
Global policy creates consistent organizational safeguards.

### 16.9 Portfolio Policy

Portfolio policy governs how several projects share resources and strategic constraints.
Examples:
Portfolio resource transfers must remain attributable and reviewable.
Project lifecycle changes require valid portfolio authority and applicable human approval.
Shared infrastructure spend requires portfolio attribution.
Cross-project data access requires explicit authorization.
Founder approval capacity is allocated through Portfolio Executive.
Portfolio policy must not weaken constitutional or global rules.

### 16.10 Project Policy

Project policy defines local rules.
Examples for The Prompt:
Source validation is required before publication.
Short news and long-form analysis use different approval rules.
Stale content should be archived or unpublished before deletion is considered.
Newsletter sending remains approval-gated.
Examples for Familje-Stunden:
Child-related communication receives stricter review.
Sensitive customer complaints require approval.
Discounts and refunds require defined authority.
Brand tone must remain family-safe.
Local rules may be stricter than global policy.
They may not silently weaken it.

### 16.11 Mission Constraints

An Executive Mission Brief may impose narrower constraints than the project policy.
Examples:
Use only approved data sources.
Do not publish.
Do not spend.
Prepare three alternatives.
Escalate customer-sensitive findings.
Do not modify production systems.
Mission constraints apply only within the mission.

### 16.12 Workflow Policy

Workflow policy governs one workflow instance.
It may define:
Allowed trigger.
Allowed inputs.
Allowed tools.
Allowed external channels.
Approval gates.
Retry policy.
Autonomy level.
Cost limit.
Rollback.
Monitoring.
Workflow permissions must remain project-specific.

### 16.13 Tool Permissions

Tool permissions define what a tool connection may do.
Examples:
Read analytics.
Create draft content.
Publish to one specific channel.
Read Stripe summaries.
Issue refunds up to a defined amount.
Create calendar proposals.
Send approved email.
Tool capability should be constrained below the maximum capability of the external service.

### 16.14 Task Authority

A task should receive the smallest authority required.
Example:
Mission authority:
Prepare a newsletter campaign.
Task authority:
Draft subject-line alternatives.
Tool authority:
Read approved campaign context only.
Authority should narrow as work approaches execution.

### 16.15 Temporary Exceptions

A temporary exception allows a narrow deviation from normal rules.
It should define:
Rule being overridden.
Reason.
Scope.
Authority.
Duration.
Allowed actions.
Monitoring.
Expiration.
Review.
Exceptions should remain rare and visible.

### 16.16 Crisis Restrictions

Crisis Mode may temporarily strengthen governance.
Examples:
Pause external publishing.
Require approval for all customer communication.
Freeze spending.
Suspend cross-project access.
Reduce autonomy.
Increase logging.
Restrict tool use.
Crisis restrictions should not silently become permanent.

### 16.17 Governance Hierarchy

A conceptual authority hierarchy is:
Omnira Constitution.
Founder authority.
Global governance.
Portfolio governance.
Project governance.
Mission governance.
Workflow governance.
Task authority.
Tool action.
Lower levels operate inside higher-level authority.

### 16.18 Policy Precedence

When policies conflict, precedence should consider:
Authority level.
Scope.
Specificity.
Time.
Exception validity.
Crisis state.
A lower-level policy cannot override a higher-level prohibition unless the higher-level system explicitly permits exceptions.

### 16.19 Stricter-Rule Principle

Where two valid policies apply, the stricter rule should generally govern.
Example:
Global rule:
Low-risk publishing may use autonomy.
Project rule:
All child-related public communication requires human approval.
Result:
Human approval is required.
The stricter valid rule protects the more sensitive context.

### 16.20 Specificity Principle

A more specific policy may refine a broader policy.
Example:
Global:
External publishing requires governance.
Project:
The Prompt short-news posts in approved categories may use L4 autonomy.
Workflow:
Only posts passing source, quality, and brand checks may publish.
Specificity refines authority.
It does not create authority beyond the broader rule.

### 16.21 Temporal Precedence

A newer policy may replace an older policy only when:
Authority is valid.
Scope matches.
Supersession is explicit.
Higher-level rules remain satisfied.
Newness alone is insufficient.

### 16.22 Default-Deny Principle

When authority is unclear, the default should be to deny or safely limit the action.
Default deny applies especially to:
External publishing.
Spending.
Data export.
Customer-sensitive communication.
Permanent deletion.
Tool connection.
Credential use.
Autonomy expansion.
Cross-project access.

### 16.23 Fail-Closed Behavior

If the Governance & Policy Engine cannot evaluate the action reliably, the system should fail closed.
Possible safe responses:
Prepare only.
Pause.
Queue for approval.
Use read-only access.
Apply a lower autonomy level.
Escalate.
Take no external action.
The system should not fail open because a policy service is unavailable.

### 16.24 Safe Continuation

Fail closed does not always require stopping all work.
Example:
External sending is blocked.
Draft preparation continues.
Internal QA continues.
Evidence collection continues.
Approval package is prepared.
Governance should preserve safe productivity where possible.

### 16.25 The Policy Decision

A policy evaluation should produce a structured result.
Possible results:
Allow.
Allow with conditions.
Require approval.
Require additional evidence.
Deny.
Escalate.
Pause.

### 16.26 Allow

### 16.28 Require Approval

Approval should be required when:
Policy explicitly requires it.
Authority is insufficient.
The Damage Boundary is crossed.
Risk is high.
Autonomy scope does not cover the action.
A project-mode change is involved.
Spend exceeds mandate.
Customer-sensitive judgment is required.

### 16.29 Require Additional Evidence

Project scope is uncertain.
Cost impact is unknown.

### 16.30 Deny

Whether escalation is possible.
Whether the denial is temporary or permanent.

### 16.31 Escalate

Escalation is appropriate when:

Authority is unclear.

### 16.213 Failure Modes

The Governance & Policy Engine must prevent several failure modes.
Failure Mode 1 — Recommendation Equals Permission
Executive reasoning directly triggers unauthorized action.
Failure Mode 2 — Capability Equals Authority
A connected tool is treated as universally usable.
Failure Mode 3 — Approval Leakage
Approval from one action or project is reused elsewhere.
Failure Mode 4 — Autonomy Leakage
A license expands beyond its workflow or action category.
Failure Mode 5 — Policy Conflict
Contradictory active rules remain unresolved.
Failure Mode 6 — Fail-Open Governance
Actions proceed when policy evaluation fails.
Failure Mode 7 — Implicit Permission
Missing policy is interpreted as permission.
Failure Mode 8 — Permanent Exception
Temporary deviations never expire.
Failure Mode 9 — Self-Granted Autonomy
Executive Intelligence increases its own authority.
Failure Mode 10 — Project Scope Failure
The correct action affects the wrong project.
Failure Mode 11 — Over-Governance
Low-risk work is buried in unnecessary approvals.
Failure Mode 12 — Under-Governance
High-impact action proceeds without adequate authority.
Failure Mode 13 — Policy Theatre
Rules exist in documents but are not enforced.
Failure Mode 14 — Unexplainable Denial
The system blocks work without a clear reason or alternative.
Failure Mode 15 — Authority Ambiguity
No one can determine who may decide or act.
Failure Mode 16 — Governance Drift
Permissions and policies gradually broaden without formal decisions.
These failure modes should be treated as foundational architecture constraints.
