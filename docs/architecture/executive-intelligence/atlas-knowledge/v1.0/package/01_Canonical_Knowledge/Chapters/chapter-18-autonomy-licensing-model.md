## Canonical Text

### 18.1 Purpose of This Chapter

Autonomy in Omnira must never be assumed.
It must be granted deliberately.
A workflow may perform well.
An agent may be capable.
A model may be highly accurate.
A Project Executive may strongly recommend expanded authority.
None of these conditions automatically create permission to act autonomously.
Omnira therefore requires an Autonomy Licensing Model.
The model defines how a specific workflow receives bounded authority to act without requesting approval for every individual action.
An autonomy license must answer:
Which project is covered?
Which workflow is covered?
Which actions are allowed?
Which actions remain forbidden?
Which channels may be used?
Which data may be accessed?
Which tools may be used?
What budget applies?
What damage class is permitted?
Which controls are mandatory?
How is performance monitored?
When does the license expire?
What causes suspension or revocation?
What fallback applies?
Who approved the license?
The license converts earned trust into explicit operating authority.

### 18.2 The Core Thesis

Autonomy is not a property of intelligence.
It is a governed permission.
The canonical principle is:
Executive Intelligence may recommend higher autonomy.
Executive Intelligence may not grant itself higher autonomy.
A workflow may receive autonomy only through a valid license issued by an authorized decision-maker.
The license must remain:
Project-scoped.
Workflow-scoped.
Action-scoped.
Risk-scoped.
Time-scoped.
Tool-scoped.
Channel-scoped.
Budget-scoped.
Reviewable.
Revocable.

### 18.3 Autonomy Is Not Global

Atlas should not have one global autonomy level.
A Project Executive should not have one global autonomy level.
An agent should not have one universal autonomy level.
Autonomy should be attached to a specific operating context.
Example:
The Prompt short-news workflow may publish approved low-risk news categories to theprompt.nu.
The same workflow may not:
Send newsletters.
Change the homepage.
Publish sensitive allegations.
Spend money.
Access Familje-Stunden data.
Modify its own autonomy license.

### 18.4 Autonomy Belongs to the Workflow Instance

Autonomy should normally belong to a workflow instance, not merely a reusable workflow template.
A shared template may define:
Recommended checks.
Default controls.
Expected actions.
Required permissions.
The active license belongs to the project-specific instance because:
Project policies differ.
Credentials differ.
Channels differ.
Risk profiles differ.
Brand rules differ.
Customer sensitivity differs.

### 18.5 Autonomy vs Automation

Automation means that a system can perform work automatically.
Autonomy means that it is authorized to choose or execute within a defined scope without individual approval.
A workflow may be automated but not autonomous.
Example:
A newsletter workflow automatically creates a draft.
Sending still requires approval.
The drafting is automated.
The sending is not autonomous.

### 18.6 Autonomy vs Capability

Capability answers:
Can the workflow perform the action?
Autonomy answers:
May the workflow perform the action without individual approval?
Capability must never be interpreted as permission.

### 18.7 Autonomy vs Access

A tool connection may technically provide write access.
That does not mean the workflow has autonomous authority to use it.
Example:
The social publishing integration can post to Instagram.
The autonomy license may permit posting only approved video content generated by one specific workflow.

### 18.8 Autonomy vs Delegation

Delegation gives a role or workflow responsibility.
Autonomy determines which parts of that responsibility may be executed without additional approval.
A Mission Brief may delegate:
Prepare and manage The Prompt short-news pipeline.
The autonomy license may allow:
Draft.
Validate sources.
Classify risk.
Queue for approval.
but still forbid publication.

### 18.9 Autonomy vs Approval

Approval authorizes one action, object, batch, or defined policy scope.
An autonomy license authorizes repeated action under stable conditions.
The progression may be:
Per-action approval.
Batch approval.
Policy approval.
Time-limited autonomy license.
Conditional business autonomy.

### 18.10 The Autonomy Levels

The canonical Omnira autonomy levels are:
L0 — Observe.
L1 — Recommend.
L2 — Prepare.
L3 — Execute Internally.
L4 — External Low-Risk.
L5 — Conditional Business Autonomy.
L6 — Full Strategic Autonomy.
These levels describe the maximum kind of authority that may be licensed.
They do not automatically grant authority.

### 18.11 L0 — Observe

At L0, the system may:
Read approved information.
Monitor workflows.
Measure performance.
Detect events.
Identify risks.
Produce internal observations.
It may not recommend, prepare, modify, or act unless separately allowed.

### 18.12 L1 — Recommend

At L1, the system may:
Analyze.
Generate recommendations.
Compare alternatives.
Identify risks.
Propose priorities.
Propose decisions.
Propose policy or autonomy changes.
It may not prepare or execute the recommended external action unless another authority allows it.

### 18.13 L2 — Prepare

At L2, the system may create decision-ready or execution-ready artifacts.
Examples:
Draft article.
Prepare newsletter.
Create customer-reply draft.
Build campaign proposal.
Prepare budget request.
Generate calendar proposal.
Prepare code change.
Create mission plan.
It may not create the consequential external effect.

### 18.14 L3 — Execute Internally

At L3, the system may perform internal reversible actions.
Examples:
Update internal plans.
Create internal records.
Run tests.
Collect evidence.
Generate reports.
Create draft workflow objects.
Organize approved project data.
Retry safe internal jobs.
Route work to Workforce.
External side effects remain restricted.

### 18.15 L4 — External Low-Risk

At L4, the system may perform explicitly defined external low-risk actions.
Examples may include:
Publish approved low-risk content categories.
Send standard acknowledgement messages.
Update non-sensitive public metadata.
Post approved scheduled media.
Apply approved reversible content-state changes.
Create approved calendar events.
L4 authority must remain narrow.

### 18.16 L5 — Conditional Business Autonomy

At L5, a workflow may perform broader business actions under defined operating conditions.
Examples may include:
Manage a bounded content pipeline.
Respond to approved customer-support categories.
Operate within a budget mandate.
Adjust routine campaign parameters within limits.
Archive stale content.
Run bounded growth experiments.
Escalate exceptions.
L5 requires mature monitoring, rollback, policy, and trust.

### 18.17 L6 — Full Strategic Autonomy

L6 represents the long-term state in which Executive Intelligence may make and execute significant strategic decisions within a constitutional mandate.
L6 does not mean unlimited authority.
It still requires:
Constitutional rules.
Decision Ledger.
Budgets.
Project isolation.
Damage Boundary.
Audit.
Review dates.
Human override.
Emergency brake.
Revocation.
L6 should be treated as a future design horizon, not an initial implementation target.

### 18.18 Level Is Not Scope

An autonomy level describes the category of authority.
The license defines the actual scope.
Two L4 licenses may differ completely.
Example A:
Publish approved short news posts to one website.
Example B:
Send standard acknowledgement replies to support requests.
Both are L4.
They do not authorize each other’s actions.

Conceptually, it may contain:

### 18.20 License Identifier

Every license should have a stable identifier.
The identifier should be referenced by:
Workflow runs.
Policy evaluations.
Tool calls.
External actions.
Audit logs.
Decision Ledger.
Performance analysis.
Incident reports.

### 18.21 Project Scope

Every license must identify one project or one explicitly governed cross-project scope.
A license without clear project scope must be invalid.
The project scope determines:
Data.
Credentials.
Brand.
Customers.
Budgets.
Policies.
Channels.
Decision authority.

### 18.22 Workflow Scope

The license should identify the exact workflow instance it governs.
It should not automatically transfer to:
A copied workflow.
A new workflow version.
A different agent team.
Another project.
A new provider configuration.
A materially changed prompt.

### 18.23 Action Scope

Allowed actions should use explicit verbs.
Examples:
Read.
Analyze.
Draft.
Classify.
Approve internally.
Publish.
Send.
Archive.
Update.
Spend.
Refund.
Schedule.
Retry.
Escalate.
Vague permissions such as:
Manage content.
should be avoided unless broken into precise actions.

### 18.24 Forbidden Actions

Every license should define important prohibited actions.
Examples:
No permanent deletion.
No pricing changes.
No newsletter sending.
No customer refunds.
No cross-project access.
No new tool connection.
No production-code changes.
No policy changes.
No autonomy modification.
Explicit prohibitions reduce ambiguous interpretation.

### 18.25 Allowed Channels

The license should identify allowed external destinations.
Examples:
theprompt.nu.
The Prompt Instagram account.
The Prompt Facebook page.
The Prompt YouTube channel.
Approved support email account.
Approved customer-chat surface.
Authority for one channel must not imply authority for another.

### 18.26 Audience Scope

The audience may be:
Internal.
One approver.
One customer.
Existing subscribers.
Public website visitors.
Public social audience.
A defined test cohort.
Audience expansion should trigger reevaluation.

### 18.27 Data Scope

The license should define the data the workflow may access.
Examples:
The Prompt public content.
Approved news-source data.
Project-scoped performance summaries.
Project brand guidelines.
Approved customer account fields.
Aggregated revenue data.

### 18.28 Data Prohibitions

The license should identify sensitive data that remains unavailable.
Examples:
Founder-private calendar details.
Raw customer payment data.
Other project data.
Restricted credentials.
Health-sensitive information.
Child-related personal data.

### 18.29 Tool Scope

The license should identify:
Allowed tools.
Allowed operations.
Environment.
Credentials.
Rate limits.
External destinations.
A connected MCP or integration should not become generally available.

### 18.30 Model Scope

A license may require specific model classes or capabilities.
It may define:
Allowed reasoning tier.
Data-handling requirements.
Provider restrictions.
Fallback model policy.
Minimum quality threshold.
A material model change may require review.

### 18.31 Financial Scope

Where spending is allowed, the license should define:
Maximum per action.
Maximum daily spend.
Maximum monthly spend.
Approved vendors.
Approved purpose.
Recurring commitments allowed or forbidden.
Cost-reporting requirements.

### 18.32 Volume Scope

The license should define maximum scale.
Examples:
Maximum two publications per day.
Maximum fifty standard support replies per week.
Maximum one campaign adjustment per day.
Maximum ten calendar events per month.
Scale changes damage potential.

### 18.33 Frequency Scope

Frequency limits should prevent:
Spam.
Runaway retries.
Excess spend.
Audience fatigue.
Approval overload.
Cumulative quality decline.

### 18.34 Time Scope

A license should define when actions may occur.
Examples:
Only during business hours.
Only after daily source validation.
Only during a trial period.
Only before a campaign deadline.
Not during Crisis Mode.

### 18.35 Damage-Class Scope

The license should define the maximum permitted Damage Boundary class.
Example:
Permitted:
D0 and D1 actions.
Requires approval:
D2.
Prohibited:
D3 and D4.
The workflow should not classify its own high-impact action downward to preserve autonomy.

### 18.36 Risk Categories

The license may define allowed and forbidden risk categories.
Example:
Allowed:
Low-risk public content.
Forbidden:
Health, legal, financial, child-sensitive, or defamatory content.

### 18.37 Required Controls

Controls may include:
Project-scope validation.
Source validation.
Brand validation.
Quality threshold.
Duplicate detection.
Idempotency.
Budget check.
Rate limit.
Rollback.
Logging.
Post-action verification.
Human escalation.
A license is valid only when required controls are functioning.

### 18.38 Monitoring Requirements

Monitoring should define:
Metrics.
Thresholds.
Frequency.
Owner.
Alert routing.
Review period.
Stopping conditions.
Autonomy without monitoring is ungoverned delegation.

### 18.39 Success Criteria

A trial or active license should define success.
Examples:
Thirty successful runs.
Approval-equivalent quality above 95%.
Correction rate below 5%.
No P0 or P1 violations.
No wrong-project action.
Rollback success rate 100%.
Founder override rate below threshold.

### 18.40 Failure Criteria

Failure criteria may include:
Any P0 violation.
Any cross-project action.
Repeated P1 violations.
Correction rate above threshold.
Uncontrolled spend.
Rollback failure.
Monitoring outage.
Trust Score collapse.

### 18.41 Stopping Conditions

Stopping conditions should define when the license is automatically suspended.
Examples:
Project enters Crisis Mode.
Critical source validation fails.
Budget is exhausted.
Policy service is unavailable.
Credential is revoked.
Workflow changes materially.
Required monitoring stops.

### 18.42 Fallback Level

Every license should define a fallback.
Examples:
L4 → L2:
Stop publishing, continue drafting.
L5 → L3:
Stop external action, continue internal operation.
L3 → L1:
Stop execution, continue recommendation.
Any level → L0:
Observe only.

### 18.43 Rollback

Rollback should define:
Which action can be reversed.
How quickly.
By whom.
Through which tool.
What cannot be fully restored.
How affected customers are handled.
Technical rollback does not guarantee trust recovery.

### 18.44 License Duration

A license may be:
One-time.
Run-limited.
Time-limited.
Threshold-limited.
Project-mode-limited.
Campaign-limited.
Indefinite until review.
High-impact licenses should rarely be indefinite.

### 18.45 Expiration

Expiration should:
Change license status.
Stop new autonomous actions.
Apply fallback.
Notify affected systems.
Preserve history.
Create review if needed.

### 18.46 Review Date

Every license should have a review date or review condition.
Possible conditions:
After thirty runs.
After fourteen days.
At month end.
When workflow version changes.
When project mode changes.
After any serious violation.

### 18.47 Approving Authority

The license should identify who approved it.
Possible authorities:
Founder.
Authorized portfolio approver.
Authorized project owner.
Delegated specialist approver.
Governance process.
The workflow may not approve its own license.

### 18.48 Decision Ledger Reference

Every material autonomy grant should reference the Decision Ledger decision that authorized it.
The decision should preserve:
Why autonomy was granted.
Evidence.
Risk.
Alternatives.
Conditions.
Review.
Authority.

### 18.49 License Status

A license may have status:
Draft.
Proposed.
Under Review.
Approved.
Scheduled.
Active.
Restricted.
Suspended.
Expired.
Revoked.
Superseded.
Completed.

### 18.50 Draft License

A Draft license is incomplete.
It grants no authority.

### 18.51 Proposed License

A Proposed license is ready for review but inactive.

### 18.52 Approved License

An Approved license has authority but may wait for:
Effective date.
Deployment.
Validation.
Credential setup.
Monitoring activation.

### 18.53 Active License

An Active license currently grants autonomy within its exact scope.

### 18.54 Restricted License

A Restricted license remains active with narrower conditions.
Example:
Publishing remains allowed to the website.
Social-channel publishing is suspended.

### 18.55 Suspended License

A Suspended license grants no new autonomous execution.
Safe preparation may continue if another authority allows it.

### 18.56 Expired License

An Expired license no longer grants authority.
Expiration should be enforced automatically.

### 18.57 Revoked License

A Revoked license has been actively withdrawn.
Revocation may result from:
Violation.
Risk change.
Founder decision.
Security issue.
Project-mode change.
Strategy change.

### 18.58 Superseded License

A newer license may replace an older one.
Both should remain historically visible.

### 18.59 License Versioning

A material license change should create a new version.
Material changes include:
Autonomy level.
Allowed action.
Channel.
Data scope.
Budget.
Volume.
Damage class.
Monitoring.
Expiration.
Fallback.

### 18.60 Action Mutation

If the object or action changes after approval, the license must be reevaluated.
Examples:
Article category changes.
Audience expands.
Financial amount increases.
Destination changes.
Customer case becomes sensitive.
Content contains a new claim.

### 18.61 Workflow Version Changes

A material workflow change may invalidate earned trust.
Changes include:
Prompt.
Model.
Provider.
Tool.
Data source.
Agent composition.
QA process.
Output schema.
Publishing destination.

### 18.62 Trust Does Not Transfer Automatically

Trust earned by one workflow version should not fully transfer to a materially different version.
The new version may begin with:
Reduced authority.
Trial status.
Stronger monitoring.
Smaller scale.

### 18.63 Autonomy Request

A request for autonomy should include:
Workflow.
Current level.
Requested level.
Business reason.
Evidence.
Successful runs.
Approval history.
Correction rate.
Violations.
Risk.
Controls.
Rollback.
Trial proposal.
Review date.

### 18.64 Autonomy Recommendation

Executive Intelligence may recommend:
Grant.
Grant with narrower scope.
Grant as trial.
Maintain current level.
Reduce level.
Suspend.
Revoke.
Request more evidence.

### 18.65 License Readiness

A workflow may be ready for licensing when:
Scope is stable.
Outputs are measurable.
Policy is clear.
Project isolation is reliable.
Monitoring exists.
Rollback is tested.
Violation handling exists.
Authority is defined.

### 18.66 Evidence Requirements

Evidence may include:
Successful workflow runs.
Approval outcomes.
Edit rates.
Rejection rates.
Performance results.
Incident history.
Near misses.
Trust Score.
Rollback tests.
Project-isolation tests.
Monitoring reliability.

### 18.67 Successful Runs

A successful run should mean more than technical completion.
It should include:
Correct project.
Correct scope.
Correct destination.
Policy compliance.
Quality threshold.
Expected outcome.
No hidden side effect.

### 18.68 Approval Equivalence

A workflow may demonstrate autonomy readiness when its outputs are repeatedly approved without material changes.
Approval equivalence may consider:
Unchanged approval rate.
Minor-edit rate.
Major-edit rate.
Rejection rate.
Reason for corrections.
Risk category.

### 18.69 Correction Rate

Correction rate should distinguish:
Formatting correction.
Style correction.
Factual correction.
Policy correction.
Project-scope correction.
Sensitive-risk correction.
A low total correction rate may hide serious rare errors.

### 18.70 Violation History

Any autonomy decision should consider:
P0 violations.
P1 violations.
Repeated P2 violations.
Near misses.
Control failures.
Unreported incidents.

### 18.71 Trust Score

Trust Score may summarize behavioral evidence.
It should not issue the license automatically.
Trust Score is evidence.
Governance and authorized human authority grant the license.

### 18.72 Trust Score Limitations

A high score cannot override:
Damage Boundary.
Missing rollback.
Project-isolation failure.
Constitutional prohibition.
Expired authority.
Sensitive action category.

### 18.73 Trial License

A Trial License is the preferred mechanism for uncertain autonomy expansion.
It should define:
Narrow action scope.
Small audience.
Limited volume.
Short duration.
Strong monitoring.
Immediate fallback.
Explicit success and failure criteria.

### 18.74 Trial Philosophy

A trial should reduce uncertainty without exposing the organization to disproportionate harm.
The purpose is:
Learn safely.
Validate controls.
Measure quality.
Measure founder burden.
Test rollback.
Calibrate Trust Score.

### 18.75 Trial Scale

Trial scale may be limited by:
Number of actions.
Number of customers.
One channel.
One content category.
Small budget.
Short time period.

### 18.76 Trial Expansion

A trial may expand only after:
Success criteria are met.
No serious violations occurred.
Monitoring remained reliable.
Rollback remained available.
Review was completed.

### 18.77 Trial Failure

A failed trial should:
Suspend autonomy.
Apply fallback.
Preserve evidence.
Create review.
Update Trust Score.
Identify required changes.
Prevent automatic retry at the same scope.

### 18.78 License Progression

A canonical progression may be:
Observe.
Recommend.
Prepare.
Execute internally.
External low-risk trial.
Stable external low-risk autonomy.
Conditional business autonomy.
Strategic autonomy.
Progression should not skip required readiness gates.

### 18.79 No Automatic Promotion

A workflow should not move to a higher level merely because time passed.
Promotion requires:
Evidence.
Review.
Valid authority.
Updated risk analysis.
License issuance.

### 18.80 No Self-Promotion

A workflow, agent, Project Executive, or Executive Intelligence component may not activate broader authority for itself.
It may only propose it.

### 18.81 Promotion Gate

A promotion gate should ask:
Has trust been earned?
Is the workflow stable?
Are policies complete?
Is the Damage Boundary respected?
Is project isolation reliable?
Is monitoring active?
Is rollback proven?
Does the business value justify autonomy?

### 18.82 Demotion

Autonomy should be reduced when:
Risk increases.
Corrections increase.
Policy violations occur.
Monitoring fails.
Workflow changes materially.
Founder trust decreases.
Project mode changes.
Damage occurs.

### 18.83 Automatic Demotion

Policy may permit automatic demotion to a safer level.
Example:
Any P1 violation:
Suspend L4 and return to L2.
Monitoring outage:
Return to L2 until restored.
Budget threshold reached:
Stop spend actions.
Automatic demotion is safer than automatic promotion.

### 18.84 Manual Demotion

The founder or authorized governance role may reduce autonomy at any time.
The system should apply the reduction immediately or at the earliest safe point.

### 18.85 Suspension

Suspension is appropriate when:
Investigation is required.
Evidence is incomplete.
Risk may be temporary.
A provider outage affects reliability.
A policy conflict exists.

### 18.86 Revocation

Revocation is appropriate when:
The authority is no longer justified.
A serious violation occurred.
The workflow is retired.
The project is hibernated or archived.
The business purpose no longer exists.

### 18.87 Renewal

A license may be renewed when:
Success criteria remain satisfied.
Risk remains acceptable.
Controls remain active.
Business value remains valid.
Authority remains valid.
Scope remains appropriate.

### 18.88 Renewal Is a New Decision

Renewal should preserve:
Current evidence.
Outcome.
Violations.
Changes.
Updated expiration.
New review date.
Approving authority.
It should not silently extend the date.

### 18.89 Automatic Renewal

Automatic renewal should be limited to low-risk licenses explicitly designed for it.
It should require:
No serious violations.
Stable controls.
Stable scope.
Stable workflow version.
Reliable monitoring.
Short renewal period.
Easy revocation.

### 18.90 High-Impact Renewal

### 18.271 Failure Modes

The Autonomy Licensing Model must prevent several failure modes.
Failure Mode 1 — Global Autonomy
One autonomy level is applied across unrelated projects and actions.
Failure Mode 2 — Capability Equals Authority
A workflow acts because it can.
Failure Mode 3 — Tool Access Equals Autonomy
A connected integration becomes broadly usable.
Failure Mode 4 — Self-Promotion
Executive Intelligence or a workflow grants itself greater authority.
Failure Mode 5 — Scope Leakage
A license is reused across projects, channels, or workflows.
Failure Mode 6 — Workflow-Version Leakage
Authority survives a material workflow change.
Failure Mode 7 — Permanent Trial
Temporary authority never expires.
Failure Mode 8 — Promotion by Approval Fatigue
Autonomy increases only because the founder is overloaded.
Failure Mode 9 — Success-Count Blindness
Many successful runs hide one severe failure.
Failure Mode 10 — Unmonitored Autonomy
External action continues without reliable monitoring.
Failure Mode 11 — No Fallback
Expiration or violation creates uncontrolled behavior.
Failure Mode 12 — Scale Blindness
Small-scope authority expands to large scale.
Failure Mode 13 — License Evasion
Actions are renamed, split, copied, or rerouted.
Failure Mode 14 — Shadow Autonomy
Legacy workflows act without formal licenses.
Failure Mode 15 — Autonomy Without Value
The workflow acts independently but creates little business benefit.
Failure Mode 16 — Crisis Persistence
Normal autonomy remains active during a serious incident.
These failure modes should be treated as foundational governance constraints.

### 18.272 Design Requirements

Any implementation of the Autonomy Licensing Model should preserve these requirements:
Every autonomous external action must be covered by a valid license or specific approval.
Every license must have explicit project scope.
Every license must identify a specific workflow instance.
Every license must define allowed and forbidden actions.
Every license must define channels, data, tools, scale, frequency, and time scope where relevant.
Every license must define its maximum permitted damage class.
Every license must define required controls and monitoring.
Every license must define expiration or review conditions.
Every license must define a fallback level.
Every material license must connect to the Decision Ledger.
Every promotion must require evidence and valid authority.
No component may promote its own autonomy.
Every material workflow change must trigger license review.
Every expired license must stop granting authority.
Every serious violation must suspend, reduce, or revoke authority according to policy.
Every project-mode change must review affected licenses.
Every autonomous action must remain traceable to its license.
Every license must be revocable.
Every autonomy decision must consider business value and founder burden, not only technical success.
Full autonomy must remain governed, auditable, bounded, and reversible where possible.

### 18.274 The Autonomy Licensing Contract

Omnira must:
Treat autonomy as licensed authority.
Keep authority project-, workflow-, action-, risk-, and time-scoped.
Separate capability from permission.
Separate recommendation from approval.
Require valid human or constitutional authority for promotion.
Use trials to reduce uncertainty.
Require monitoring and rollback.
Reduce autonomy when conditions weaken.
Expire temporary authority safely.
Prevent authority transfer between unrelated contexts.
Preserve every grant, renewal, restriction, suspension, and revocation.
Measure business value as well as technical reliability.
Reduce founder burden without weakening safety.
Keep human override and emergency control real.
Ensure that greater autonomy always creates stronger accountability, not less.
A feature that does not support this contract should not grant repeated autonomous authority.

### 18.275 Closing Principle

Autonomy should not be something Omnira claims.
It should be something a workflow earns and the organization grants.
A valid autonomy license must make it possible to answer:
What may this workflow do?
For which project?
Using which tools?
To which audience?
At what scale?
Within which budget?
Under which controls?
For how long?
Who approved it?
What stops it?
What happens when it fails?
At low maturity, workflows should observe, recommend, and prepare.
As trust grows, they may execute internally.
When evidence, controls, and value are strong, they may perform bounded external actions.
Eventually, selected workflows may receive conditional business autonomy.
But no workflow should become more autonomous than its governance, monitoring, rollback, and demonstrated judgment can support.
Autonomy in Omnira is not the absence of permission.
It is permission made precise.
