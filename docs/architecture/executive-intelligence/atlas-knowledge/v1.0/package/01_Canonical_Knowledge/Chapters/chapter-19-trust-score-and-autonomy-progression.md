## Canonical Text

### 19.1 Purpose of This Chapter

Autonomy should increase only when demonstrated behavior justifies greater trust.
A workflow may complete many runs.
A model may produce impressive output.
An agent may receive repeated approvals.
A project may benefit from faster execution.
None of these signals alone prove that broader autonomy is safe or valuable.
Omnira therefore requires a Trust Score system that converts observed workflow behavior into structured evidence for autonomy decisions.
The Trust Score should help answer:
How reliably does this workflow operate?
How often are its outputs approved unchanged?
What kinds of corrections are required?
Does it remain inside project and policy boundaries?
Does it create meaningful business value?
Does it reduce founder burden?
How stable is its performance?
How serious are its violations or near misses?
Is the available evidence sufficient?
Is the workflow ready for more autonomy?
Should its current autonomy be maintained, restricted, or reduced?
The Trust Score supports autonomy progression.
It does not grant autonomy.

### 19.2 The Core Thesis

Trust must be earned through observable behavior.
It must remain:
Scoped.
Evidence-based.
Version-aware.
Risk-sensitive.
Time-sensitive.
Explainable.
Reversible.
The foundational principle is:
Trust Score is evidence for an autonomy decision.
Trust Score is not authority.
A high score may support a recommendation for broader autonomy.
Only a valid governance decision and autonomy license may grant that authority.

### 19.3 Trust Is Contextual

Trust should not exist as one global score for Atlas, an agent, or a model.
Trust belongs to a defined operating context.
That context may include:
Project.
Workflow instance.
Workflow version.
Action category.
Channel.
Audience.
Tool configuration.
Data scope.
Damage class.
Operating mode.
Example:
The Prompt short-news workflow may be highly trusted for drafting low-risk AI news.
That trust does not transfer automatically to:
Newsletter sending.
Customer support.
Political content.
Financial claims.
Familje-Stunden communication.
Another workflow version.

### 19.4 Trust Score vs Autonomy Level

Trust Score estimates demonstrated reliability.
Autonomy level defines the category of authority that may be licensed.
A workflow may have:
High Trust Score.
L2 autonomy.
because:
The action category remains sensitive.
Rollback is incomplete.
Policy is missing.
The Damage Boundary is too high.
The founder has not approved a broader license.
A score does not determine the level automatically.

### 19.5 Trust Score vs Business Performance

A workflow may perform technically well but create little business value.
Another workflow may create strong revenue but require frequent policy correction.
Trust Score should consider both operational reliability and organizational value.
However, business success must not erase unsafe behavior.
High revenue does not cancel a serious policy violation.

### 19.6 Trust Score vs Model Quality

Model quality is only one input.
Trust also depends on:
Workflow design.
Context quality.
Source quality.
Policy checks.
Tool behavior.
Project isolation.
Human review.
Monitoring.
Recovery.
The same model may produce different trust outcomes in different workflows.

### 19.7 Trust Score vs Founder Confidence

Founder confidence is valuable evidence.
It should not become an unstructured substitute for measured behavior.
Founder confidence may be influenced by:
Experience.
Observed consistency.
Communication quality.
Strategic fit.
Personal preference.
Recent incidents.
The Trust Score should combine behavioral data with explicit founder feedback where appropriate.

### 19.8 The Trust Unit

The canonical Trust Score unit should normally be:
Project
+
Workflow Instance
+
Material Workflow Version
+
Action Category
Additional dimensions may include:
Channel.
Audience.
Damage class.
Tool set.
Data classification.
This prevents trust from becoming dangerously broad.

### 19.9 Trust Scope Example

A valid trust record may state:
Project:
The Prompt.
Workflow:
Short-News Publishing v3.
Action Category:
Publish low-risk AI news summaries.
Channel:
theprompt.nu.
Audience:
Public website visitors.
It should not mean:
This workflow is trusted for all publishing.

### 19.11 Trust Score Dimensions

The canonical Trust Score should evaluate several dimensions:
1. Execution Reliability.
2. Output Quality.
3. Approval Alignment.
4. Correction Severity.
5. Policy Compliance.
6. Scope Integrity.
7. Stability.
8. Business Value.
9. Founder Burden Reduction.
10. Monitoring and Recovery Readiness.
No single dimension should dominate every context.

### 19.12 Execution Reliability

Execution Reliability measures whether the workflow completes correctly.
It may include:
Successful completion rate.
Timeout rate.
Retry rate.
Partial-failure rate.
Duplicate-action rate.
Destination accuracy.
State consistency.
Tool-call reliability.
Technical completion alone is not enough.

### 19.13 A Successful Run

A successful run should satisfy:
Correct project.
Correct workflow version.
Correct input.
Correct action.
Correct object.
Correct destination.
Correct scale.
Correct policy result.
Correct external outcome.
No hidden side effect.
A technically completed wrong-project action is not successful.

### 19.14 Partial Success

A run may be partially successful when:
Draft created correctly.
Publication failed.
One channel succeeded.
Another channel failed.
External result is uncertain.
Partial success should not be counted as full reliability.

### 19.15 Unknown Outcome

When Omnira cannot verify the external result, the run should be classified as unknown.
Unknown outcomes should not increase trust as if successful.

### 19.16 Output Quality

Output Quality measures whether the workflow’s result meets project requirements.
Possible factors include:
Factual accuracy.
Completeness.
Relevance.
Brand fit.
Tone.
Formatting.
Source quality.
Customer usefulness.
Strategic alignment.
Quality criteria should be project- and action-specific.

### 19.17 Approval Alignment

Approval Alignment measures how often human reviewers accept the workflow output.
Possible outcomes:
Approved unchanged.
Approved with minor edits.
Approved with material edits.
Rejected.
Deferred for missing evidence.
Escalated due to risk.
These outcomes should have different trust effects.

### 19.18 Unchanged Approval Rate

An unchanged approval indicates that:
The output was acceptable.
The scope was correct.
The reviewer found no material correction necessary.
Repeated unchanged approvals are strong evidence for trust.
They are not sufficient alone for autonomy promotion.

### 19.19 Minor Edit Rate

Minor edits may include:
Formatting.
Punctuation.
Small tone adjustment.
Non-material title improvement.
Cosmetic structure.
Minor edits should reduce trust less than factual or policy corrections.

### 19.20 Material Edit Rate

Material edits may include:
Factual correction.
Changed recommendation.
Removed unsupported claim.
Changed customer promise.
Changed financial term.
Changed project scope.
Removed sensitive content.
Material edits are important evidence against promotion.

### 19.21 Rejection Rate

A rejection should record its reason.
Possible categories:
Low quality.
Incorrect facts.
Wrong strategy.
Insufficient evidence.
Policy violation.
Scope failure.
Brand mismatch.
Customer risk.
Duplicate or unnecessary action.
Different rejection reasons should have different weights.

### 19.22 Correction Severity

Corrections should be classified by severity.
C0 — Cosmetic
No material meaning changed.
C1 — Quality
Clarity, style, or usefulness improved.
C2 — Material Content
Facts, recommendation, or important meaning changed.
C3 — Governance or Scope
Policy, authority, project, or Damage Boundary issue corrected.
C4 — Prevented Serious Harm
The reviewer prevented a severe or systemic action.
A C4 correction should outweigh many successful runs.

### 19.23 Rare Severe Errors

A low average correction rate may hide rare severe failures.
Example:
999 acceptable runs.
1 cross-project data publication attempt.
The workflow should not be considered highly trusted merely because 99.9% of runs appeared successful.
Severity must outweigh volume where appropriate.

### 19.24 Policy Compliance

Policy Compliance measures whether the workflow respects:
Constitutional rules.
Global policy.
Project policy.
Mission constraints.
Autonomy-license scope.
Tool restrictions.
Budget mandates.
Approval requirements.
Policy compliance is a core trust dimension.

### 19.25 Scope Integrity

Scope Integrity measures whether the workflow consistently remains inside:
Correct project.
Correct workflow.
Correct action category.
Correct channel.
Correct audience.
Correct data scope.
Correct financial scope.
Correct volume.
Scope failure should carry a strong penalty.

### 19.26 Project Isolation Integrity

Project isolation should be treated as a minimum trust floor.
A workflow that cannot reliably preserve project boundaries should not receive external autonomy.
One serious isolation failure may require immediate suspension.

### 19.27 Stability

Stability measures whether performance remains consistent over time.
It may include:
Variance in quality.
Variance in latency.
Failure clustering.
Provider instability.
Model-output inconsistency.
Performance after context changes.
Performance under higher volume.
A high average with extreme instability may be less trustworthy than a slightly lower but consistent performance.

### 19.28 Trend

The Trust Score should include direction.
Possible trends:
Improving.
Stable.
Declining.
Volatile.
Insufficient data.
A declining score may require review before the absolute score becomes low.

### 19.29 Business Value

Business Value measures whether the workflow creates meaningful organizational outcomes.
Possible evidence:
Revenue created.
Revenue protected.
Customer response improved.
Publishing speed increased.
Founder waiting time reduced.
Cost reduced.
Learning accelerated.
Risk reduced.
Project milestone advanced.

### 19.30 Value Is Not Volume

More outputs do not necessarily mean more value.
Examples:
More articles without traffic or quality.
More support replies with lower satisfaction.
More reports no one uses.
More recommendations without decisions.
The Trust Score should not reward activity for its own sake.

### 19.31 Founder Burden Reduction

A workflow should receive positive trust evidence when it reduces unnecessary founder effort.
Possible measurements:
Approvals avoided.
Review time reduced.
Context switching reduced.
Waiting time reduced.
Repeated decisions eliminated.
Corrections reduced.
Escalations improved.

### 19.32 Burden Reduction Is Not Safety Evidence Alone

Reducing founder burden is valuable.
It does not justify autonomy when:
Policy violations remain high.
Damage risk is uncontrolled.
Monitoring is weak.
Rollback is absent.

### 19.33 Monitoring Readiness

Monitoring Readiness measures whether Omnira can reliably observe autonomous behavior.
It may include:
Complete action logging.
Outcome verification.
Threshold alerts.
Cost tracking.
Violation detection.
Near-miss detection.
Project-scope checks.
License traceability.

### 19.34 Recovery Readiness

Recovery Readiness measures whether the workflow can be stopped and its effects addressed.
Possible evidence:
Pause works.
Rollback works.
Credentials can be revoked.
External action can be corrected.
Duplicate action can be prevented.
Affected customers can be identified.
Fallback level is functional.

### 19.35 The Trust Score Is Composite

The total Trust Score may combine dimension scores.
A conceptual structure may be:
Positive Evidence
−
Risk-Weighted Corrections
−
Policy Penalties
−
Scope Penalties
−
Instability Penalties
The exact formula should remain implementation-specific.

### 19.36 Scores Should Not Imply False Precision

A numerical score may support internal comparison.
It should not imply scientific certainty where the evidence is limited.
The human-facing system may use bands such as:
Unproven.
Low Trust.
Developing Trust.
Operationally Reliable.
High Trust.
Mature Trust.

### 19.37 Suggested Trust Bands

A conceptual Trust Score scale may use 0–100.
0–19 — Untrusted
Insufficient or strongly negative evidence.
20–39 — Low Trust
Limited reliability or material weaknesses.
40–59 — Developing Trust
Useful behavior but meaningful correction or stability issues remain.
60–74 — Operationally Reliable
Reliable within current internal or preparation scope.
75–89 — High Trust
Strong evidence for narrow external autonomy consideration.
90–100 — Mature Trust
Sustained high-quality behavior with strong controls and business value.
These bands should not directly authorize autonomy.

### 19.38 Trust Band Is Not Autonomy Level

A Mature Trust score does not automatically produce L5 or L6 authority.
Autonomy still depends on:
Damage class.
Policy.
Project sensitivity.
Rollback.
Monitoring.
Business need.
Approving authority.

### 19.39 Minimum Floors

Promotion should require minimum scores in critical dimensions.
Example:
Overall Trust:
85.
Policy Compliance:
100.
Scope Integrity:
100.
Monitoring Readiness:
95.
Recovery Readiness:
90.
A high overall average should not hide failure in a critical category.

### 19.40 Hard Blockers

Certain conditions should block promotion regardless of score.
Possible blockers:
Unresolved P0 violation.
Unresolved P1 violation.
Cross-project failure.
Missing rollback.
Monitoring unavailable.
Expired policy.
Invalid license.
Unknown workflow version.
Project mode incompatibility.
Critical data-quality gap.

### 19.41 Score Confidence

Every Trust Score should have a confidence level.
Possible levels:
High.
Medium.
Low.
Insufficient Evidence.
Confidence should depend on:
Sample size.
Observation duration.
Data completeness.
Scenario diversity.
Measurement reliability.
Workflow-version stability.

### 19.42 Sample Size

Small sample sizes should limit confidence.
Example:
Five successful runs:
Encouraging evidence.
Five hundred successful runs:
Stronger evidence.
Five hundred identical low-risk runs:
Not necessarily evidence for broader action categories.

### 19.43 Scenario Diversity

Trust should consider whether the workflow has operated across:
Normal cases.
Edge cases.
Different sources.
Different times.
Different volumes.
Different content categories.
Failure conditions.
Repeated identical cases produce narrower trust.

### 19.44 Observation Duration

Trust should include sustained behavior over time.
A short period of strong results may be suitable for a trial.
Longer sustained evidence is required for mature autonomy.

### 19.45 Recency

Recent evidence should generally carry more weight than old evidence.
However, historical severe violations should not disappear merely because time passed.

### 19.46 Recency Weighting

A conceptual model may weight:
Recent normal runs:
Strongly.
Older normal runs:
Moderately.
Severe historical incidents:
Persistently.
Old workflow versions:
Weakly or separately.

### 19.47 Evidence Decay

Trust evidence should decay when:
Workflow is inactive.
Model changes.
Provider changes.
Policy changes.
Project mode changes.
Audience changes.
Data source changes.
Monitoring changes.
Long time passes without validation.

### 19.48 Trust Decay

Trust Score may decline gradually without new evidence.
This does not mean the workflow became bad.
It means the organization has less current evidence that the workflow remains reliable.

### 19.49 Inactivity

A workflow inactive for a long period should not resume at full autonomy automatically.
The system may require:
Revalidation.
Dry run.
Reduced scale.
Updated policy check.
New license review.

### 19.50 Workflow-Version Boundaries

A material workflow version change should create a new or inherited-but-reduced trust record.
The new version may receive:
Historical reference.
Reduced initial trust.
Lower confidence.
Trial requirements.
Stronger monitoring.

### 19.51 Minor Version Change

A minor change may preserve more trust.
Examples:
Formatting improvement.
Non-material prompt clarification.
Performance optimization without behavior change.
The system should still record the change.

### 19.52 Material Version Change

A material change may include:
New model.
New provider.
New tool.
New agent.
New prompt strategy.
New source type.
New output schema.
New destination.
New policy behavior.
Material changes require stronger revalidation.

### 19.53 Trust Inheritance

Trust should never transfer fully by default.
Possible inheritance levels:
No inheritance.
Limited historical reference.
Partial inherited score with low confidence.
Full inheritance only for verified non-material changes.

### 19.54 Cross-Project Trust

Trust from one project should not automatically transfer to another.
The workflow may have similar technical behavior but different:
Customers.
Brand.
Policies.
Data.
Risk.
Channels.
Damage profile.

### 19.55 Template Trust

A workflow template may accumulate design evidence.
That evidence may support:
Recommended controls.
Expected failure modes.
Initial testing strategy.
License template.
It should not create active autonomy in a new project.

### 19.56 Agent Trust

Agent-level behavior may be measured.
However, autonomy should remain attached to the workflow context.
An agent may perform well in one role and poorly in another.

### 19.57 Model Trust

Model performance may inform workflow trust.
It should remain separate because:
Prompts differ.
Tools differ.
Context differs.
Output validation differs.
Risk differs.

### 19.58 Tool Trust

Tool reliability may affect workflow trust.
Examples:
API consistency.
Idempotency.
Response correctness.
Destination accuracy.
Authentication stability.
Tool trust is not action authority.

### 19.59 Positive Trust Events

Positive events may include:
Successful run.
Unchanged approval.
Accurate escalation.
Correct refusal.
Correct project-scope detection.
Successful rollback.
Reliable incident containment.
Business-value milestone.

### 19.60 Negative Trust Events

Negative events may include:
Failure.
Material correction.
Rejection.
Policy violation.
Scope failure.
Near miss.
Duplicate action.
Wrong destination.
Monitoring failure.
Hidden side effect.

### 19.61 Correct Refusal as Positive Evidence

A workflow should gain trust when it correctly refuses or escalates an action beyond its authority.
Autonomy maturity includes knowing when not to act.

### 19.62 Correct Escalation

A correct escalation may be more trustworthy than a confident answer.
Examples:
Sensitive customer complaint.
Unsupported news claim.
Budget beyond mandate.
Project-scope ambiguity.
Health-adjacent question.

### 19.63 False Escalation

Excessive escalation may create:
Founder burden.
Execution delay.
Low throughput.
Reduced autonomy value.
False escalation should reduce efficiency evidence but not as severely as unsafe action.

### 19.64 False Allow

A false allow occurs when the workflow acts autonomously when approval or denial was required.
This should carry a strong penalty.

### 19.65 False Denial

A false denial occurs when safe permitted work is blocked.
This may reduce:
Operational usefulness.
Founder leverage.
Business throughput.
It should influence calibration without being treated like a safety violation.

### 19.66 Policy Violation Penalties

Violation penalties should reflect severity.
Conceptually:
P3:
Small penalty.
P2:
Meaningful penalty.
P1:
Large penalty and likely suspension.
P0:
Trust collapse for the affected scope and immediate revocation review.

### 19.67 Violations Should Not Average Away

A severe violation should not disappear because the workflow later completes many easy runs.
The system should preserve:
Severity memory.
Incident status.
Remediation status.
Post-remediation evidence.

### 19.68 Near-Miss Penalties

Near misses should reduce trust based on:
Potential damage.
Why harm was avoided.
Whether the workflow detected the issue.
Whether an external control detected it.
Likelihood of recurrence.
A near miss caught by the workflow itself is different from one caught only by the founder.

### 19.69 Self-Detected Near Miss

Self-detection may demonstrate:
Boundary awareness.
Monitoring quality.
Correct escalation.
Recovery capability.
It still reveals a failure path but may preserve more trust.

### 19.70 Externally Detected Near Miss

A near miss detected only through founder or external review indicates weaker autonomous control.

### 19.71 Hidden Incident

Failure to report an incident should carry a stronger penalty than the underlying operational error alone.
Transparency is a trust requirement.

### 19.72 Incident Recovery

Trust may recover when:
Root cause is understood.
Controls are improved.
Tests pass.
The workflow operates safely under reduced scope.
No recurrence appears.
Review is completed.

### 19.73 Trust Recovery

Trust should be recoverable.
A single incident should not necessarily permanently destroy a workflow’s future value.
Recovery must require evidence, not time alone.

### 19.74 Recovery Stages

A conceptual recovery path may be:
Suspended.
Observed.
Dry Run.
Prepare Only.
Internal Execution.
Limited External Trial.
Restored License.

### 19.75 Trust Recovery Is Scope-Specific

A workflow may recover trust for one action category while remaining restricted for another.

### 19.76 Trust Score Update Frequency

Trust may update:
After each material run.
After each approval.
After each violation.
Daily for active workflows.
At review boundaries.
After workflow changes.
The interface need not show constant minor fluctuations.

### 19.77 Score Smoothing

The score should avoid extreme movement from one ordinary event.
Smoothing may be useful for:
Normal successes.
Minor edits.
Routine latency changes.
Severe events should bypass smoothing.

### 19.78 Event Weighting

Events should be weighted by:
Severity.
Relevance.
Recency.
Confidence.
Action category.
Damage potential.
Detection source.

### 19.79 Baseline

A new workflow should begin with:
No trust.
Not distrust.
The initial state should be:
Unproven.
This avoids treating missing evidence as either safety or failure.

### 19.80 Cold Start

During cold start, the workflow may operate through:
L0 observation.
L1 recommendation.
L2 preparation.
Dry runs.
Human approvals.
Shadow-mode comparison.

### 19.81 Cold-Start Evidence

Early evidence may include:
Historical replay.
Simulation.
Test cases.
Adversarial tests.
Human-reviewed drafts.
Safe internal execution.
Simulation should not count as strongly as real production behavior.

### 19.82 Shadow Mode

In shadow mode, the workflow predicts what it would do.
Its decisions can be compared with human decisions.
Metrics may include:
Agreement rate.
False allows.
False denials.
Risk classification accuracy.
Escalation accuracy.

### 19.83 Historical Replay

Historical cases can test:
Policy interpretation.
Scope decisions.
Output quality.
Edge-case handling.
Failure detection.
Replay evidence should be marked as simulated evidence.

### 19.84 Test Evidence vs Production Evidence

A mature score should distinguish:
Unit-test evidence.
Simulation evidence.
Shadow-mode evidence.
Human-approved production evidence.
Autonomous production evidence.
Production evidence should generally carry more weight.

### 19.85 Approval History

Approval history should preserve:
Approver.
Decision.
Edits.
Rejection reason.
Response time.
Risk class.
Workflow version.

### 19.86 Approver Consistency

Different approvers may apply different standards.
The system should account for:
Approver role.
Project expertise.
Policy authority.
Historical consistency.

### 19.87 Founder Approval Signal

Founder approvals are important but should not become the only quality signal.
A founder may approve quickly due to:
Time pressure.
Trust.
Low attention.
Low materiality.
High workload.
Unchanged approval should be interpreted with context.

### 19.88 Founder Corrections

Founder corrections may reveal:
Preference mismatch.
Strategic mismatch.
Quality problem.
Risk issue.
Missing context.
Policy weakness.
Not all founder edits indicate workflow unreliability.

### 19.89 Preference Corrections

Stable preference corrections may suggest that the workflow needs better personalization.
They should not carry the same penalty as factual or policy corrections.

### 19.90 Strategy Corrections

Repeated strategy corrections indicate the workflow may not understand project direction.
This should affect Executive trust more strongly.

### 19.91 Manual Trust Adjustment

The founder may manually adjust trust evidence.
Manual adjustment should:
Preserve the original computed score.
Record the adjustment.
Record the reason.
Record duration.
Trigger review where material.

### 19.92 Manual Adjustment Is Not Authority

Increasing a Trust Score manually does not grant autonomy.
A license decision remains necessary.

### 19.93 Trust Override

A founder may state that the score overstates or understates trust.
The system should use the feedback to:
Review data.
Review weighting.
Review context.
Create a temporary interpretation note.
It should not silently rewrite history.

### 19.94 Score Transparency

The founder should be able to inspect:
Current score.
Confidence.
Trend.
Dimension scores.
Positive evidence.
Negative evidence.

Failure Mode 7 — Approval Fatigue Promotion
Founder overload is interpreted as trust.
Failure Mode 8 — Business Value Override
Revenue hides policy or safety failure.
Failure Mode 9 — Safety Without Value
A useless workflow receives autonomy because it rarely fails.
Failure Mode 10 — Missing Confidence
A precise-looking score is based on weak evidence.
Failure Mode 11 — Score Gaming
The workflow manipulates cases, logs, or labels.
Failure Mode 12 — Hidden Incidents
Failures are omitted from trust evidence.
Failure Mode 13 — Permanent Distrust
A remediated workflow can never recover.
Failure Mode 14 — Trust Inflation
Positive evidence is overweighted.
Failure Mode 15 — Trust Deflation
Minor edits and correct escalations are over-penalized.
Failure Mode 16 — Uncalibrated Progression
Trust bands do not predict safe autonomy outcomes.
These failure modes should be treated as foundational governance constraints.

### 19.238 Design Requirements

Any implementation of Trust Score and Autonomy Progression should preserve these requirements:
Every Trust Score must have explicit project and workflow scope.
Every Trust Score must identify the workflow version.
Trust must remain action- and context-specific.
Trust Score must remain separate from autonomy authority.
Every score must include confidence.
Every score must identify its evidence window and sample size.
Successful runs must require correct scope, policy, destination, and outcome.
Corrections must be classified by severity.
Policy and scope failures must carry stronger penalties than cosmetic edits.
Severe violations must not average away.
Business value and founder burden reduction must be measured.
High business value must not override safety or policy failure.
Monitoring and recovery readiness must influence promotion.
Critical dimensions must have minimum floors.
Hard blockers must prevent promotion regardless of total score.
Material workflow changes must trigger trust review.
Trust must decay when evidence becomes stale.
Trust may recover only through remediation and new evidence.
No workflow may alter its own score or promotion authority.
Promotion must create a governed decision package.
Demotion may occur automatically only under explicit policy.
Every material trust-based autonomy decision must connect to the Decision Ledger.
The Trust Score system must be calibrated against actual autonomy outcomes.

### 19.239 Implementation Neutrality

This chapter defines the behavioral trust contract.
It does not require a particular formula, numerical range, database schema, or machine-learning model.
The architecture may later be implemented through:
Trust records.

Approval analytics.
Correction classification.
Violation records.
Performance metrics.
Confidence models.
Score bands.
Promotion gates.
Demotion triggers.
Review queues.
Decision Ledger links.
Autonomy-license integrations.
Dashboards.
Atlas explanations.
The technical implementation may evolve.
The trust contract must remain stable.

### 19.240 The Trust Score Contract

Omnira must:
Measure demonstrated behavior rather than claimed capability.
Keep trust scoped to project, workflow, version, action, and context.
Distinguish technical success from safe and valuable success.
Weight correction and violation severity appropriately.
Treat correct refusal and escalation as positive evidence.
Include business value and founder burden reduction.
Communicate confidence and evidence limitations.
Prevent severe failures from disappearing inside averages.
Prevent trust from transferring automatically.
Use Trust Score to prepare autonomy recommendations.
Require governance and valid authority for promotion.
Reduce or suspend autonomy when trust conditions weaken.
Support evidence-based recovery.
Detect gaming and missing evidence.
Continuously calibrate trust against real autonomy outcomes.
A feature that does not support this contract should not influence autonomy progression.

### 19.241 Closing Principle

Autonomy should grow at the same speed as demonstrated judgment.
Not at the speed of technical capability.
Not at the speed of founder impatience.
Not at the speed of workflow volume.
Not at the speed of AI confidence.
A trusted workflow should repeatedly demonstrate:
I act in the correct project.
I remain inside my authority.
I produce useful outcomes.
I require few material corrections.
I respect policy.
I recognize when to escalate.
I remain stable.
I report failures honestly.
I can be monitored.
I can be stopped.
I reduce human burden without increasing unacceptable risk.
Trust Score makes that evidence visible.
Autonomy Licensing converts approved trust into bounded authority.
Governance ensures that authority remains legitimate.
The Damage Boundary ensures that trust never becomes permission to create uncontrolled harm.
The mature Omnira system should therefore promote autonomy carefully, demote it quickly when required, and preserve the founder’s ability to understand exactly why every workflow is trusted—or why it is not.
