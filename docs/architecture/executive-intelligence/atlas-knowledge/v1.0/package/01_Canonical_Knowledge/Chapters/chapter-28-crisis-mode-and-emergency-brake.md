## Canonical Text

### 28.1 Purpose of This Chapter

Omnira must be capable of operating autonomously.
It must also be capable of stopping itself.
Normal governance assumes that:
Systems are functioning.
Policies are available.
Authority can be verified.
Execution state is known.
Risks remain within expected bounds.
Monitoring is reliable.
Escalation paths are available.
A crisis exists when one or more of these assumptions fail in a way that may cause material harm.
Examples include:
Uncontrolled external publishing.
Unauthorized spending.
Cross-project data leakage.
Credential compromise.
Customer harm.
Serious production failure.
Systemic policy violation.
Unknown external execution state.
Corrupted decision or approval state.
Failure of critical governance controls.
Omnira therefore requires:
Crisis detection.
Crisis classification.
Emergency containment.
The Emergency Brake.
Evidence preservation.
Authority escalation.
Recovery planning.
Controlled restoration.
Post-crisis learning.
This chapter defines the architecture for those capabilities.

### 28.2 The Core Thesis

Normal autonomy depends on reliable governance.
When governance or execution becomes unreliable, autonomy must contract.
The foundational principle is:
When Omnira can no longer prove that continued execution is safe and authorized, it must stop the affected execution before attempting to optimize it.

### 28.3 Crisis Mode

Crisis Mode is a temporary operating state in which:
Safety takes priority over throughput.
Containment takes priority over optimization.
Current evidence takes priority over historical assumptions.
Authority becomes narrower.
External actions are restricted.
Spend may be frozen.
Monitoring becomes more intensive.
Human escalation becomes faster.
Recovery requires explicit validation.

### 28.4 Emergency Brake

The Emergency Brake is the technical and governance mechanism that rapidly restricts or stops consequential execution.
It may:
Pause workflows.
Block new external actions.
Freeze spend.
Suspend autonomy licenses.
Disable tool writes.
Revoke temporary credentials.
Stop scheduled execution.
Require human approval.
Activate incident workflows.

### 28.5 Crisis Mode vs Emergency Brake

Crisis Mode is the organizational state.
The Emergency Brake is one of the mechanisms used inside that state.
Example:
Crisis Mode:
The Prompt public-publishing incident.
Emergency Brake:
Stop all The Prompt publishing workflows and disable external publication tools.

### 28.6 Crisis Mode vs Workflow Pause

A workflow pause may address one operational issue.
Crisis Mode is broader and may change:
Authority.
Monitoring.
Escalation.
Communication.
Budget behavior.
Recovery requirements.

### 28.7 Crisis Mode vs Project Pause

A Project Pause stops or restricts project execution.
Crisis Mode additionally activates:
Incident command.
Evidence preservation.
Damage assessment.
Governance review.
Recovery control.

### 28.8 Crisis Mode vs Lifecycle Mode

Crisis is an operating mode.
It does not automatically mean the project lifecycle becomes:
Hibernate.
Archived.
Retired.
A project may temporarily enter Crisis Mode while remaining lifecycle-active.

### 28.9 Crisis Mode vs Policy Violation

A policy violation may be minor and contained.
A crisis exists when the violation or its consequences create material, severe, systemic, or rapidly escalating harm.

### 28.10 Crisis Mode vs Incident

An incident is an event requiring response.
A crisis is an incident whose:
Severity.
Blast radius.
Uncertainty.
Velocity.
Governance impact.
Customer impact.
requires emergency organizational control.

### 28.11 Crisis Mode Is Not Punishment

Crisis Mode should not be used to punish:
Agents.
Workflows.
Managers.
Human operators.
Projects.
Its purpose is containment, recovery, and learning.

### 28.12 Crisis Mode Is Not Failure by Definition

Correctly entering Crisis Mode may demonstrate mature governance.
The greater failure may be continuing unsafe execution.

An Autonomy Crisis may occur when a licensed workflow:

Uses the wrong project.
Repeatedly causes harm.

Continues after suspension.

Unexpected data-retention change.

### 28.24 Portfolio Crisis

Shared credential compromise.
Shared governance failure.
Shared AI-provider failure.
Financial exposure.
Systemic data incident.

### 28.25 Founder-Capacity Crisis

A Founder-Capacity Crisis may occur when:
Critical decisions accumulate.
The founder becomes unavailable during material risk.
Approval queues prevent containment.
Crisis load becomes unsustainable.
Several projects require immediate human authority.
This should trigger prioritization and delegation safeguards, not surveillance.

### 28.26 Systemic Intelligence Crisis

This may occur when Executive or AI Intelligence:
Uses corrupted knowledge.
Retrieves wrong-project context.
Produces widespread unsafe recommendations.
Cannot distinguish authority.
Generates false decision state.
Loses reliable evidence access.
Part II — Crisis Severity

### 28.27 Crisis Severity Model

A conceptual crisis-severity model may include:
C0 — Operational Disturbance.
C1 — Contained Incident.
C2 — Material Crisis.
C3 — Severe Crisis.
C4 — Systemic Emergency.
This is separate from:
Damage severity.
Policy-violation severity.
Mission priority.
Operational alert priority.

### 28.28 C0 — Operational Disturbance

Characteristics:
Low impact.
Known cause.
Narrow scope.
Reliable monitoring.
Easy recovery.
No material customer or external harm.
Response:
Manager handles.
Routine incident record.
No formal Crisis Mode required.

### 28.29 C1 — Contained Incident

Characteristics:
Limited impact.
Known affected workflow.
Contained blast radius.
Recovery available.
No ongoing severe harm.
Response may include:
Workflow pause.
Project Executive notification.
Targeted approval.
Short incident review.

### 28.30 C2 — Material Crisis

Characteristics:
Material customer, financial, brand, data, or production impact.
Risk of continued harm.
Multiple workflows may be affected.
Executive intervention required.
Response:
Project Crisis Mode.
Emergency Brake for affected scope.
Founder notification.
Formal incident command.

### 28.31 C3 — Severe Crisis

Characteristics:
Severe damage.
Rapid escalation.
Large external exposure.
Uncertain system state.
Critical governance failure.
Several project systems affected.
Response:
Broad project shutdown.
Portfolio Executive involvement.
Freeze external actions.
Freeze material spend.
Specialist escalation.
Frequent crisis reporting.

### 28.32 C4 — Systemic Emergency

Characteristics:
Several projects affected.
Core governance compromised.
Tenant or project isolation uncertain.
Shared infrastructure unsafe.
Organizational authority cannot be trusted.
Severe ongoing damage possible.
Response:
Portfolio Emergency Brake.
Default-deny external execution.
Revoke broad autonomy.
Restrict all write tools.
Human-controlled recovery.
Independent validation before restoration.

### 28.33 Severity Factors

Crisis severity should consider:
Impact.
Likelihood of further harm.
Velocity.
Blast radius.
Detectability.
Reversibility.
Containment.
Customer vulnerability.
Authority failure.
Data sensitivity.
Financial exposure.
Public exposure.
Systemic dependency.

### 28.34 Impact

Impact asks:
What harm has already occurred?
What assets are affected?
What business value is at risk?

### 28.35 Velocity

Velocity asks:
How quickly can damage grow?
Can another automated action occur within seconds?
Is a scheduled batch imminent?

### 28.36 Blast Radius

Blast radius may include:
One task.
One workflow.
One project.
Several projects.
All external channels.
All customer tenants.

### 28.37 Detectability

Low detectability increases crisis severity.
A workflow producing unknown external effects is more dangerous than one with fully observed state.

### 28.38 Reversibility

Irreversible actions increase severity.
Examples:
Public misinformation.
Permanent deletion.
Customer financial harm.
Sensitive data disclosure.
Legal commitment.

### 28.39 Governance Failure

If authority, policy, or approval state cannot be trusted, the crisis may require broader containment than the visible incident alone.

### 28.40 Residual Risk

Even after containment, residual risk may remain through:
Cached jobs.
Pending queues.
External scheduled posts.
Long-lived credentials.
Third-party automation.
Customer effects.
Search-engine indexing.

### 28.48 Detection Confidence

Uncertain.

### 28.49 Low-Confidence High-Impact Signal

A low-confidence signal may still justify a narrow preventive pause when the worst credible outcome is severe.

### 28.50 False Positive

A false positive is less costly when containment is narrow and reversible.
The system should prefer:
Narrow pause.
Rapid validation.
Controlled restart.
over broad unnecessary shutdown.

### 28.51 False Negative

A false negative may permit continuing harm.
Detection thresholds should be stricter for:
Security.
Customer safety.
Sensitive data.
Spend.
Public publication.
Project isolation.

### 28.52 Detection Delay

The system should measure:
Time from first harmful event.
Time to first signal.
Time to confirmed detection.
Time to containment.

### 28.53 Detection Integrity

A compromised workflow should not be the only system responsible for reporting its own failures.

### 28.54 Independent Monitoring

High-risk workflows should have independent monitoring where possible.

### 28.55 Monitoring Failure

Monitoring failure may itself trigger restriction.
A high-impact workflow should not continue autonomously when its required monitoring disappears.

Part V — The Emergency Brake

### 28.71 Emergency Brake Objectives

The Emergency Brake should:
Stop new harm.
Prevent repeated action.
Restrict uncertain authority.
Preserve evidence.
Maintain recoverability.
Allow safe internal analysis.
Enable controlled restoration.

### 28.72 Emergency Brake Scope

Possible scopes:
Action.
Task.
Workflow.
Mission.
Tool.
Channel.
Credential.
Project.
Provider.
Portfolio.
All external execution.

### 28.73 Narrowest Effective Scope

The system should use the narrowest scope that reliably contains the threat.

### 28.74 Action-Level Brake

Stops one action before execution.

### 28.75 Workflow-Level Brake

Stops one workflow and its scheduled runs.

### 28.76 Mission-Level Brake

Stops all execution belonging to one mission.

### 28.77 Tool-Level Brake

Blocks defined tool operations.
Example:
Publishing tool remains readable but all write operations are disabled.

### 28.78 Channel-Level Brake

Stops activity on:
Website.
Email.
Social platform.
Payment system.
Customer support channel.

### 28.79 Credential-Level Brake

Revokes or suspends a credential.

### 28.80 Project-Level Brake

Stops consequential project execution while preserving project-isolated analysis.

### 28.81 Provider-Level Brake

Removes one provider from eligible execution routes.

### 28.82 Portfolio-Level Brake

Restricts several or all projects.
This should be reserved for systemic exposure.

### 28.83 External-Action Freeze

An External-Action Freeze may block:
Publishing.
Sending communication.
Spending.
Deploying.
Changing customer state.
Writing to third-party systems.

### 28.84 Spend Freeze

A Spend Freeze may block:
New purchases.
Campaign spend.
Provider upgrades.
Automated refunds above policy.
New recurring commitments.
Existing unavoidable infrastructure charges may continue unless specifically stopped.

### 28.85 Write Freeze

A Write Freeze blocks defined state changes while permitting read-only investigation.

### 28.86 Approval Freeze

Approval Freeze may invalidate pending approvals when:
The object is compromised.
The evidence is stale.
The context changed.
The approval system is untrusted.

### 28.87 License Suspension

Affected autonomy licenses should move to:
Restricted.
Suspended.
Revoked.
depending on the crisis.

### 28.88 Schedule Suspension

Scheduled jobs should be stopped or held safely.

### 28.89 Queue Containment

Pending actions should be:
Frozen.
Identified.
Deduplicated.
Inspected.
Released only after validation.

### 28.90 Retry Disablement

Automatic retries should stop when external state is uncertain.

### 28.91 Safe Internal Continuation

Even during Crisis Mode, the system may permit:
Read-only analysis.
Evidence collection.
Drafting.
Simulation.
Testing in isolated environments.
Recovery planning.
Internal communication.

### 28.92 Emergency Brake Idempotency

Activating the brake repeatedly should not create inconsistent state.

### 28.93 Emergency Brake Confirmation

The system should verify that:
New actions are blocked.
Schedules are stopped.
Permissions are restricted.
Queues are frozen.
External channels are contained.

### 28.94 Brake Failure

If the Emergency Brake fails, the system should escalate to:
Credential revocation.
Provider-side disablement.
Infrastructure shutdown.
Manual intervention.
Portfolio emergency.

### 28.95 Brake Coverage

The organization should know which systems are:
Brake-controlled.
Partially controlled.
Externally controlled.
Not controllable.

### 28.96 External-System Limitation

Some external actions cannot be recalled.
The brake may stop future actions but not reverse completed effects.

### 28.310 Anti-Local-Only Principle

Systemic exposure should not be treated as a local workflow issue.

### 28.311 Anti-Availability-Equals-Safety Principle

A technically running service may still be unsafe.

### 28.312 Anti-Recovery-Equals-Restart Principle

Recovery requires restored control and validated state.

### 28.313 Anti-Root-Cause-Theatre Principle

A superficial explanation must not close the incident.

### 28.314 Anti-Learning-Theatre Principle

Lessons without corrective missions are insufficient.

### 28.315 Anti-Founder-Overload Principle

The crisis process should compress information and focus founder attention.

### 28.316 Anti-Customer-Opacity Principle

Affected customers should not be kept uninformed when notification is necessary.
Part XXIII — Tests

### 28.317 The Crisis Test

Omnira should ask:
Is material harm occurring or plausible?
Is normal governance sufficient?
Is system state known?
Is continued execution safe?

### 28.318 The Severity Test

Omnira should ask:
What is the impact?
How quickly can it grow?
What is the blast radius?
How reversible is it?

### 28.319 The Scope Test

Omnira should ask:
Which action, workflow, project, provider, or portfolio scope is affected?

### 28.320 The Brake Test

Omnira should ask:
What is the narrowest control that reliably stops further harm?
Can it be verified?

### 28.321 The Authority Test

Omnira should ask:
Who may activate the restriction?
Who may approve recovery?
Does temporary authority exist?

### 28.322 The Evidence Test

Omnira should ask:
Which evidence must be preserved immediately?
Could affected systems alter it?

### 28.323 The Customer Test

Omnira should ask:
Which customers are affected?
Is harm ongoing?
Is direct communication required?

### 28.324 The Financial Test

Omnira should ask:
Is money still moving?
Can spend or billing be frozen?
What exposure exists?

### 28.325 The Data Test

Omnira should ask:
Was sensitive data accessed, changed, exported, or exposed?
Which projects or tenants are affected?

### 28.326 The Communication Test

Omnira should ask:
Who must know?
What can be stated confidently?
Who may communicate externally?

### 28.327 The Containment Test

Omnira should ask:
Has continued harm actually stopped?
Which paths remain active?

### 28.328 The Investigation Test

Omnira should ask:
What is known?
What remains unknown?
Which hypothesis best fits the evidence?

### 28.329 The Recovery Test

Omnira should ask:
Is the target state safe?
Are controls restored?
Can the fix be rolled back?

### 28.330 The Restart Test

Omnira should ask:
Is external state known?
Could actions duplicate?
Are approvals and licenses valid?
Is monitoring active?

### 28.331 The Exit Test

Omnira should ask:
Has damage stopped?
Is residual risk understood?
Who accepts that risk?
Which restrictions remain?

### 28.332 The Learning Test

Omnira should ask:
What should change in policy, architecture, monitoring, missions, tools, or autonomy?

### 28.333 The Founder-Burden Test

Omnira should ask:
Is the founder receiving decision-ready summaries?
Can operational detail remain delegated?
Part XXIV — Failure Modes

### 28.334 Failure Mode 1 — Late Crisis Activation

The system continues harmful execution while waiting for perfect evidence.

### 28.335 Failure Mode 2 — Over-Broad Shutdown

A local incident unnecessarily stops the entire organization.

### 28.336 Failure Mode 3 — Under-Containment

One path is paused while another harmful path remains active.

### 28.337 Failure Mode 4 — Blind Restart

Paused jobs resume without state reconciliation.

### 28.338 Failure Mode 5 — Evidence Loss

Logs, state, or external evidence disappear before investigation.

### 28.339 Failure Mode 6 — Unclear Crisis Authority

No one knows who may stop or restore execution.

### 28.340 Failure Mode 7 — Crisis Self-Promotion

A system expands its permanent authority through emergency action.

### 28.341 Failure Mode 8 — Permanent Emergency Exception

Temporary authority becomes normal operation.

### 28.342 Failure Mode 9 — Governance Bypass

Urgency is used to ignore policy and approval without formal exception.

### 28.343 Failure Mode 10 — Customer Harm Hidden

The organization focuses on technical recovery while customers remain affected.

### 28.344 Failure Mode 11 — Root-Cause Theatre

The review names a superficial cause and closes early.

### 28.345 Failure Mode 12 — Recovery Without Validation

Systems return online because the immediate symptom disappeared.

### 28.346 Failure Mode 13 — Crisis Notification Noise

Repeated low-value alerts overload the founder.

### 28.347 Failure Mode 14 — Crisis Data Overreach

Emergency access becomes unnecessarily broad.

### 28.348 Failure Mode 15 — Single-Point Crisis Control

The brake depends on the compromised system or one unavailable person.

### 28.349 Failure Mode 16 — No Corrective Follow-Through

The crisis closes but architecture, policy, and workflow weaknesses remain.
These failure modes should be treated as foundational safety and governance constraints.
Part XXV — Design Requirements

### 28.350 Design Requirements

Any implementation of Crisis Mode and the Emergency Brake should preserve these requirements:
Omnira must distinguish operational disturbance, incident, crisis, and systemic emergency.
Crisis severity must consider impact, velocity, blast radius, reversibility, detectability, and governance failure.
Crisis Mode must be activatable by authorized humans and narrowly defined automated controls.
The Emergency Brake must support action-, workflow-, mission-, tool-, channel-, credential-, project-, provider-, and portfolio-level restriction.
The narrowest effective containment scope should be preferred.
High-impact workflows must stop when required monitoring or governance becomes unavailable.
Crisis activation must create a traceable declaration and receipt.
Affected autonomy licenses must be restrictable, suspendable, and revocable.
External actions, spend, writes, schedules, and retries must be independently controllable.
Emergency controls must preserve evidence.
Crisis response must identify one accountable owner and a clear command structure.
Every crisis must use a bounded Crisis Mission Brief.
Founder communication must remain compressed and decision-ready.
Customer, financial, data, legal, security, and brand consequences must be assessed explicitly.
Containment must be verified rather than assumed.
Recovery must restore governance and observability before full execution.
Queues, approvals, credentials, and external state must be reconciled before restart.
No paused workflow may restart blindly.
Autonomy licenses must not reactivate automatically after a material crisis.
Emergency authority and exceptions must be narrow, time-limited, audited, and automatically expiring.
Project and tenant isolation must remain enforced during crisis.
Crisis controls must not expose unnecessary founder or customer data.
Material crisis decisions must link to the Decision Ledger.
The Operating Graph and Intelligence Graph must represent crisis scope, authority, containment, recovery, and learning.
Post-crisis review must produce assigned corrective missions.
Crisis Mode must reduce harm without becoming a permanent operating state.
The Emergency Brake must be tested before it is needed.

### 28.351 Implementation Neutrality

It does not require a specific incident platform, queue system, workflow engine, infrastructure provider, monitoring system, or interface.
The architecture may later be implemented through:

Emergency Brake controls.
Workflow suspension.
Tool-write restrictions.
Credential revocation.
Spend freezes.
Queue containment.
Crisis Mission Briefs.
Evidence snapshots.
Crisis dashboards.
Crisis graphs.
Approval escalation.
Recovery gates.
Post-crisis reviews.
Decision Ledger links.
The technical implementation may evolve.
The crisis contract must remain stable.

### 28.352 The Crisis Mode & Emergency Brake Contract

Omnira must:
Detect material harm and governance failure early.
Differentiate local incidents from systemic crises.
Activate the narrowest effective containment.
Stop unsafe external actions, spending, writes, schedules, and retries.
Preserve evidence before investigation alters state.
Assign clear crisis ownership and authority.
Protect customers, data, money, brand, and project isolation.
Provide the founder with concise, decision-ready crisis information.
Allow safe internal analysis while consequential execution is frozen.
Use temporary emergency authority only within explicit limits.
Verify containment.
Investigate root and systemic causes.
Restore observability and governance before normal operation.
Reconcile external state before retry or restart.
Restore workflows gradually.
Require explicit review before restoring affected autonomy.
Communicate honestly with affected people.
Record material crisis decisions and outcomes.
Convert lessons into corrective missions, policy, architecture, monitoring, and autonomy changes.
Test the Emergency Brake before a real emergency.
Remain capable of stopping itself without losing the ability to recover.
A feature that does not support this contract should not govern Omnira’s crisis response or emergency control.

### 28.353 Closing Principle

A mature autonomous organization is not defined only by how much it can do without human intervention.
It is also defined by how safely it can stop.
Omnira should be able to say:
Something is wrong.
The current state is uncertain.
Continued execution may cause harm.
I have stopped the affected actions.
I have preserved the evidence.
I have protected unaffected projects.
I know which decision is required.
I will not restart until safety, authority, and state are verified.
The Emergency Brake is not the opposite of autonomy.
It is one of the conditions that makes autonomy credible.
Without containment, autonomy becomes exposure.
Without evidence, recovery becomes guesswork.
Without authority, emergency action becomes another governance failure.
Without controlled restart, containment only delays the next incident.
The mature Omnira organization should therefore be capable of moving quickly when healthy, slowing down when uncertain, stopping when unsafe, and recovering only when it can prove that execution is once again governed, observable, and contained.
The strongest autonomous system is not the system that never stops.
It is the system that knows exactly when it must stop—and knows how to return safely.
