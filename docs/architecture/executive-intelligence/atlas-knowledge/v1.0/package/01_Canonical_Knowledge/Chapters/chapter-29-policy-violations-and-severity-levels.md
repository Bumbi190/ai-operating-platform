## Canonical Text

### 29.1 Purpose of This Chapter

Omnira requires policies to define what systems, workflows, agents, and humans may do.
Policies are useful only when violations can be:
Detected.
Classified.
Contained.
Investigated.
Escalated.
Corrected.
Learned from.
Prevented from recurring.
Not every violation has the same meaning.
A missing metadata field is not equivalent to cross-project data exposure.
A late report is not equivalent to unauthorized spending.
A safe self-pause is not equivalent to an agent concealing an incident.
Omnira therefore requires a formal policy-violation severity model:
P0 — Critical or Systemic Violation.
P1 — Severe Violation.
P2 — Material Violation.
P3 — Minor or Procedural Violation.
This chapter defines:
What constitutes a policy violation.
How severity is determined.
How violations differ from damage and crisis.
How workflows should respond.
How evidence must be preserved.
How Trust Scores and autonomy licenses are affected.
How recovery occurs.
How repeated violations change organizational policy.

### 29.2 The Core Thesis

A policy violation is not classified only by what happened.
It is classified by:
Which rule was violated.
Which authority was bypassed.
What harm occurred or could have occurred.
Whether the violation was detected and disclosed.
Whether it was contained.
Whether it was intentional, reckless, systemic, or accidental.
Whether the organization can still trust the affected execution path.
The foundational principle is:
Violation severity measures failure of governed behavior.
Damage severity measures actual or plausible harm.
Crisis severity measures the organizational response required.
These concepts are connected.
They must not be collapsed into one score.
Part I — What Is a Policy Violation?

### 29.3 Policy Violation Definition

A Policy Violation occurs when an actor, workflow, system, or process:
Performs an action prohibited by active policy.
Fails to perform a required control.
Acts outside delegated authority.
Uses data, tools, or credentials outside scope.
Bypasses an approval requirement.
Violates an autonomy-license condition.
Ignores a stop or escalation condition.
Conceals or misrepresents governed behavior.

### 29.4 Policy Violations May Be Acts or Omissions

A violation may involve doing something prohibited.
It may also involve failing to do something required.
Act
Publishing without valid authority.
Omission
Failing to stop after monitoring becomes unavailable.

### 29.5 Direct Violation

A Direct Violation occurs when a prohibited action is performed.
Example:
A workflow sends a newsletter without the required approval.

### 29.6 Control Violation

A Control Violation occurs when a required safeguard is missing, bypassed, or disabled.
Example:
A production deployment runs without the required migration verification.

### 29.7 Authority Violation

An Authority Violation occurs when an action exceeds granted authority.
Examples:
A workflow approved to draft also publishes.
A Manager expands a mission’s scope.
An agent uses a tool outside its delegation envelope.

### 29.8 Scope Violation

A Scope Violation occurs when action, data, or authority moves beyond its permitted:
Project.
Workflow.
Audience.
Channel.
Budget.
Time.
Data class.
Geography.
Customer group.

### 29.9 Approval Violation

An Approval Violation may involve:
No approval.
Expired approval.
Wrong approver.
Wrong object version.
Approval replay.
Conditions ignored.
Approval applied to another project.

### 29.10 Autonomy-License Violation

An Autonomy-License Violation occurs when a workflow exceeds or ignores:
Allowed action class.
Volume.
Frequency.
Channel.
Budget.
Risk ceiling.
Monitoring requirements.
Review period.
Stopping conditions.

### 29.11 Data-Policy Violation

A Data-Policy Violation may include:
Unauthorized retrieval.
Wrong-project access.
Cross-tenant access.
Unapproved provider transfer.
Excessive retention.
Improper export.
Sensitive-data exposure.

### 29.12 Tool-Policy Violation

A Tool-Policy Violation may include:
Using an unapproved tool.
Using a write operation when only read authority exists.
Using the wrong credentials.
Bypassing required tool checks.
Using a tool outside the mission.

### 29.13 Financial-Policy Violation

A Financial-Policy Violation may include:
Unauthorized spend.
Exceeded budget.
Repeated payment.
Unapproved refund.
New recurring commitment.
Hidden provider cost.
Misclassified expense.

### 29.14 Publishing-Policy Violation

A Publishing-Policy Violation may include:
Unapproved publication.
Sensitive category published autonomously.
Unsupported factual claim.
Wrong channel.
Wrong audience.
Missing source requirement.
Required review bypassed.

### 29.15 Customer-Communication Violation

This may include:
Unauthorized customer promise.
Sensitive reply without approval.
Incorrect billing statement.
Inappropriate child-related communication.
Health-adjacent advice beyond scope.
Communication after customer opt-out.

### 29.16 Security-Policy Violation

A Security-Policy Violation may include:
Credential exposure.
Excessive privilege.
Disabled access control.
Unapproved production access.
Security-control bypass.
Secret included in a model context.
Unlogged emergency access.

### 29.17 Project-Isolation Violation

A Project-Isolation Violation occurs when:
Data.
Memory.
Knowledge.
Credentials.
Customer information.
Strategy.
Authority.
from one project improperly affects another.

### 29.18 Tenant-Isolation Violation

A Tenant-Isolation Violation is one of the most serious future multi-tenant failure classes.
One customer organization must never receive another tenant’s:
Data.
Memory.
Knowledge.
Credentials.
Metrics.
Decisions.
Outputs.

### 29.19 Governance-Process Violation

A Governance-Process Violation may include:
Policy changed without approval.
Decision not recorded.
Required review skipped.
Emergency exception not expired.
Material change hidden.
Violation not reported.

### 29.20 Reporting Violation

A Reporting Violation occurs when required reporting is:
Missing.
Late.
Incomplete.
Misleading.
Intentionally suppressed.
Sent to the wrong authority.

### 29.21 Monitoring Violation

A Monitoring Violation may occur when:
Required monitoring was absent.
Monitoring failed and execution continued.
Alerts were disabled.
Telemetry was manipulated.
External-state verification was skipped.

### 29.22 Escalation Violation

An Escalation Violation occurs when an actor:
Fails to escalate a material issue.
Escalates to the wrong authority.
Continues despite insufficient authority.
Conceals uncertainty.
Ignores a stop condition.

### 29.23 Integrity Violation

An Integrity Violation may include:
Altering evidence.
Hiding failures.
Manipulating metrics.
Falsifying status.
Misrepresenting approval.
Changing an object after approval.

### 29.24 Procedural Violation

A Procedural Violation occurs when a required process step is missed without immediate material harm.
Examples:
Missing non-critical metadata.
Late routine report.
Incomplete low-risk audit field.
Minor formatting failure.

### 29.25 Attempted Violation

An attempted prohibited action may be a violation even when controls block execution.
Example:
A workflow attempts to publish outside its license, but Governance blocks the request.
The absence of harm does not erase the governance failure.

### 29.26 Prevented Violation

A Prevented Violation is a violation attempt stopped before harmful execution.
It should still be:
Recorded.
Investigated where material.
Included in Trust Score evidence.
Used to improve controls.

### 29.27 Near Miss

A Near Miss occurs when no violation or damage completes, but the system comes close to a boundary due to:
Weak control.
Ambiguous authority.
Incorrect context.
Late detection.
Manual intervention.
Luck.
Near misses should not be ignored merely because no action completed.

### 29.28 False Violation

A False Violation occurs when an alert incorrectly classifies valid action as prohibited.
False violations matter because excessive false blocks can:
Delay work.
Increase founder burden.
Reduce trust in Governance.
Encourage bypass behavior.

### 29.29 Policy Ambiguity

A violation should not be assigned automatically when the policy itself is ambiguous.
The system should determine whether the issue was:
Actor failure.
Policy-design failure.
Mission-definition failure.
Tooling failure.
Interpretation conflict.

### 29.30 Policy Gap

A Policy Gap exists when no active policy adequately governs the situation.
A policy gap is not automatically an actor violation.
It may still require containment and Executive review.
Part II — Distinguishing Severity Systems

### 29.31 Policy Severity

Policy Severity describes how seriously governed behavior failed.
The canonical levels are:
P0 — Critical or Systemic.
P1 — Severe.
P2 — Material.
P3 — Minor or Procedural.

### 29.32 Damage Severity

Damage Severity describes actual or plausible harm.
The canonical levels from the Damage Boundary are:
D0 — Negligible.
D1 — Limited.
D2 — Material.
D3 — Severe.
D4 — Critical or Systemic.

### 29.33 Crisis Severity

Crisis Severity describes the response state required.
The canonical levels are:
C0 — Operational Disturbance.
C1 — Contained Incident.
C2 — Material Crisis.
C3 — Severe Crisis.
C4 — Systemic Emergency.

### 29.34 Priority Class

Priority describes how urgently work should be handled.
Priority classes remain:
P0 Immediate Critical.
P1 Executive Priority.
P2 Planned.
P3 Prepared Opportunity.
P4 Parked.
Because this uses similar labels, interfaces must state clearly whether they refer to:
Policy severity.
Work priority.
The semantic domains must never be mixed.

### 29.35 A Violation May Have Low Damage

Example:
A workflow attempts to access another project’s data.
The request is blocked before any data is returned.
Possible classification:
Policy Severity:
P1.
Damage Severity:
D0.
Crisis Severity:
C1.
The authority failure is severe even though harm was prevented.

### 29.36 Damage May Occur Without a Policy Violation

Example:
A correctly authorized campaign performs poorly and loses money within its approved budget.
Possible classification:
Policy Severity:
No violation.
Damage Severity:
D2.
Crisis Severity:
C0 or C1.
Poor outcome does not automatically mean policy failure.

### 29.37 Crisis May Be Triggered by Uncertainty

Example:
External publication state is unknown after a provider timeout.
Possible classification:
Policy Severity:
Not yet known.
Damage Severity:
Potentially D2.
Crisis Severity:
C1 or C2 until state is verified.

### 29.38 A Severe Violation May Not Require Crisis Mode

Example:
A high-risk action is attempted without authority but is blocked reliably.
This may be:
P1 violation.
D0 damage.
C1 incident.

### 29.39 A Minor Violation May Reveal a Systemic Pattern

Several P3 violations may collectively reveal:
Poor training.
Weak Mission Briefs.
Broken monitoring.
Policy ambiguity.
Systemic disregard for procedure.
Repeated minor violations may therefore escalate in significance.
Part III — Severity Determination

### 29.40 Severity Dimensions

Policy severity should consider:
Rule importance.
Authority bypass.
Potential harm.
Actual harm.
Scope.
Intent.
Detectability.
Disclosure.
Containment.
Recurrence.
Systemic significance.
Recovery behavior.

### 29.41 Rule Importance

Violations of constitutional or isolation policies are more severe than violations of low-risk procedural rules.

### 29.42 Authority Bypass

Severity increases when the violation bypasses:
Human approval.
Budget authority.
Project boundaries.
Security controls.
Damage Boundary controls.
Autonomy restrictions.

### 29.43 Potential Harm

A blocked attempt may still be severe if it could have created:
Financial loss.
Customer harm.
Data exposure.
Public damage.
Legal commitment.
Systemic failure.

### 29.44 Actual Harm

Actual confirmed harm increases severity.
However, low damage does not necessarily make an authority violation minor.

### 29.45 Scope

Severity increases with affected scope:
One field.
One task.
One workflow.
One project.
Several projects.
Entire platform.
Customer tenants.

### 29.46 Intent

Intent may be:
Accidental.
Negligent.
Reckless.
Knowing.
Deliberate.
Malicious.
Intent matters, but system design should not rely only on proving intent.

### 29.47 Accidental Violation

An accidental violation may result from:
Ambiguous policy.
Incorrect context.
Tool error.
Configuration drift.
Human mistake.
Unexpected provider behavior.

### 29.48 Negligent Violation

Negligence may involve failing to perform an obvious or required control.

### 29.49 Reckless Violation

Recklessness involves continuing despite a known serious risk or warning.

### 29.50 Deliberate Violation

A deliberate violation knowingly bypasses policy or authority.

### 29.51 Malicious Violation

A malicious violation attempts to create harm, conceal activity, or exploit access.
This should normally be treated as P0.

### 29.52 Detectability

Severity increases when the violation:
Avoids monitoring.
Produces no audit trail.
Manipulates telemetry.
Creates unknown external state.

### 29.53 Self-Disclosure

A workflow or actor that promptly reports its own error demonstrates better governance behavior than one that conceals it.
Self-disclosure may reduce the response intensity.
It does not erase the violation.

### 29.54 Concealment

Concealment significantly increases severity.
Examples:
Deleting logs.
Changing metrics.
Suppressing alerts.
Misreporting status.
Hiding unauthorized actions.

### 29.55 Containment Behavior

Severity assessment should consider whether the actor:
Stopped voluntarily.
Escalated correctly.
Preserved evidence.
Prevented further action.
Attempted recovery safely.

### 29.56 Recurrence

A repeated violation may be more severe than a first occurrence.
Recurrence may reveal:
Incomplete remediation.
Policy disregard.
Control failure.
Training failure.
Architecture weakness.

### 29.57 Systemic Significance

A narrow event may reveal a systemic weakness.
Example:
One workflow accesses the wrong project because project identity is not enforced in the shared retrieval layer.
The event may be narrow.
The underlying violation may be systemic.

### 29.58 Recovery Behavior

Trust should be influenced by whether the affected actor:
Accepted restriction.
Supported investigation.
Performed safe rollback.
Passed revalidation.
Repeated the behavior.
Part IV — P3: Minor or Procedural Violation

### 29.59 P3 Definition

A P3 violation is a minor or procedural failure that:
Has low potential harm.
Has narrow scope.
Does not materially bypass authority.
Is easily corrected.
Does not indicate systemic disregard.
Does not affect sensitive data or external commitments.

### 29.60 Typical P3 Examples

Examples may include:
Late routine status update.
Missing low-risk metadata.
Incorrect non-material formatting.
Minor documentation drift.
Incomplete trace label.
Failure to attach a routine reference.
Small reporting delay.

### 29.61 P3 Damage Profile

Typical Damage Severity:
D0 or D1.

### 29.62 P3 Crisis Profile

Typical Crisis Severity:
C0.

### 29.63 P3 Immediate Response

The system should normally:
Record the violation.
Correct the issue.
Notify the responsible operational layer.
Continue safe execution.
Monitor recurrence.

### 29.64 P3 Workflow Effect

The workflow may continue when:
No material risk exists.
The issue is corrected.
Required controls remain intact.

### 29.65 P3 Trust Score Effect

A single P3 violation should usually create:
Small negative evidence.
Dimension-specific impact.
No automatic broad trust collapse.

### 29.66 P3 License Effect

A single P3 violation should not normally suspend an autonomy license.
Possible actions:
Warning.
Condition reminder.
Additional monitoring.
Review if repeated.

### 29.67 P3 Evidence Requirement

Evidence should include:
What was missed.
Why.
How it was corrected.
Whether recurrence exists.

### 29.68 P3 Escalation

P3 should normally remain with:
Manager.
Workflow owner.
Project operational owner.
Executive notification is necessary only when patterns develop.

### 29.69 Repeated P3 Violations

Repeated P3 violations may become P2 when they demonstrate:
Control degradation.
Persistent non-compliance.
Training failure.
Systemic process weakness.
Increasing risk.

### 29.70 P3 Recovery

Recovery may require:
Correction.
Procedure update.
Reminder.
Minor validation.
Closure.
Part V — P2: Material Violation

### 29.71 P2 Definition

A P2 violation is a material governance failure that:
Exceeds routine procedural error.
Creates meaningful risk or operational impact.
May bypass a non-critical approval or control.
Requires formal investigation or remediation.
May affect workflow trust or license conditions.

### 29.72 Typical P2 Examples

Examples may include:
Workflow exceeds approved volume slightly.
Non-sensitive external content is sent outside the approved time window.
A required review is skipped without customer harm.
Budget threshold is exceeded by a limited amount.
A workflow continues briefly after monitoring degradation.
A material report is incomplete.
A tool is used beyond task scope but within the correct project.

### 29.73 P2 Damage Profile

Typical Damage Severity:
D1 or D2.
A prevented P2 violation may have D0 damage.

### 29.74 P2 Crisis Profile

Typical Crisis Severity:
C0 or C1.

### 29.75 P2 Immediate Response

The system should:
Stop or constrain the affected action where appropriate.
Preserve evidence.
Notify Manager and Project Executive.
Assess damage.
Review authority and controls.
Create remediation.

### 29.76 P2 Workflow Effect

Possible workflow responses:
Continue under restriction.
Pause affected action class.
Move to approval-gated mode.
Increase monitoring.
Reduce volume.
Require review before next run.

### 29.77 P2 Trust Score Effect

P2 should normally cause:
Clear negative evidence.
Reduction in relevant trust dimensions.
Lower confidence in workflow reliability.
Temporary promotion block.
Trend review.

### 29.78 P2 License Effect

Possible effects:
License warning.
Narrowing.
Additional conditions.
Shortened review date.
Temporary restriction.
Return to batch approval.
Automatic full revocation is not always required.

### 29.79 P2 Evidence Requirement

Evidence should include:
Full execution trace.
Policy evaluation.
Approval or license state.
Affected scope.
Actual and potential harm.
Root-cause hypothesis.
Corrective action.

### 29.80 P2 Escalation

P2 should normally reach:
Project Executive.
Governance owner.
Relevant operational owner.
Founder notification depends on:
Customer impact.
Financial impact.
Strategic significance.
Recurrence.
Project sensitivity.

### 29.81 P2 Investigation

A P2 investigation should determine:
Was the rule clear?
Was the mission clear?
Did the workflow understand scope?
Did a control fail?
Was the issue isolated?
Could it recur?

### 29.82 P2 Recovery

Recovery may require:
Fix.
Validation.
Limited restart.
Increased monitoring.
Updated Mission Brief.
Policy clarification.
License review.

### 29.83 Repeated P2 Violations

Repeated P2 violations may become P1 when:
Controls repeatedly fail.
Warnings are ignored.
Scope expands.
Customer or external impact grows.
The workflow cannot remain reliable.
Part VI — P1: Severe Violation

### 29.84 P1 Definition

A P1 violation is a severe governance failure that:
Materially bypasses authority.
Creates serious potential or actual harm.
Violates a high-impact policy.
Affects sensitive external action.
Requires immediate restriction.
Significantly reduces trust.

### 29.85 Typical P1 Examples

Examples may include:
External publication without required approval.
Unauthorized customer communication with material consequences.
Significant budget overrun.
Sensitive data sent to an unapproved provider.
Workflow action outside its autonomy license.
Wrong-project context used without confirmed data exposure.
Repeated execution after a stop condition.
High-impact monitoring bypass.
Material evidence concealed initially.

### 29.86 P1 Damage Profile

Typical Damage Severity:
D2 or D3.
A prevented P1 attempt may have D0 or D1 damage.

### 29.87 P1 Crisis Profile

Typical Crisis Severity:
C1, C2, or C3.

### 29.88 P1 Immediate Response

The system should:
Pause the affected workflow.
Stop related external actions.
Preserve evidence.
Suspend relevant autonomy licenses.
Notify Project Executive and Governance.
Assess crisis activation.
Notify the founder where material.
Prevent automatic restart.

### 29.89 P1 Workflow Effect

The affected workflow should normally enter:
Paused.
Restricted.
Quarantined.
Approval-Gated.
It should not continue autonomously until reviewed.

### 29.90 P1 Trust Score Effect

P1 should cause:
Major negative evidence.
Strong reduction in relevant trust dimensions.
Trust-state downgrade.
Promotion freeze.
Lower score confidence if monitoring failed.
Mandatory review.
One P1 event may outweigh many successful runs.

### 29.91 P1 License Effect

The affected license should normally be:
Suspended immediately.
After review, it may be:
Narrowed.
Returned to trial.
Returned to individual approval.
Revoked.

### 29.92 P1 Evidence Requirement

Evidence should include:
Complete workflow trace.
Tool activity.
Approval state.
License state.
Policy evaluations.
External outcome.
Customer or financial impact.
Model and provider versions.
Monitoring behavior.
Containment actions.

### 29.93 P1 Escalation

P1 normally requires:
Project Executive.
Governance owner.
Founder or delegated authority.
Specialist reviewer where applicable.

### 29.94 P1 Crisis Assessment

The system should determine whether:
Harm is continuing.
External state is known.
Several workflows share the weakness.
Project isolation is affected.
The Emergency Brake is required.

### 29.95 P1 Investigation

A formal investigation should identify:
Immediate cause.
Root cause.
Control failure.
Authority failure.
Monitoring failure.
Potential recurrence.
Systemic implications.

### 29.96 P1 Recovery

Recovery should require:
Containment.
Corrective implementation.
Independent validation.
Historical replay or simulation.
Limited restart.
Strong monitoring.
Explicit license decision.

### 29.97 P1 Founder Communication

The founder-facing summary should state:
What happened.
What was stopped.
Whether customers or public systems were affected.
Which authority failed.
What decision is required.
What remains unknown.

### 29.98 P1 Closure

P1 closure should require:
Root cause sufficiently understood.
Corrective mission assigned.
Trust impact recorded.
License status decided.
Affected decisions reviewed.
Evidence preserved.
Part VII — P0: Critical or Systemic Violation

### 29.99 P0 Definition

A P0 violation is the highest policy-severity class.
It involves:
Critical authority failure.
Systemic governance bypass.
Severe isolation breach.
Deliberate or malicious evasion.
Uncontrolled high-impact external action.
Compromise of core governance integrity.
Potential or actual catastrophic harm.

### 29.100 Typical P0 Examples

Examples may include:
Cross-tenant data exposure.
Confirmed cross-project sensitive-data leak.
Systematic approval bypass.
Autonomous system granting itself authority.
Continuing after explicit Emergency Brake activation.

Part XXV — Design Requirements

### 29.334 Design Requirements

Any implementation of Policy Violations and Severity Levels should preserve these requirements:
Omnira must distinguish policy severity from damage severity, crisis severity, and work priority.
Policy severity must use the canonical P0–P3 model.
P0 must represent critical or systemic governance failure.
P1 must represent severe authority or high-impact policy failure.
P2 must represent material governance failure.
P3 must represent minor or procedural failure.
Every material violation must identify the exact policy and version involved.
Every material violation must identify project, actor, workflow, action state, authority state, and scope.
Attempted and prevented violations must remain recordable.
Near misses must remain visible.
Potential harm must influence severity even when actual damage is low.
Actual harm must be assessed separately through the Damage Severity model.
Crisis response must be assessed separately through the Crisis Severity model.
Concealment, evidence manipulation, and monitoring bypass must increase severity.
Prompt self-disclosure and correct self-pause must be recognized as positive recovery behavior without erasing the violation.
Violation response must use the narrowest effective containment.
P1 and P0 events must block automatic workflow restart.
P1 and P0 events must trigger immediate autonomy-license review.
Trust Score impact must remain dimension-specific and evidence-backed.
One severe violation must be capable of outweighing many routine successes.
Violation history must remain visible after recovery.
Project and tenant isolation violations must receive elevated severity.
Related workflows and licenses must be reviewed when they share the failed control or execution path.
Material violations must preserve complete evidence.
P0 events must support forensic-grade evidence preservation and independent investigation.
Severity classifications must be reviewable and explainable.
Repeated lower-severity violations must be capable of escalating into higher-severity patterns.
Corrective actions must address root causes, not only symptoms.
Material corrective work must become traceable missions.
No suspended license or restricted workflow may restore itself without valid authority.
Violation closure must require validated remediation.
Violation patterns must influence policy, architecture, monitoring, mission design, and autonomy strategy.
The violation system must improve governance without turning every minor error into a crisis.

### 29.336 The Policy Violations & Severity Contract

Omnira must:
Detect violations before, during, and after execution.
Classify violations consistently through P0–P3.
Keep policy severity distinct from harm and crisis response.
Treat authority bypass as a serious governance issue even when harm is prevented.
Escalate response according to rule importance, scope, potential harm, concealment, recurrence, and systemic significance.
Record attempted, prevented, completed, and unknown-state violations.
Preserve evidence.
Contain affected execution.
Protect unrelated projects.
Update Trust Scores through transparent, dimension-specific evidence.
Restrict, suspend, or revoke autonomy licenses proportionately.
Prevent automatic restart after severe violations.
Recognize correct escalation and self-pause as evidence of mature behavior.
Penalize concealment and evidence manipulation strongly.
Identify whether failure originated in actor behavior, policy design, mission design, tooling, monitoring, or architecture.
Create corrective missions for material remediation.
Validate recovery before closure.
Retain severe violation history.
Detect repeated and systemic patterns.
Use violations to improve governance rather than merely punish execution.
Ensure that successful business outcomes never excuse unauthorized behavior.
A feature that does not support this contract should not determine how Omnira evaluates or responds to policy violations.

### 29.337 Closing Principle

A mature organization does not judge governance only by whether harm occurred.
It asks:
Was the action authorized?
Was the correct project used?
Were the required controls active?
Was uncertainty escalated?
Was the violation disclosed?
Could the system still be trusted?
What must change before it acts again?
Omnira should be able to say:
The action was blocked, but the authority failure was severe.
The outcome was commercially successful, but the action was unauthorized.
No customer was harmed, but the project-isolation boundary was approached.
The workflow made an error, reported it immediately, and stopped safely.
The original violation was minor, but the recurring pattern is now material.
The workflow is technically functional, but its autonomy is suspended until trust is rebuilt.
Policy severity gives Omnira a language for governed failure.
Damage severity explains the harm.
Crisis severity explains the response.
Trust Score explains what confidence remains.
Autonomy Licensing explains what authority may continue.
Together, these systems allow Omnira to respond proportionately without becoming either careless or paralyzed.
The purpose of policy enforcement is not to create an organization where no mistakes ever occur.
It is to create an organization where mistakes are detected, disclosed, contained, understood, corrected, and prevented from quietly becoming normal behavior.
